# Romowe Fire Festival — Old Prussian Eternal Flame at the Sacred Grove

## Comprehensive Overview

**Romowe** (also *Rickoyto* in some sources) was the paramount sacred grove of Old Prussia, described by Peter of Dusburg (*Chronicon terrae Prussiae*, 1326) and later elaborated by Simon Grunau (*Preussische Chronik*, 1529). At its centre stood an eternal fire — the Romowe Fire — maintained by the high priest (*Kriwe*) and his subordinate priests (*Worskaito*, fire-keepers). The fire burned within and around a great evergreen oak, and the grove served as the national religious centre of Old Prussian religious life before the Teutonic conquest. The renewal and tending of this eternal fire was a continuous ritual, the most sacred act in Old Prussian religion.

### English (EN)
The **Romowe Fire Festival** refers to the sustained ritual complex centred on the eternal sacred fire maintained at the grove of **Romowe** in Old Prussia. Peter of Dusburg (1326) describes a holy area (*sanctissimum et sanctum*) in the district of Nadruvia, where there stood a huge evergreen oak whose trunk was adorned with the cloth images of three deities: **Patollo** (death/underworld), **Patrimpas** (youth/fertility), and **Perkuns** (thunder). Before the image of Patollo, old men lay dying; before Patrimpas, a snake was kept in grain; before Perkuns, an eternal fire of oak-branches burned day and night. This fire — the Romowe fire — was never permitted to go out. If it was accidentally extinguished, it could only be relit by friction of dry wood (*ex arboribus silvestribus*), not by transferred flame from another source. Simon Grunau (1529) adds that the sacred grove was fenced with cloths — pilgrims could enter only with the high priest's authorisation — and that the fire was fed exclusively with dry oak. The high priest (*Kriwe*) wielded national religious authority across all Prussian tribes; his staff (*staffa Kriwia*) was circulated as a messenger-symbol, obliging tribal leaders to attend the grove. The eternal fire represented the immortality of the divine triad and the continuity of the Prussian people. The extinguishing of the Romowe fire by the Teutonic Knights around 1350–1400 CE marked the symbolic end of Old Prussian religion.

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Romowės ugnis** — Prūsų amžinoji šventoji ugnis, deganti prie didžiojo amžinai žaliuojančio ąžuolo šventame Romowės ąžuolyne. Petras iš Dusburgo (1326) aprašo tris dievų atvaizdus ant ąžuolo kamieno: **Patollo** (mirtis), **Patrimpas** (jaunystė), **Perkūnas** (perkūna). Prieš Perkūno atvaizdą degė amžinoji ugnis — niekada nebuvo leidžiama užgesinti. Jei ji atsitiktinai užgesdavo, galima buvo uždegti tik trinant sausus vynmedžius — ne iš kito šaltinio ugnies. Vyriausiasis kunigas (*Kriwis*) valdė visus prūsų gentis švento personalo lazdelės (*staffa Kriwia*) pagalba.

### Latvian (LV)
**Romowes ugunssvētks** — Senprūšu mūžīgās svētās uguns kulta rituāls pie Romowes ozolu birzs. Petrs no Dusburgas (1326) apraksta lielo mūžzaļo ozolu ar triju dievu tēliem: **Patollo** (nāve/pazeme), **Patrimpas** (auglība/jaunība), **Perkuns** (pērkons). Perkuna attēla priekšā dega mūžīga uguns, ko drīkstēja kurināt tikai ar sausu ozola malku un nekad nedrīkstēja dzēst. Augstais priesteris (*Kriwe*) kontrolēja reliģiskās dzīves tīklu visā Prūsijā. Romiešu tipa mūžīgā uguns tradīcija liecina par senu un sarežģītu reliģisku infrastruktūru, kas pastāvēja pirms Vācu ordeņa iekarošanas.

## Primary Source Details

- **Peter of Dusburg (Dur):** Primary description of the Romowe grove, the eternal fire, and the triad of deities. [Peter of Dusburg, *Chronicon terrae Prussiae*, 1326; Book III, Chapter 5]
- **Simon Grunau (Grunau):** Extended description of the grove enclosure, the Kriwe priestly hierarchy, and the *staffa Kriwia* messenger-staff. [Simon Grunau, *Preussische Chronik*, ca. 1529]
- **Einhorn (Ein):** Reference to Old Prussian fire practices and priestly hierarchy. [Paul Einhorn, *Historia Lettica*, 1649]

## Key Metadata
- **Region**: Nadruvia (northeastern Old Prussia); sacred grove believed to be near the Nemunas/Memel delta
- **Tribe**: Old Prussians (all tribes)
- **Period**: Pre-Christian; first described 1326 CE; grove likely destroyed by Teutonic Knights c. 1350–1400 CE
- **Category**: Sacred Site / Perpetual Fire / National Religious Centre
- **Date**: Continuous — the fire was maintained without interruption
- **Symbol**: Evergreen oak, Eternal flame, Three-deity cloth images, Kriwe's staff (*staffa Kriwia*), Grove fence-cloth
- **Deities invoked**: Patollo (death/underworld), Patrimpas (youth/spring/fertility), Perkuns (thunder)
- **Ritual actions**: Eternal fire tending with dry oak, friction-relighting if extinguished, pilgrimage to grove, receipt of Kriwe's staff
- **References**:
    - "Peter of Dusburg: Chronicon terrae Prussiae (1326)"
    - "Grunau, Simon: Preussische Chronik (c. 1529)"
    - "Einhorn, Paul: Historia Lettica (1649)"
