# Kolka Surge Watch / Kolkos Bangų Sargyba / Kolkas Viļņu Sardze

## Comprehensive Overview

The **Kolka Surge Watch** is the northernmost tip of the Kurzeme Peninsula at Cape Kolka (Kolkasrags), Latvia — the sharp headland where the Gulf of Rīga meets the open Baltic Sea and two sets of waves collide. This is one of the most meteorologically intense locations on the Baltic coast, where opposing wind-generated swells meet and create confused seas. The site is dedicated to **Bangpūtys** in his Latvian coastal expression as the dual-faced wind deity — one face toward the Gulf (inland, gentle), one face toward the open sea (outward, violent). Wind vanes carved as twin faces have been documented in this tradition, placed at the cape to track which aspect is active when gusts arrive. The cape's extreme practical danger for fishermen gave its ritual tradition an urgency found nowhere else on the Latvian coast.

### English (EN)
**Bangpūtys** at Kolka manifested his dual nature most literally: the cape is the exact point where the two seas meet and his two faces confronted each other directly. The carved twin-face vanes were instrumental rather than merely symbolic: boats were delayed or redirected based on which vane-face turned first when gusts arrived. If the inward face (toward the Gulf) turned first, the wind was bringing conditions from the calmer eastern water — a favorable omen for boats heading out. If the outward face (toward the open Baltic) turned first, the wind was bringing open-sea conditions inland — boats stayed harbored. On certain mornings — calm, cold dawns after a long blow — foam piled into parallel ridges exactly at the cape where the seas met. This "foam ridge" was a communal divination site: children leapt over each ridge for luck, counting jumps to calm sand. The number of ridges requiring jumps was read as the number of days before conditions would stabilize. The silence required when observing these ridges was a form of ritual respect — speaking too loudly was believed to call Bangpūtys's attention and restart the wind.

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Bangpūtys** Kolkos kyšulyje reiškėsi savo dvejopa prigimtimi labiausiai tiesiogiai: kyšulys yra tikslus taškas, kur susitinka dvi jūros ir jo du veidai susidūrė tiesiogiai. Drožtos dvynių veidų vėjarodės buvo skirtingos, o ne vien simbolinės: laivai buvo atidedami ar nukreipiami pagal tai, kuris vėjarodės veidas pasuko pirmiausia, kai atvykdavo gūsiai. Jei vidinis veidas (link įlankos) pasuko pirmiausia, vėjas nešė sąlygas iš ramesnio rytinio vandens — palankus ženklas laivams plaukiantiems. Jei išorinis veidas (link atviros Baltijos) pasuko pirmiausia, vėjas nešė atvirosios jūros sąlygas žemyn — laivai liko uoste. Tam tikrais rytais putos straupdavo lygiagrečiomis keteromis tiksliai ten, kur susitiko jūros.

### Latvian (LV)
**Bangpūtys** Kolkā izpaudās savā divkāršajā dabā visdramatiskāk: zemesrags ir precīzs punkts, kur satiekas divas jūras un viņa divi sejas tieši sastapās. Grebtās dvīņu sejas vēja rādītāji bija instrumentālas, nevis tikai simboliskas: laivas tika aizkavētas vai novirzītas, pamatojoties uz to, kura vēja rādītāja seja pagriežas pirmā, kad brāzmas ieradās. Ja iekšējā seja (pret līci) pagriežas pirmā, vējš nesa apstākļus no mierīgākajiem austrumu ūdeņiem — labvēlīga zīme laivām, kas dodas ārā. Ja ārējā seja (pret atvērto Baltiju) pagriežas pirmā, vējš nesa atklātās jūras apstākļus uz iekštelpu — laivas palika ostā. Noteiktos rītos putas sakrājās paralēlās grēdas tieši zemesragā, kur satikās jūras.

## Primary Source Details

- **Šmits (Šmits):** Latvian coastal folk legend documentation including Cape Kolka's special status as a dual-sea threshold and the associated twin-spirit veneration. [Pēteris Šmits, *Latviešu pasakas un teikas*, Vol. 14, 1936]
- **LNE:** National encyclopaedia entry on Kolkasrags and its pre-Christian maritime ritual significance (*latvijasenoklopedija.lv*)
- **Einhorn (Ein):** 17th-century Livonian account noting the Kurzeme fisher communities' special wind-divination practices at capes. [Paul Einhorn, *Historia Lettica*, 1649]
- **Barons (Bar):** Dainas from the Kurzeme coastal tradition including songs about the dual-nature sea-wind and twin-face offerings at headlands. [Krišjānis Barons, *Latvju dainas*, 6 vols, 1894–1915]
- **Biezais (Biez):** Documents the Latvian concept of sea-wind as dual (creative and destructive) in folk-religious belief. [Haralds Biezais, *Die himmlische Götterfamilie*, 1972]
