# Lithuanian Creatures & Spirits II / Night, Swamp, and Transformation

## Comprehensive Overview

The "darker" side of Lithuanian folklore is populated by entities that personify the fears of the night, the dangers of the swamp, and the liminality of human transformation. These figures serve both as cautionary tales and as remnants of archaic, pre-Christian beliefs in the supernatural.

### Ragana (Witch / Seer)
**Ragana** is one of the most prominent figures in the Baltic tradition. Etymologically linked to the word *regėti* (to see), she was originally a "seer" or a goddess of wisdom, life, and death. In later folklore, she became the classical witch—a powerful, often elderly woman who lived in the forest and mastered shape-shifting (into cats, birds, or sows). Raganos were known for flying to **Šatrija Hill** for their nocturnal sabbaths. While they were feared for as "milk-thieves" and bringers of disease, they were also respectably sought out as herbalists and healers.
- **Symbol**: Broom (Šatra), Cauldron, Cat, Šatrija Hill
- **Aspects**: Seer of the future, Mistress of herbs, Shape-shifter.

### Slogutė (Nightmare / Sleep Paralysis)
**Slogutė** (also called *Sloga* or *Marė*) is the personified spirit of sleep paralysis and nightmares. She is described as a heavy, invisible weight or a small, pale creature that sits on the chest of a sleeper, making it impossible to breathe or move. To drive her away, one had to move a toe or keep a iron object (like a knife or scissors) near the bed. She represents the physiological terror of the "night-presser."
- **Symbol**: Iron knife, Scissors, Weight on the chest
- **Action**: Pressing or "slowing" the breath of the sleeper.

### Baubas & Maumas (Child-Frighteners)
These are "bogeymen" used primarily in pedagogical folklore to discipline children.
- **Baubas**: A dark, shadowy figure with long arms and glowing eyes that hides in dark corners or under furniture. He is the master of home-based fear.
- **Maumas**: A specifically swamp- or water-dwelling spirit. He is used to warn children away from deep wells, lakes, and treacherous bogs. He "drags" the unwary into the depths.
- **Symbol**: Glowing eyes in the dark, Reeds of the swamp.

### Vilkacis / Vilkatis (Werewolf)
The **Vilkacis** is a human who has transformed into a wolf. Unlike the modern horror movie werewolf, the Baltic werewolf is often a tragic figure—a person cursed by a witch or someone who voluntarily transformed via a ritual (e.g., crawling under a specific root) to gain strength or hide. They retain human intelligence but are bound by wolf-nature. Historic trial records from the 16th-17th centuries show that beliefs in werewolves (sometimes seen as "hounds of God" who fought against witches) were widespread in both Lithuania and Latvia.
- **Symbol**: Wolf pelt, Belt of transformation, Iron teeth.

## Primary Source Details

- **Basanavičius (Bas):** Collected extensive accounts of raganos and werewolves in his ethnographic works. [Lietuviškos pasakos, 1898]
- **Vėlius (Vel):** Analyzed the etymology and structural roles of the Ragana as a cosmic figure. [Mitinės būtybės, 1977]
- **Trial Records (WTR):** Documented accounts of shape-shifting and milk-stealing in historical witchcraft trials.
- **VLE:** Provides the formal encyclopedic context for these mythic figures.

## Key Metadata
- **Region**: Pan-Lithuanian / Samogitian (Šatrija focus)
- **Tribe**: Lithuanian / Curonian
- **Period**: Archaic to Early Modern
- **Category**: Witches / Bogeymen / Transformation Spirits
- **Function**: Healing/Magic (Ragana), Disciplining children (Baubas), Explaining paralysis (Slogutė), Personifying the lupine nature (Vilkacis)
