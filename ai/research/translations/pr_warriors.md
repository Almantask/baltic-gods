# Old Prussian Resistance Figures / The Last Defenders of the Baltic World

## Comprehensive Overview

When the Teutonic Knights systematically subjugated the Old Prussian tribes between 1230 and 1283, the resistance produced several figures whose names – preserved in medieval chronicles – developed legendary dimensions. **Glande** and **Skomantas** represent the final chapters of Old Prussian armed resistance: one a tribal chief whose people were among the first to be conquered, the other the last Sudovian leader to hold out before the final collapse.

---

### Glande — The Galindian Chief

**Glande** was a chief of the **Galinds** (*Galindians*), an Old Prussian sub-tribe who inhabited the southeastern interior of the Prussian lands. He is mentioned in **Peter of Dusburg's** *Chronicon terrae Prussiae* (1326) in the context of the Teutonic conquest, which began in the 1230s.

The Galinds were one of the eleven Old Prussian clans recognized in medieval sources. They inhabited territory around the headwaters of the Alna and Omra rivers (present-day Masuria/northeastern Poland). Glande appears in the chronicle context of early Prussian resistance to the Knights' advance across the Vistula River.

Though the chronicles give him comparatively few lines, in Old Prussian folk memory (preserved in later German-Prussian chronicle compilations) Glande represents the archetypal local tribal chief who chose armed resistance over conversion — the **first-generation defender** before the systematic consolidation of Teutonic power. His name may preserve a Proto-Baltic personal-name form related to "*galindas*" (possibly "end-one" — those living at the edge/boundary of the Baltic lands).

- **Symbol**: Galindian hillfort, Tribal lance, The eastern Baltic borderland
- **Role**: Tribal chief of the Galinds; early resistance leader; emblem of local sovereignty before the Crusading conquest

### Old Prussian Context (EN)
The Galinds were known to be cattle-herders and forest-dwellers who worshipped the forest deities (*Girriti*, *Baurstucke*) and maintained hillforts (*piliskas*) as community centers. Glande's defense of these sites against the heavily armored Teutonic forces was part of the first wave of the 50-year Prussian resistance.

### Skomantas — The Last Sudovian

**Skomantas** (fl. 1270s–1283) is the last documented leader of the **Sudovians** (*Sūduviai* in Lithuanian; *Jatvingians* / *Yotvingians* also used interchangeably), the powerful and warlike Old Prussian sub-tribe who held territory in south-central Prussia (present-day northeastern Poland and Lithuania's Suvalkija region).

The Sudovians (along with the Yotvingians, who may be the same or a closely related group) were among the most fierce and most prolonged opponents of the Teutonic Order. Their resistance lasted decades longer than that of the northern Prussian tribes. Skomantas appears in the chronicles as the final leader of organized Sudovian armed resistance — his forces were the last to hold out, and when they were ultimately broken (c.1283), the Sudovians underwent either:
- Mass deportation (the so-called "*Sudovian Diaspora*" — tribes were scattered across Prussia and Lithuania)
- Absorption into the expanding Lithuanian state (many Sudovians fled north into Lithuania and merged with Lithuanian tribes in the Suvalkija district)

Skomantas thus has a dual significance:
1. As the **last defender**: the final armed chieftain of the Prussian tribal world
2. As the **mythic exodus figure**: he led his surviving people into the Lithuanian sphere, contributing to the preservation of Baltic pagan culture in a new political context

- **Symbol**: Broken spear, The Sudovian migration north, Sacred oak stump, Final bonfire
- **Role**: Last Sudovian resistance leader; keeper of the final ember of Prussian paganism; migration leader into Lithuania

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Skomantas** — paskutinis sūduvių/jotvingių genties karinis vadas, chronikose minimas kaip baigiamosios fazės kovotojas prieš Kryžiuočius. Apie 1283 m. jo pajėgos buvo galutinai sutriuškintos. Likę sūduviai arba buvo ištremti ("sūduvių diaspora"), arba pabėgo į Lietuvą ir susilydė su lietuvių gentimis Suvalkijoje. Skomantas yra paskutinis iš senojo prūsiško pasipriešinimo simbolių.

### Latvian (LV)
**Skomantas** ir pēdējais sudāviešu/jatvingu karavadonis — dokumentēts Teitoņu ordeņa iekaro hronikās kā galīgais senprūšu bruņotās pretestības aizstāvis. Pēc apmēram 1283. gada viņa ļaudis tika vai nu izsūtīti (sudāviešu diaspora), vai bēga uz ziemeļiem uz Lietuvu, kur iesūcās lietuviešu ciltīs. Skomantas pārstāv pēdējo no senprūšu pagāniskās pasaules sargiem.

## The Great Prussian Uprisings — Context

Both Glande and Skomantas operated within the larger context of the **Great Prussian Uprisings**:
- **First Uprising (1242–1249):** Following the Mongol invasion's weakening of the Teutonic Order's allies. Ended with the **Treaty of Christburg (1249)** — the first major document of Prussian religious life, where tribes formally renounced specific pagan practices under duress.
- **Second Uprising (1260–1274):** Triggered by the Prussian/Lithuanian victory at the **Battle of Durbe (1260)**, which destroyed a Teutonic army. **Herkus Monte** led the Natangians; many tribes rose simultaneously. This is the most extensively documented Prussian resistance.
- **Third Uprising (1276–1283):** Final wave, during which Skomantas led the Sudovians. The crushing of this uprising ended the last phase of organized Prussian armed resistance.

After 1283 there were no more major Prussian uprisings. The Old Prussian language and distinct identity survived into the 17th century (the last known Old Prussian speaker died c.1700), but political sovereignty was gone.

## Primary Source Details

- **Peter of Dusburg: *Chronicon terrae Prussiae* (1326, Dur):** The primary chronicle source for both Glande and Skomantas; describes the Teutonic conquest campaign by campaign, naming tribal chiefs.
- **Livländische Reimchronik / Livonian Rhymed Chronicle (c.1290, LRC):** Contemporary verse chronicle covering the Baltic campaigns, including Sudovian resistance.
- **Sudauer Büchlein (Sudovian Book, 16th c., Sud):** Lists the deities and ritual practices of the Sudovians — provides the religious backdrop for Skomantas's world.
- **Toporov, Vladimir N.: *Prusskij jazyk* (1975–90, Topo):** Linguistic analysis of Prussian names and their tribal associations; relevant to Galindian name-forms of Glande.
- **Iwinski, Tadeusz:** Academic studies of Yotvingian tribal history and the Sudovian Diaspora (*Jaćwieź i Sudowie*, various journal articles, 20th c.)
