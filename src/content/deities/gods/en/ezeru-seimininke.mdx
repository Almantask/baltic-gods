## Ežerų Šeimininkė — Mistress of the Lake's Depths

In the Baltic lands, lakes are not emptied of meaning at their shores. Below the reflecting surface lies a second world — a silver kingdom ruled by a woman whose hair ripples like underwater grass and whose authority extends from the deepest trenches to the mist that lifts at dawn. The Ežerų Šeimininkė decides the fate of every fishing expedition, every swimmer who crosses her territory, and every soul that enters her domain through drowning. Jonas Balys documented the offerings customarily made at the lakeside before the first fishing of the year: a coin thrown in, a lock of hair, a scattering of bread — tokens acknowledging that the catch belongs first to her.

Her role is not malevolent but sovereign. She does not seek to harm those who respect her; she simply maintains the order of her domain. The souls of the drowned are not lost but taken in — they become part of her underwater court, and their families knew that they had gone somewhere specific, to a complete if cold world with its own hierarchy and geography. This theology softened grief while maintaining the warning: her realm is beautiful but it is not safe for the living, and those who take from her waters without acknowledgment may find the next visit more difficult.

Lake traditions varied locally across the Baltic. The emerald-skinned Lady of Sartai Lake in eastern Lithuania was a figure distinct from the generic Ežerų Šeimininkė, with her own appearance, temperament, and catalog of demands. In Latvia, Ezera māte was equally specific to each body of water. The general term for lake mistress was always a local figure — not an abstract deity but the particular spirit of this lake, these reeds, this mist.
