## Pirts māte — Mother of the Bathhouse

Pirts māte is the Latvian guardian spirit of the bathhouse — the *pirts*, the wooden sauna that was among the most sacred secondary structures on every Latvian farm. The bathhouse was not merely a place for hygiene but a threshold space: the site of birth, healing, and certain funerary preparations. Pirts māte presided over all these functions, making her one of the most frequently invoked of the māte spirits.

Women in labor were traditionally brought to the bathhouse rather than the main house, placing the birth under the direct protection of Pirts māte. She was invoked alongside Laima at such moments, the two goddesses jointly governing the fate and health of mother and child. The steam, the heat, and the cleansing properties of the birch-branch slota (bath whisk) were all understood as her gifts.

Healers and herbalists used the bathhouse as a ritual space for treating illness, calling on Pirts māte to amplify the power of their remedies. The combination of steam and heat could break fevers and drive out harmful influences; the privacy of the bathhouse away from the household made it suitable for the secret knowledge needed in serious healing work.

After use, it was customary to leave a small offering of water or a portion of steam for Pirts māte, acknowledging her presence before departing. To leave the bathhouse messy or to speak disrespectfully within it risked offending her, bringing illness or difficult births to the household in consequence. Her demanding but benevolent nature made the bathhouse a space of both great power and great caution.
