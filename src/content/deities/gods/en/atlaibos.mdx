## Atlaibos — Spirit of Liberation and Release

The Baltic world knew captivity as a real and ever-present danger. Tribal raiding, inter-clan warfare, and the long twilight wars against the advancing Teutonic Order all created prisoners and hostages as routine facts of life. Between the captivity and the freedom lay a prayer — and that prayer went to Atlaibos.

Jan Łasicki recorded *Atlaiba* among the Samogitian deities with the laconic Latin note *pro captivis* — "for prisoners." The name carries its meaning transparently for any Lithuanian speaker: *atleisti* or *atlaibyti* means to let go, to release, to loosen, and the deity's name is the verb made divine. Atlaibos was the god of the cut rope, the opened door, the ransom paid. He was invoked by the families of those held captive, by prisoners themselves in whatever limited privacy they could find, and by the negotiators seeking the release of hostages.

In the broader theology of Baltic religion, Atlaibos represents the principle that every defined human situation — including captivity — had its own divine overseer. This was a comprehensive theology: not only the harvest and the weather and the household fire required divine attention, but the specific fate of every human being in every possible circumstance. The prisoner in a foreign camp was not beyond the reach of the divine. There was a god for this, and that god could be appealed to.

Some scholars, including Algirdas Julius Greimas, have interpreted the *atlaib-* function as a form of divine grace understood through the mechanism of forgiveness and release — a concept that the Baltic peoples later found easier to translate into Christian terms precisely because they already had a deity whose domain was liberation from bondage.
