## Kempe / Kempė — Spirit of Beetles

Kempė occupies one of the smallest niches in the Baltic divine order: she is the guardian of beetles, recorded by Łasicki in the formula *pro scarabaeis* — "for beetles." In a pantheon remarkable for the specificity of its functional assignments, Kempė represents the ultimate in divine micro-management: a deity whose entire domain consists of one family of insects.

Yet beetles were not inconsequential in the Baltic agricultural world. Cockchafers, in particular, were feared agricultural pests. A cockchafer outbreak in late spring — when the grubs attack roots and the adults defoliate trees and grain — could devastate a harvest. A farmer watching newly emerged cockchafers swarm across his rye field was not watching the idle curiosity of nature; he was watching a potential famine in slow motion.

Invoking Kempė was therefore an act of agricultural risk management. Offering her a symbolic tribute — a handful of grain, a crust of bread laid in the field — acknowledged her power over the insect and petitioned her to redirect the swarm, to limit its hunger, or at least to ensure its destructive phase passed quickly.

Kempė's existence tells us something important about the structure of Baltic belief: the divine covered the entire spectrum of nature, from the thunderstorm down to the beetle in the clod. The size of the phenomenon did not determine the importance of its deity. What determined importance was the relationship between that phenomenon and human survival — and beetles, during outbreak years, could be as dangerous as floods.
