# Ausklauts — The Divine Listener

No word spoken in the human world was ever truly lost. The Samogitians knew this because they had a name for the divine faculty that heard everything: **Ausklauts**. Recorded by Jan Łasicki as the deity invoked *pro auditu* — for the sake of hearing — Ausklauts personified the belief that the gods were not remote and indifferent but actively present, listening to every oath, every prayer, and every false promise made under the open sky.

The etymology of his name is transparent and revealing: *ausis* (ear) and *klausyti* (to listen, to hear). Ausklauts was the divine ear turned toward the earth. In the legal and social culture of Baltic village life, where written records were rare and agreements were sealed by spoken word before witnesses, the knowledge that a god heard every oath was not merely comforting — it was the foundation of social order. No one could lie before Ausklauts and expect the gods to remain indifferent to the deception.

The scholar A. J. Greimas linked Ausklauts to a broader moral-ritual system in Lithuanian mythology where specialized spirits monitored specific domains of human conduct. Just as **Dimstipatis** watched over the farmstead's physical order, Ausklauts watched over the order of words and agreements. His domain was the spoken covenant — marriage vows, land treaties, promises made at the deathbed. When a man swore an oath and then broke it, Ausklauts had already noted the transgression, and the consequences would manifest in the failure of crops, the illness of children, or the inexplicable run of bad fortune that plagued the oath-breaker's family.

In prayer, Ausklauts was invoked not just by those seeking justice but by anyone who needed to be heard — the sick who prayed for healing, the widow who appealed to the divine for fairness, the young man who swore loyalty to his clan. Ausklauts was the assurance that the universe was listening.
