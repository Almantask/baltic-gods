## Okopirmus — The Very First

Okopirmus, whose name literally means "The Very First," is the supreme sky deity of the Old Prussians. He stands above even the famous Romuva triad of Patrimpas, Perkuns, and Patols, representing the primordial, uncaused cause — the sky of skies, the first moment of order before motion began.

In the chronicles of Simon Grunau (1529), Okopirmus appears at the apex of the Prussian divine hierarchy. He is not an interventionist deity in the manner of Perkuns. Okopirmus instead represents static, eternal order — the structure of the cosmos which all other powers obey. He is the final judge of the other gods and the one to whom the final accounts of the world are rendered.

The Sudovian Book, a 16th-century catalogue of Prussian deities compiled by German missionaries, lists Okopirmus at the head of the god-list. This placement emphasizes his role as source of all life, fertility, and cosmic law. His symbols are the vault of the sky, the sun-wheel at rest, and the first point of a circle — the still center from which all things radiate.

Scholars including Norbertas Velius have analyzed Okopirmus as the Old Prussian counterpart to the Lithuanian Praamzius and the Latvian Dievs — the distant sky-father archetype shared across all Baltic peoples. In an era when Baltic religions were suppressed by the Teutonic Knights, the memory of Okopirmus was preserved primarily in chronicles written by those very suppressors, lending his mythology a poignant fragility.
