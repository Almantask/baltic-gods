## Ratainyčia / Kremata / Mjurė / Meilus — Guardians of Livestock and the Cart

Of all the deity-names preserved in Łasicki's Samogitian catalogue, the four livestock guardians — Ratainyčia, Kremata, Mjurė, and Meilus — offer the most direct window into the Baltic farmstead economy. Łasicki records them in clinical Latin: *Ratainyczam pro rotis* (Ratainyčia for wheels), *Krematam pro porcis* (Kremata for pigs), *Mjuram pro bobus* (Mjurė for oxen), and *Meilum pro capris* (Meilus for goats). Four spirits. Four essential productive categories. A micro-pantheon of the barnyard.

Ratainyčia's domain — wheels and carts — might seem prosaic until one considers the centrality of the wheeled vehicle to Baltic rural life. The wagon transported grain to mill and market, carried timber and stone, bore the sick to healers, and moved the dead to burial. A broken axle or a cracked wheel rim during harvest season could mean catastrophe. Ratainyčia absorbed the anxious prayers of everyone who depended on these machines to function without failure.

Kremata, Mjurė, and Meilus each presided over a specific livestock category. Pigs were slaughtered for winter provisions; oxen drew the plow and the drag; goats provided milk, cheese, and supplementary fiber. Each animal class required careful management, was vulnerable to specific diseases, and generated its own ritual occasions. These specialized guardians reflect a theology that took specialization seriously: no single deity of livestock sufficed when the distinctions between pig-keeping and ox-hauling were as different as the lives of the people who performed them.

The four together teach a lesson about Baltic religion's micro-scale logic: just as modern insurance divides liability by category, so Baltic religious practice assigned spiritual protection to every productive category of the farmstead.
