## Udens mate — Mother of Fresh Water

Udens mate, the Mother of Water, presides over the fresh waters of Latvia: the lakes, the rivers, the springs that emerge from hillsides, and the wells that were dug deep into the earth. Where Juras mate governs the vast and unpredictable sea, Udens mate is the keeper of the intimate, life-sustaining waters that flow through the landscape and through the daily rituals of human existence.

In Latvian folk practice, a spring or well was a place of great ritual significance. Clean water was essential for every aspect of life: drinking, cooking, washing, livestock, and the ritual cleansing of the body before sacred occasions. Udens mate governed all of it. Women brought offerings of beads, coins, or woven ribbons to riverbanks on feast days, acknowledging her sovereignty over the water they used each day.

Flowing water also played a central role in Latvian beliefs about the passage of time and the journey of the soul. The phrase par upi — 'across the river' — was a common euphemism for death in folk songs. Udens mate thus has a liminal quality: she presides over transitions as well as sustenance, standing at the threshold between the living world and what lies beyond the current.

Udens mate and Upes mate (Mother of Rivers) are sometimes treated as distinct figures in scholarly analysis — the former governing still water, the latter moving water. Both represent the profound reverence that Baltic people maintained for the water that gave them life and that ultimately carried all living things away.
