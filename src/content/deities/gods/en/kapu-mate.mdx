## Kapu māte — Mother of Graves

Kapu māte is the Latvian guardian spirit of burial grounds, the deity who watches over the physical graves of the community’s dead. While Velņu māte governs the souls of the departed in their new existence, Kapu māte attends to the places where their bodies were laid: the mounds, the markers, the perimeter of the cemetery, and the peace that should prevail within it.

Her presence ensured that the dead rested undisturbed. Burial grounds in Latvian tradition were not places of terror but of solemn responsibility. The community owed the dead proper burial, periodic visits, and offerings at the major ancestor festivals of the year. Kapu māte was the divine steward of these obligations, ensuring that the living fulfilled their duties and that the dead were not disturbed by careless intrusion.

Visitors to the cemetery addressed Kapu māte implicitly through their behavior: speaking quietly, walking carefully, not cutting branches or disturbing markers. Offerings of flowers, candles, and food left at gravesides were partly for the dead and partly a recognition of the goddess who maintained the ground’s sanctity.

Kapu māte also served a protective function toward the living who entered her domain. A cemetery was a liminal space, a point of contact between the living world and the realm of the dead, and Kapu māte regulated that contact. Her authority prevented the dead from wandering and the living from importing harmful influences into the resting place of the community’s ancestors. She was the warden of one of the most important thresholds in Latvian sacred geography.
