## The Double Grain

Jumis appears in the grain field as a double-stalked plant — two stalks sharing one root, two heads of rye growing as one. The farmer who spots it before harvest stops and calls the others over. It cannot be cut carelessly: the jumis stalk must be braided into a crown and carried back to the farmhouse with songs, for it carries the field's prosperity for the coming year.

## The Earliest Mention

Henricus de Lettis, writing his Chronicle of Livonia around 1227, mentions harvest rituals that scholars have identified with the jumis tradition. By the time Krišjānis Barons compiled the dainas (1894–1915), thousands of folk songs described Jumis dances, Jumis found in fields, and the proper ceremony for bringing him home. No other aspect of Latvian harvest ritual generated such consistent poetic attention.

## The Last Sheaf

At harvest's end, the last sheaf of grain was woven into an effigy — given arms, legs, a head — and dressed in linen. This was also called Jumis, or sometimes the Rye Grandmother. It was kept in the farmhouse through winter, fed a little grain and water, and burned or plowed back into the first field turning of spring. The cycle closed: Jumis returns to the ground from which the next year's plenty will come.

## The Festival

Finding a jumis stalk was the signal for an impromptu village feast. Beer was brewed from the same double-grain barley, and the family that found the jumis hosted the celebration. Wilhelm Mannhardt, working from nineteenth-century fieldwork, documented this practice across the Latvian and Prussian countryside, noting parallels to Germanic last-harvest rituals that suggest great antiquity.
