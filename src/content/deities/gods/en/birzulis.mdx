## Birzulis — Spirit of the Birch Forest

Birzulis is the guardian deity of the birch grove — one of the most elegant entries in the Lithuanian pantheon recorded by Jan Lasicki as silvae betulinae deus, the god of the birch forest. He personifies the soft, white world of the birch stand: filtering light, catkin-smell, and the spring sap that rises before any other tree has woken from winter.

The birch (berzelis) held a special place in Baltic ritual life. Its sap was among the first natural foods of spring, tapped in March when the ground was still frozen, and fermented into a mildly sweet beverage. Birch branches were used in the midsummer sauna ritual — the vanta, or birch bundle — to cleanse and invigorate the body. The white bark was associated with purity and new beginnings.

Birzulis governs all these uses. His protection was sought before tapping a birch grove for sap, to ensure the trees would yield abundantly and recover without harm. Cutting birch branches for ritual use without acknowledging Birzulis was considered disrespectful. The grove itself was a sacred space: its openness and light made it a preferred location for seasonal rites.

As a minor nature deity, Birzulis represents the Baltic principle that every ecosystem, every specific category of tree and plant, had its own divine guardian. The land was not a single undifferentiated wilderness but a mosaic of sacred presences, each requiring its own acknowledgment.
