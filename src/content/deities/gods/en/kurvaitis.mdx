## Kurvaitis / Sveitiks / Aperušis — Guardians of Foals, Guests, and the Harvest Threshold

Three minor but revealing deities from Łasicki's list: Kurvaitis, Sveitiks, and Aperušis. Each governs a transition — biological, social, or agricultural — where things could go wrong and divine support was needed.

Kurvaitis (*Kurvvaiczin pro equulis*, "for foals") watched over young horses in the critical months after birth. The foal was the most valuable animal birth in a Baltic household — a future warhorse or draft animal worth years of work. Foal mortality was a real and painful economic loss. Invoking Kurvaitis during the foal's early weeks was insurance theology: placing the vulnerable young animal under divine protection during the period when it was most exposed.

Sveitiks (*Sveitix pro hospitibus*, "for guests") governed the social technology of hospitality. In a world without inns, the reception of guests was a moral obligation and a social institution. Hospitality created alliances, exchanged news, sustained kinship networks, and affirmed community bonds. A guest who arrived to a cold reception, or who brought sickness or misfortune into the household, disrupted all these threads. Sveitiks was invoked to ensure that arriving guests brought fortune and that their presence wove benefit rather than discord.

Aperušis governed the harvest threshold: the moment when cut grain passed from the open field into the protected barn. That crossing was both physically significant (grain stored incorrectly would rot) and spiritually loaded (the grain's spirit needed proper acknowledgment). Aperušis ensured the passage went smoothly and the grain retained its vital force. The three together sketch the Baltic micro-theology of transitions: every threshold needed its guardian.
