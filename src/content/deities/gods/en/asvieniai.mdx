## Ašvieniai — The Divine Horse Twins

On the ridge of every well-kept Lithuanian farmhouse stood *žirgeliai*: two carved wooden horse heads crossed at the peak of the roof, facing outward to guard the household from every direction. These modest finials were the last living trace of one of the most ancient divine pairs in the Baltic world — the Ašvieniai, the divine horse twins who pulled the chariot of the Sun goddess Saulė on her daily journey across the sky.

The name Ašvieniai derives from *ašva*, the Baltic word for horse, a term that connects directly to the Sanskrit *aśva* and the Vedic divine horsemen known as the Aśvins. The parallel is unmistakable: like the Baltic twins, the Aśvins were celestial horsemen who heralded the dawn, rescued the distressed, and served as protectors of those who called upon them. Their Latvian counterparts, the *Dieva dēli* (Sons of God), appear throughout the *dainas* as cosmic riders bridging the gap between heaven and earth.

In Lithuanian cosmological tradition, the Ašvieniai were the engine of solar time. Each dawn they harnessed Saulė's chariot and drew it from the eastern edge of the world to the west, completing the cycle that made agriculture, animal husbandry, and human life possible. Their labour was not servitude but devotion — the unceasing act that kept the cosmic order turning.

Household protection was their other great role. A home that bore their carved image on the rooftop was under divine guardianship; illness, misfortune, and malevolent spirits would be repelled by the twins' watchful presence. When a new house was built, the *žirgeliai* were the last element raised, placed by the craftsman with a prayer that the divine twins would take up residence in the home and bless all who lived beneath the crossed horse heads.
