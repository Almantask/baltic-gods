## Upinis / Ežerinis — Guardians of Rivers and Lakes

Upinis (*Upitrum*) and Ežerinis (*Aszarinis*) are the Lithuanian divine guardians of flowing and still water respectively. In a landscape as waterway-rich as the Baltic—where rivers defined territorial boundaries, lakes provided protein through fishing, and winter ice was both highway and hazard—the personification of water was not poetic metaphor but theological necessity.

Upinis presided over rivers in their dynamic aspect: current, flood, the seasonal variation between trickle and torrent. He was the lord of the river's generosity — its fish, its irrigation water, its power to carry boats — and of its danger: the spring floods that could wash away crops, the treacherous fords where travelers drowned. Fishermen offered to Upinis before casting their nets; travelers prayed before crossing. Every river had its resident deity, but Upinis was the general principle underlying all river cult.

Ežerinis governed the still and deep: the lake. Baltic lakes often reach considerable depths in geologically young terrain; their bottoms were unknown country, their mysteries real. Ežerinis was the lord of this silent world, controlling the fish populations, the quality of the water, and the temperament of the surface. Lake offerings were typically made from a boat — food, grain, small household items — dropped to the god below.

Together they represent the full hydrological cycle of the Baltic interior: the moving water and the standing water, the river's energy and the lake's depth, the dynamism and the stillness that together constitute the aquatic world on which Baltic civilization depended.
