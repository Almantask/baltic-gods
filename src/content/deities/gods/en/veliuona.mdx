## Veliuona — Mistress of the Dead

In the Lithuanian understanding of death, the departed did not vanish from the world — they transformed into *vėlės*, ancestral spirits who lingered near the places they had loved, required feeding at seasonal feasts, and could influence the fortune of the family they had left behind. Presiding over this delicate, ongoing relationship between the living and the dead was Veliuona, the solemn mistress of the realm of spirits.

Her name belongs to the vast *vel-* root that permeates Baltic and Slavic reflections of the afterlife: the same root that gives us *Velnias* the cattle god and guardian of the underworld, the spirit-feast of *Vėlinės*, and the *vėlės* themselves. Veliuona was the feminine aspect of this domain — where Velnias was associated with the cattle, the wealth, and the generative power of the underworld, Veliuona governed the arrivals, the welcome, and the ongoing care of the dead in their new home.

Jan Łasicki's record names her *Velluonam* among the Samogitian deities, and her cult centred on the autumn festival of Vėlinės, when families gathered at gravesites, lit candles, left food, and opened a dialogue with their ancestors. Veliuona was invoked to ensure that the dead were settled and content — a restless spirit was a dangerous spirit, and keeping Veliuona's realm in good order was an act of survival as much as piety.

In folk tradition, Veliuona was associated with ancient oaks, deep wells, and the crossroads at the edge of the village — liminal places that marked the boundary between ordinary life and the spirit world. To stand at such a crossroads at midnight during Vėlinės was to stand in her presence, in the thin moment when the living and the dead could speak directly to one another.
