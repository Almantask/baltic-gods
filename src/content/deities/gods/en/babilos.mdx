# Babilos and Matergabiae — The Domestic Cultivators

In the Samogitian tradition recorded by Jan Łasicki, two companion spirits presided over the most vital acts of domestic life: **Babilos**, the guardian of brewing and fermentation, and **Matergabiae**, the mother of the sacred hearth fire. Together they embodied the spiritual heart of the farmstead — the warmth that sustained life through winter and the fermented drink that sealed every covenant, feast, and rite of passage.

Babilos (*pro potionibus* — for the sake of drinks) was invoked before every batch of beer or mead was prepared. The fermenting vessel was considered a sacred space, and the yeast's mysterious work of transformation was believed to require divine permission. Scholars have linked Babilos to **Bubilas**, the bee god, suggesting a common origin in the veneration of sweet, intoxicating liquids that brought humans into temporary communion with the divine. The ale that foamed in the wooden keg was not merely food; it was a living offering, and Babilos ensured that it rose properly and brought joy rather than illness.

Matergabiae — literally "Mother Gabija" — was a title applied to the hearth goddess **Gabija** in her most intimate role as the nurturer of the newborn flame. She was the *matrem ignis*, the primal mother whose fire had been carried from the tribal sanctuary into the family home. Every evening, the eldest woman of the household would tend the embers and whisper prayers to Matergabiae, ensuring the continuity of the fire through the night. To let the hearth fire die was an omen of terrible misfortune.

Babilos and Matergabiae were thus the twin pillars of Samogitian domestic religion: the one ensuring the fruitfulness of fermentation, the other the permanence of the sacred flame. Together they blessed every feast, every naming ceremony, every wedding toast with their invisible presence, weaving the spiritual thread that connected the family to the gods above and the ancestral dead below.
