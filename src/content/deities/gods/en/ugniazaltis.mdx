# Ugniažaltis — The Fiery Snake of the Hearth

At the intersection of fire and earth, where the hearth flame met the sacred serpent coiled in the darkness, there lived **Ugniažaltis** — the Fiery Snake. His very name fused the two most potent sacred forces of Baltic domestic religion: *ugnis* (fire) and *žaltys* (the grass snake). Where the common **Pagirinis** was a gentle, milk-drinking spirit of household protection, the Ugniažaltis was something more formidable — a luminous, golden-scaled being whose presence announced either great blessing or terrible destruction.

In folklore compiled by Jonas Basanavičius and others in the 19th century, the Ugniažaltis appears as a creature that flies by night, trailing a streak of flame across the dark sky. Farmers who saw such a sight might interpret it as a spirit coming to fill their grain stores with supernatural abundance — or as a harbinger of the fire that would consume a neighbor's barn. The Ugniažaltis was not malicious by nature, but it carried power so condensed that it could not pass through the human world without leaving a mark.

The mythologist Norbertas Vėlius situates the Ugniažaltis within the liminal category of Baltic creatures that bridge the chthonic and celestial realms. The snake, by its nature, belongs to the underworld — it lives in the earth, emerges from beneath stones, and is connected to ancestral spirits. But when that snake becomes fire, it ascends toward the sky, toward **Perkūnas** and the lightning bolt. The Ugniažaltis is thus a cosmic mediator, carrying the energy of the earth's depth up to the heights of the thunder god's domain.

Households that honored **Gabija** and kept the hearth flame pure were said to occasionally glimpse the Ugniažaltis in the embers — a brief golden shimmer that told them their fire was blessed. To see its full form was both an honor and a warning: the divine was present, and the family must conduct themselves with corresponding sanctity.
