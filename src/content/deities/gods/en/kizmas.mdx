## Kizmas — Guardian of the Woodpile

In the long darkness of a Baltic winter, with snow piled against the gates and temperatures dropping below freezing for weeks at a stretch, the woodpile was the difference between life and death. The stacked cords of split timber, the bundles of kindling, the dry logs set aside from the previous summer's work — all of it required more than human vigilance to remain dry, intact, and sufficient through until spring. That divine vigilance was the work of Kizmas.

Jan Łasicki, cataloguing Samogitian deities circa 1582, recorded Kizmas as the spirit who protected woodpiles and tree fragments (*arborum ramenta*) — the chips, splinters, and offcuts that accumulated from the woodcutter's work and served as kindling. The god's domain was therefore both practical and symbolic: he watched over the stored fuel that kept the sacred household fire burning, the fire that in turn was tended by its own domestic deity and kept the family warm, the food cooked, the prayers offered.

Invoking Kizmas was part of the autumn preparation ritual, when families completed their woodcutting before the first frosts and formally committed the stacked supply to the god's protection. A small offering — a piece of bread, a drop of beer — would be left atop the woodpile with a spoken request: that the wood remain dry and sound, that no rot or theft should deplete it, and that Kizmas would hold the supply together until the warmth returned.

Kizmas represents the principle that preparation is a spiritual act. The months of hard labour that went into gathering and splitting winter wood were not merely physical exertion but an act of partnership with the divine — the human doing the work, the god ensuring its fruits would last.
