## Milinė — Spirit of the Living Mill

The watermill was not simply a machine in the Baltic agricultural world — it was a living space, operating through the living forces of water and wind, conducting the sacred transformation of grain into flour on which survival depended. Where other craftspeople worked with dead materials, the miller worked with living forces. And where living forces operate, spirits of those forces are present. Milinė was the particular spirit of the mill — the one who inhabited the millstones, watched over the grinding, and could either assist or obstruct the process depending on how she was treated.

Jonas Balys catalogued the beliefs surrounding mill spirits across Lithuania: mills were sites of late-night strangeness, places where travelers heard grinding when no one was working, where white forms were glimpsed between the mill wheels. A small offering — a handful of grain, a dish of porridge left on the millstone at nightfall — kept Milinė content and the machinery in order. Certain days were forbidden for milling (holy days, liminal calendar dates), and millers who worked through these prohibitions found their gears jammed, their stones cracking, their flour contaminated. These failures were not mechanical but moral.

Pranė Dundulienė situates Milinė within the broader Baltic tradition of professional spirits: the spirit of the kiln, the spirit of the smithy, the spirit of the fishing net. Each transformative craft had its presiding intelligence, and the mill — which transformed the raw substance of the harvest into the substance of daily bread — was among the most important sites of this kind of sacred attention. The millstone (girnos) recurred as a religious symbol in Baltic material culture, its circular grinding motion a microcosm of the agricultural cycle.
