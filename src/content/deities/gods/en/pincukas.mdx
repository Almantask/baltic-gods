## Pinčukas — The Devil Next Door

Pinčukas is not an abstract force of cosmic evil but a named, located, somewhat bumbling devil who lives in the Paudruvė marshes of northern Lithuania and is perpetually trying to make advantageous deals with his human neighbours. He builds mills that sink overnight, offers bargains that collapse at the moment of collection, and attempts to court human women in elaborate and inevitably doomed courtship schemes. Jonas Balys catalogued the regional legends that cluster around Pinčukas, noting that his personality — clumsy, scheming, easily outsmarted — distinguishes him from the grander cosmic Velnias.

What makes Pinčukas theologically interesting is precisely his proximity. Unlike the distant underworld lords of other traditions, this devil is reachable — he can be negotiated with, tricked, beaten at cards, and evaded through folklore-standard means (crowing rooster, holy water, the sign of the cross after Christianization). His failures are almost comic, and yet he remains genuinely dangerous because he is persistent and because the deals he offers are always initially attractive. He embodies the Baltic understanding that the chthonic world is not far away but immediate — in the next marsh, under the mill wheel, at the edge of the winter field.

Kazys Boruta's 1945 novel *Baltaragio malūnas* drew on authentic Pinčukas folk material and amplified it into a tragic-comic portrait that became one of the touchstones of Lithuanian national literature. In Boruta's telling, Pinčukas is simultaneously the devil and the embodiment of all the beautiful, dangerous things that old folk knowledge understood and that modernity was forgetting. The novel's Pinčukas is not evil so much as out of place — a creature of the old world trying to operate in a new one, losing every time.
