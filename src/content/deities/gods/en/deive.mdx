## Deivė — The Divine Feminine

Deivė is not a specific deity but a category: the generic Baltic word for goddess or divine woman, derived from the Proto-Indo-European root *deiw- meaning to shine, the sky, or the celestial realm. It is the feminine counterpart to *dievas* (god), and in the earliest Baltic religious consciousness it simply meant "a divine female being" without further specification.

As Baltic religious life became more differentiated over centuries, the term *deivė* accumulated specific associations depending on context. In elevated theological usage it referred to the great goddesses: Laima, Žemyna, the Governing Deivės. In folk usage, *deivės* (plural) came to describe the collective groups of supernatural women who inhabited the margins of the human world — the forests, lake shores, and meadows. These were not precisely the Laumės, though they overlapped; they were the more ancient stratum of divine feminine, less defined by trickster characteristics and more by the raw numinous power of femininity aligned with nature.

In specific regional accounts, the Deivės maintained shrines in sacred groves where local women — never men — left offerings of wool, bread, and linen. Jesuit missionaries in 17th-century Samogitia recorded the destruction of such shrines with dismay at how tenaciously the female-cult had survived Christianization.

The concept of *deivė* as a generic category also served an important intellectual function: it acknowledged that divinity was not limited to named, individuated beings. The divine could manifest as a type, a force, a category of female numinosity — the unnamed but deeply felt presence of sacred feminity in the natural world.
