# Prigirstis — The One Who Makes Heard

In Jan Łasicki's list of Samogitian deities, a brief but philosophically rich entry describes **Prigirstis**: *Prigirszczium, qui audire facit* — "Prigirstis, he who makes one hear." This deceptively simple phrase reveals a nuanced theology of prayer. Where **Ausklauts** was the divine faculty of listening, Prigirstis was the active agent who *ensured* that the channel between the human voice and the divine ear was open and functioning.

The distinction matters. Ausklauts was the cosmic ear turned toward Earth — the assurance that every utterance was registered by the divine. Prigirstis was something more dynamic: the spirit who facilitated the act of communication itself, who cleared the path between the earthly prayer and its divine recipient. In a pre-literate culture where oral formulas and prayers carried the full weight of the community's relationship with the divine, the fear that a prayer might be poorly worded, badly timed, or simply not reach the gods was real and consequential.

Prigirstis addressed this fear directly. By invoking him at the beginning of a ritual, the practitioner was not just hoping that the gods would listen — they were activating the divine infrastructure of communication, ensuring that their words would arrive with full clarity and force. He was, in a sense, the divine postman of Baltic religion: not the message itself, not the recipient, but the sacred channel through which the message traveled.

This theology of mediated prayer reflects a sophisticated understanding of ritual efficacy. The Balts did not simply shout their prayers into the sky and hope for the best. They understood that communication with the divine required appropriate preparation, the right words spoken at the right time, and the invocation of the spirits who presided over the act of communication itself. Prigirstis was the guarantor that the conversation had been properly initiated.
