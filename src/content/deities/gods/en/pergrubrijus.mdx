## Pergrubrijus

Pergrubrijus is the Prussian and Lithuanian deity of spring, vegetation, and the awakening of the earth. Also recorded as *Pergrubris* and *Pergrubius*, he appears in two of the most important 16th-century sources for Baltic religion: the Sudovian Book (*Suduviu knygele*) and the catalogue of Jan Lasicki, where he is described as the lord of the first grass and the young green shoots (*qui herbis et oleribus praeest*).

The central image associated with Pergrubrijus is a key. He is the one who holds the key to the frozen earth — and in spring, after the long winter imprisonment, he inserts that key and turns it, releasing the ice from the soil, allowing the water to flow, and inviting the first green shoots to push toward the light. This image of unlocking the earth is one of the most vivid in the Baltic mythological tradition, and it reflects the profound agricultural dependence on the coming of spring that shaped Baltic life for millennia.

The celebration of Pergrubrijus coincided with the *Jurgines* festival (St. George's Day, late April), when livestock were driven out to pasture for the first time after the winter. Community rituals involving bread, beer, and songs were conducted in the fields to welcome his presence and to invite his blessing for the coming agricultural year. In later Christian synthesis, many of his functions were absorbed by the figure of St. George, who similarly "opens" the spring with the power of his sanctity.

Pergrubrijus represents the joyful, life-affirming power of renewal — the force that assures the farmer, still shivering in the last frosts of April, that the green world is coming back and that the long, hungry wait of winter is finally over.
