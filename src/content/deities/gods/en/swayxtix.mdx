## Swayxtix — The Shining God

Swayxtix, whose name derives from the Old Prussian word *swāigstan* meaning "light" or "shining," is the god of celestial light in the Old Prussian pantheon. Where Okopirmus represents the static, eternal order of the sky, Swayxtix is the active, warm, life-giving presence of light as it falls on the physical world.

In the Sudovian Book, Swayxtix is listed among the major deities who must be honored to ensure that crops receive the sun's blessing. His function was believed to be literal: he was said to "enlighten" seeds, grasses, and livestock — meaning not merely to illuminate them but to infuse them with the life-giving energy that enables growth. Without his presence turning toward the fields, the harvest would fail.

Swayxtix represents the daytime aspect of Prussian celestial religion — the visible, tangible blessing of the sky made manifest as light. This sets him apart from the storm-god Perkuns, who rules the sky through power and thunder, and from Occopirmus, who rules through eternal authority. Swayxtix rules through radiance — the generous, unconditional outpouring of light that makes life possible.

His connection to the morning star and the sun's brilliance links him to a broader Baltic pattern of light-deities. Simon Grunau's chronicle (1529) places Swayxtix in the company of the most important Prussian gods, reflecting the central role that solar veneration played in Prussian religious life before the Christianization of the region by the Teutonic Knights in the 13th century.
