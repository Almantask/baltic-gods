## Deivės Valdytojos — The Governing Goddesses, Weavers of Fate

The Deivės Valdytojos — the Governing Goddesses — are the Baltic functional equivalent of the Greek Moirai, the Norse Norns, or the Roman Parcae. They are the weavers of fate, the divine workers who spin the thread of each human life at birth, weave it through the events of its allotted years, and cut it at the moment of death. They are not abstract concepts but active agents who labor continuously at the great loom of destiny.

Their title — *valdytojos* from *valdyti*, to govern or manage — is precise. They do not merely witness fate; they administer it. The difference is significant: a witnessing deity is passive, but a governing one is active, responsive, potentially influenceable. Baltic folk tradition preserved the belief that the Deivės could be approached at birth — through ritual, offering, and prayer — to petition for a favorable life-thread: long, strong, and brightly colored.

The number of the Deivės varies across traditions: sometimes three, sometimes seven, reflecting different aspects of the fate-complex. Some versions distinguish the spinner, the weaver, and the cutter; others assign each goddess a phase of the human lifecycle. They work alongside Laima, who decrees fate, while the Deivės Valdytojos implement it. Laima is the lawgiver; the Deivės are the craftswomen.

At the birth of a child, folk custom held that the Deivės came unseen to the cradle to determine the new life's course. The spinning sounds heard in a house on certain nights — the footfall-quiet rhythm of a wheel turning — were understood as the Governing Goddesses at their work, weaving someone's thread somewhere near.
