## Pilvytis — God of Wealth and Full Granaries

Pilvytis is the Lithuanian deity of material abundance, recorded by Jan Lasicki as Pilvittum — the one who governed the wealthy and prosperous. His name derives from pilvas, the Baltic word for belly, a concept that carried far greater cultural weight than it does today. In an agricultural society, a full belly represented divine favor, skilled stewardship, and the blessing of the earth.

Pilvytis was specifically invoked to ensure that granaries remained full through the long Baltic winter. His realm was the kleti — the separate storage buildings where dried grain, hemp, linen, and preserved goods were kept. To invoke his blessing, offerings of the first-ground flour or the first-brewed beer were left at the granary threshold as the harvest was being stored.

Unlike the sky gods or storm deities who operated on a cosmic scale, Pilvytis was a deity of intimate, domestic concern. He represented the tangible evidence of hard work rewarded: a house where the shelves were never bare, the children never went hungry, and the animals were fed through spring. His worship was part of the everyday material theology that governed Baltic rural life.

The etymological kinship between Pilvytis and the Old Prussian Pilvittis (recorded in the Sudovian Book) suggests that a deity of wealth and abundance was present across all three major Baltic tribal groups, reflecting the universal importance of food security in the ancient world.
