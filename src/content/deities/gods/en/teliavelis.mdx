## The Forge at the World's Edge

Before the sun gave its first light, there was darkness and a forge. Teliavelis — the Celestial Blacksmith — struck his divine hammer against the primordial anvil and shaped the great disc of the sun. With a single heave he flung it into the sky, and the world ignited with daylight. This is the central act of Lithuanian cosmogonic myth recorded in the Hypatian Codex (13th century), making Teliavelis one of the few Baltic gods whose first written attestation reaches medieval chronicle records.

## The Smith of Culture

Teliavelis is a cultural hero as much as a deity — the one who transforms raw material into civilised order. Just as Hephaestus forged weapons and armour for Olympus or Ilmarinen hammered the sky-vault in Kalevala, Teliavelis brings the technical mastery that makes habitation of the world possible. The sun he made is not merely a physical body but a moral force: it reveals, judges, and warms. His role therefore overlaps with both creation and justice, associating him closely with Dievas and Perkūnas.

## Amber and the Divine Sparks

In Lithuanian folk memory, amber — the Baltic's most sacred material — was sometimes interpreted as fallen sparks from Teliavelis's forge. The golden resin washed up on shores carried the memory of a primordial fire, a by-product of the creation event itself. Ritual deposits of amber and iron at burial mounds echo this mythology: the dead were sent back toward the forge that made the light, accompanied by gifts that honoured the craftsman's eternal work.

## Legacy of Light

Teliavelis stands at the intersection of craft, creation, and cosmology. Later Lithuanian tradition sometimes merged his identity with the folk figure of Kalvelis, the little blacksmith who beats new horseshoes for the sun each morning — a diminished but persistent reflection of the original myth. The image of a divine smith pounding celestial bodies into existence runs through all Baltic mythology, reminding each generation that the ordered world was made by skilled hands, not accidental chance.
