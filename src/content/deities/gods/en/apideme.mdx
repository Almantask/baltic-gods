## Apidėmė — Guardian of the Homestead's Edge

Every home in old Lithuania had a boundary: the line where the cultivated yard ended and the uncultivated world began, marked by a fence, a gate, a row of stones, or simply the invisible threshold of family territory. This boundary was not merely physical — it was sacred, spiritually charged, watched over by Apidėmė, the guardian of the homestead's edge.

Her name composes two ancient Baltic elements: the prefix *api-* (around, encompassing) and a root related to *demas* or *dem-* (home, dwelling place), giving her a name meaning something like "she who surrounds the house." Jan Łasicki's sixteenth-century catalogue records her among the Samogitian deities of the household, associated specifically with the moment of moving house — a dangerously liminal event when the family left the protection of their established sacred boundary and had to re-establish it elsewhere.

When a Lithuanian family moved to a new home, they did not simply pack their possessions — they performed rituals to invite Apidėmė to follow them. Offerings were made at the threshold of the old house: bread, salt, and beer poured onto the earth at the gate posts, with a spoken request that the guardian of the boundary should recognise the family's continued presence and accompany them to the new farmstead. Without this rite, it was believed that Apidėmė would be left behind, confused, and the new boundary would be spiritually unguarded.

At the new home, the first act was to re-establish her presence: the gate posts were anointed, bread was buried at the four corners of the yard, and the family formally acknowledged that a new edge had been drawn and a new guardian installed. Apidėmė's cult reflects the profound importance that Baltic domestic religion placed on the concept of sacred space — every boundary was a spiritual fact, and every spiritual fact required its own divine custodian.
