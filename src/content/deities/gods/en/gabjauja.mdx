## Gabjauja — Guardian of the Grain Barn

Gabjauja is the tutelary deity of the threshing barn (jauja) and the stored harvest. Her name combines gabija — sacred fire — with jauja, the specialized building where grain was dried using open fire before threshing. She is, in essence, the goddess who makes the most dangerous stage of the harvest safe.

The threshing barn was a vital and perilous space in Baltic village life. A fire burning too hot, or grain dried improperly, could destroy the community's entire food supply for the winter. Gabjauja's protection was therefore not a ceremonial nicety but a practical necessity. Jan Lasicki's 16th-century list records offerings specifically made to her for the protection of grain (pro frugibus).

In ritual practice, the completion of the harvest was marked by a ceremony in Gabjauja's honor. A rooster — the sacred bird of dawn and fire — or a young pig was sacrificed at the end of threshing, and a portion of the first-ground flour was burned or poured before the barn door. These acts acknowledged that the successful harvest was not merely the result of human labor but of divine protection.

Gabjauja represents the sanctity of stored food and the vigilance required to preserve it. She is a reminder that in the Baltic agricultural world, the boundary between survival and starvation was often just one careless fire away.
