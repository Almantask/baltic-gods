## Diviriks — Leader of the Gods

In the year 1252, the Hypatian Chronicle — one of the primary sources of medieval Rus history — recorded a remarkable moment: the Lithuanian King Mindaugas, newly baptised as the kingdom's first Christian ruler, was secretly still offering sacrifices to his old gods. Among the deities listed in this clandestine devotion was Diviriks, named alongside Laima, Teliavelis, and Andajus as one of the divine powers whose favour the king continued to seek even after his formal conversion.

The name Diviriks composes two archaic Baltic elements: *diev-* (god, divine) and *-rikis* (leader, chieftain, commander), giving a compound meaning of "Leader of the Gods" or "Commander of the Divine Assembly." This is not a personal name in the conventional sense but a title — and scholars including Norbertas Vėlius and Gintaras Beresnevičius have argued persuasively that it is an epithet for Perkūnas, the thunder god, in his political and military aspect.

In the Baltic theological imagination, Perkūnas was the active warrior-deity, the champion who defeated chaos, drove away the serpent Velnias from his cattle, and maintained the cosmic order through force. As Diviriks, he was this same deity elevated to the position of chief sovereign of the gods — the divine equivalent of the earthly duke or king. For Mindaugas, secretly worshipping Diviriks was an act of political as well as religious logic: the ruler of Lithuania was seeking the blessing of the ruler of heaven.

Diviriks survived as a concept in Lithuanian folk tradition as the "lord god" of lightning and justice, the supreme arbiter whose approval was invoked before important military expeditions and state decisions. His title reminds us that Baltic religion was not simply a nature cult but contained sophisticated ideas about divine sovereignty, hierarchy, and the relationship between earthly power and heavenly sanction.
