# Devintos — The Sacred Power of Nine

In the Baltic ritual universe, the number nine was not merely a quantity — it was a force, a threshold, a key. Nine-day festivals, nine-herb infusions (*devynžolės*), nine repetitions of a prayer, nine-headed dragons in the depths of the mythological underworld: the number nine appeared at every point where ordinary time and space gave way to the extraordinary, where the human scale opened into the divine. **Devintos** — literally "The Ninth" — was the personification of this sacred numeric power.

Jan Malecki recorded Devintos in his 16th-century *Libellus* (Small Book) on Lithuanian and Prussian customs as a deity specifically invoked for "multiplication" and the finalization of ritual processes. This matches the broader Baltic understanding of nine as the number of completeness — three multiplied by three, the sky god's number squared into the full cycle. To perform a ritual nine times was to exhaust all possible dimensions of its efficacy; to invoke a deity with nine repetitions was to leave no spiritual angle uncovered.

In agricultural practice, Devintos was most prominently associated with the harvest and sowing rituals. A farmer planting his first seeds might walk the perimeter of his field nine times, or bury nine seeds at each corner, trusting that Devintos would amplify the earth's response to the offering. In healing, the nine-herb formula (*devynžolė arbata*) combined the powers of nine specific plants, and the preparation required nine-fold attention to ensure that each plant's spirit contributed fully to the cure.

Devintos did not occupy the commanding position of Perkūnas or Laima in the divine hierarchy. He was a specialist — a ritual technician of the divine, the force that ensured a nine-fold action achieved nine-fold results. In a tradition that valued precision and respect in ritual above all else, this was not a minor role but a crucial one.
