## Javine

Javine is the Lithuanian goddess who watches over the harvested grain and the threshing barn (*klojimas*). Her name comes from *javai* (grain / crops), and she represents the protective divine presence that inhabits the barn from the moment the first sheaves are brought in after the harvest.

First mentioned in the writings of Jakubas Brodovskis in the 18th century and confirmed by Pilypas Ruigys, Javine fills a specific and vital ecological niche in the Baltic mythological system. Zemyna, the earth goddess, presides over the living, growing plants. But once the grain is cut and brought under the roof, it enters Javine's domain. She guards the stored bounty against the twin threats of rot and fire, against mice that gnaw the sacks, and against the malicious interference of dark spirits that might spoil what the land had given.

The most important ritual act associated with Javine was the handling of the first and last sheaves of the harvest. The first sheaf was laid with special care and sometimes carried into the farmhouse before being returned to the barn — a symbolic recognition of her authority. The last sheaf, known as the *babyte* (grandmother), was often braided into a doll-like form and placed in the corner of the barn as an offering and a home for the harvest spirit through the winter.

Javine represents the covenant between human labor and divine grace: we plant, we tend, we cut — but it is the goddess who decides whether the grain survives the winter intact, ready to feed the family and seed the fields of the coming year.
