## Pliuktis — God of Rain

Pliuktis is the Lithuanian deity of rain, recorded by Jan Łasicki in the sixteenth century as *Plusczium*, the god who "ruled over the rain" (*qui pluviam regit*). In the Baltic agricultural world, rain was the most critical variable in the growing season. While Perkūnas controlled the storm, Pliuktis embodied the rain itself — its arrival, its abundance, and its gentleness upon the fields of flax and grain.

During drought years, communities gathered at sacred oak groves or field boundaries to invoke Pliuktis with communal prayer and small offerings. The rituals were urgent and practical: without adequate rain the flax harvest failed, the grain withered, and the winter stores did not fill. Pliuktis represented the specific, tangible blessing of moisture falling on cultivated soil.

The deity’s name is linguistically connected to *pliūptelėti* and *pliukšti* — Lithuanian words for splashing, gurgling, and the sound of water suddenly released. His essence was the moment of rain’s arrival: the first heavy drops drumming on dry earth, the smell of wet soil, the visible darkening of the sky’s margin just before the downpour.

Pliuktis belongs to the category of minor Lithuanian deities who governed specific phenomena rather than vast cosmic domains. He was not supreme but was indispensable for anyone whose survival depended on Baltic farmland. Every farmer who watched the sky through a dry spring knew Pliuktis by another name: necessity.
