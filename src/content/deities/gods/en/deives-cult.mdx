## Deivės — The Forest Goddesses Who Would Not Leave

The Jesuit missions to Samogitia in the late 16th and early 17th centuries encountered resistance of a kind they had not expected: not organized theological opposition, not warrior princes defending their faith, but women who quietly, persistently, and privately continued to walk into the forest and leave things there. The missionaries' annual letters — *Annuae Litterae Societatis Iesu* — record cases from the 1580s through the 1620s in which Jesuit priests discovered women's offerings at specific sacred trees: balls of wool, folded linen, braided belts, pieces of bread, sometimes coins. The trees themselves were hollow old oaks, ash trees, or lindens at specific spots in the forest — not marked by any visible religious structure, but remembered.

These were the Deivės: female deities who inhabited the forest and who concerned themselves specifically with women's matters. They presided over marriage prospects, fertility, safe childbirth, and the successful outcome of women's craft work — spinning, weaving, and the production of linen. Norbertas Vėlius, who analyzed the Jesuit accounts extensively, argued that the Deivės represent a direct continuity from the cult of the Great Goddess of the Neolithic and Bronze Age Baltic littoral — the same feminine divine presence, now scattered from central shrines into the specific trees and groves of the agricultural landscape. Each grove had its own Deivė, with her own preferences and taboos.

The Jesuits burned the sacred trees and held formal exorcisms of the groves. In some documented cases, women who continued the practice were publicly shamed or given penance. But folk memory of the Deivės did not disappear — it transformed. The women became the Laumės of later folk tradition: ambivalent forest spirits still associated with spinning and weaving, still connected to women's threshold experiences of birth and death, still potentially helpful and potentially dangerous. The Jesuits succeeded in removing the specific sacred trees. They could not remove the women's knowledge that something divine lived in the forest and could be approached with the right offerings.
