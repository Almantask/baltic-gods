## Šaukstis — Flies, Grain, and the Divine Administration of Pests

Of all the entries in Łasicki's list of Samogitian deities, Šaukstis is among the most specific and, at first glance, the most surprising: *Szuksztim pro muscis* — Šaukstis for the flies. A god of flies seems, to modern readers, either comic or trivial. To a 16th-century Lithuanian farmer, it was neither. Flies in the midsummer heat were a serious threat: they spread disease among cattle, contaminated stored grain and curing meat, reduced milk production in dairy animals, and made harvest work miserable. A serious infestation of stable flies or blowflies could damage the economic foundations of a farm in ways that no amount of human effort could quickly remedy.

The existence of Šaukstis reflects the same logic that produced the entire minor-deity tier of Baltic religion: if something was important enough to demand active management, it deserved a divine address. Šaukstis was not worshipped for his own sake but approached pragmatically — invoked to ensure that the insect world operated within tolerable limits, that the summer heat did not bring a catastrophic swarm, that the flies remained at their natural density rather than multiplying into catastrophe. Some scholars have proposed that specific herbs (wormwood, tansy, elderflower) used in the same contexts as Šaukstis invocations suggest that the ritual was paired with practical pest control — the divine and the botanical working in tandem.

Šaukstis also sits within a larger pattern of Baltic attention to the very small: bees had their own cult (Austėja and the bee rituals), ants were sometimes treated as sacred, snakes were household members. Baltic animism was not scale-selective in its sense of the sacred — the divine could inhabit a fly as readily as it could inhabit a storm. The world was comprehensively inhabited by presences, and the work of living in that world was the continuous negotiation of good relationships with all of them, from the sun moving overhead to the insects hovering around the midsummer grain.
