## Mārtiņš — The Rider Who Closes the Year

On the evening of November 10th in traditional Latvian farmsteads, women pulled on extra layers and went to the stable to light a small fire near the horses' stalls. They spoke soft words to the animals, poured beer on the threshold, and sacrificed a rooster — its blood smeared on the door posts. This was Mārtiņi, the festival of Mārtiņš, and it marked the most significant administrative deadline of the agricultural year. Debts were settled today. Farm laborers received their final payment. The year's balance sheet was closed. Mārtiņš had arrived.

In the dainas, Mārtiņš arrives as a wealthy rider on a white or roan horse, frost crystallizing in the air ahead of him. He brings the first hard freeze and with it, the permission to rest. The endless labor of sowing, growing, harvesting, and storing is finished. The farmstead gates close for winter. For horses, this was a sacred moment: they had carried the year's labor and now deserved protection through the months ahead. The rooster sacrifice in the stable was explicit — its blood invoked Mārtiņš's continued care for the animals through the dark months the sun would not see.

Pēteris Šmits noted that Mārtiņš absorbed aspects of older horse-protective deities, possibly aspects of Ūsiņš's autumn face, at the turn toward winter. His name shares roots with St. Martin of Tours, but the ritual complex is pre-Christian: the masked Mārtiņbērni mummers who visit farms on his evening, the specific stable rites, the settling of accounts — all of this speaks to a November threshold deity whose primary concern is not salvation but the orderly closing of one cycle and the protection of what was earned through the year's work.
