## Praamzius — The Eternal First Cause

Praamzius, whose name translates as 'The Primordial One' or 'The Eternal,' represents the most abstract dimension of Lithuanian theology. He is not a god who interferes in daily life but a timeless principle that set the cosmos in motion — closer to a metaphysical constant than an anthropomorphic deity.

In the theological reconstructions of Teodor Narbutt and later scholars, Praamzius sits above all other gods as the equivalent of the Greek Kronos or the Vedic Brahman: the ground of being itself. He dispatched the Great Flood to cleanse the world of injustice and sent the rainbow as a sign of his continued covenant with life. The oak, the most sacred Baltic tree, was his symbol on earth.

Praamzius is often equated with the more common divine name Dievas, differing mainly in emphasis — Dievas governs the moral order of the sky, while Praamzius emphasizes beginningless time. The two names may reflect different ritual contexts: Dievas in agricultural prayer, Praamzius in cosmogonic chant and philosophical reflection.

Because Narbutt's 19th-century reconstruction drew on fragmentary sources and comparative mythology, some scholars treat Praamzius as a partially literary creation. Nevertheless, the concept of an eternal, dispassionate first cause is consistent with other Indo-European traditions and may preserve genuine archaic Baltic thought about the nature of the divine.
