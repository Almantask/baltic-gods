## Algis — The One Who Carries Prayers Upward

When Jan Łasicki described the Samogitian deity Algis, he reached for the only concept in his Catholic vocabulary that fit: *Algi tanquam angelum* — Algis, like an angel. The comparison is illuminating not because Algis was a Christian angel but because the *function* was the same: a being who moves between the divine realm and the human one, who carries messages, who ensures that prayers arrive. Long before Abrahamic angelology reached the Baltic, the idea of sacred mediation had apparently already found its form.

The name Algis (or Algė in feminine form) connects to roots suggesting dedication, commitment, service. An alga in Lithuanian means a salary or wage — Algis might be understood as the deity who is permanently employed in the work of transmission, whose job is carrying. He does not grant wishes; he ensures that they are heard. He does not decide outcomes; he ensures that the petitioner's voice reaches the place where decisions are made. In a religious system where gods are specific and numerous — each ruling their own narrow domain — there would be a critical need for an intermediary who could navigate between them, passing the farmer's prayer about crops to the harvest gods, the traveler's prayer about roads to Bendikas, the new mother's prayer about her child to Laima.

Vėlius's analysis of the Łasicki material suggests that Algis represents a very early stratum of Baltic religious thinking — older than the individuated deity personalities, perhaps rooted in the shamanic tradition of the spiritual messenger who travels between worlds. Lithuanian folk tradition preserves traces of this figure in stories about *raganos* (witches) who send their spirits on missions, and in the *kaukučiai* (household spirits) who can be sent to carry information between the living and the dead. Algis was the sacred institutionalization of this need: a permanent courier between worlds.
