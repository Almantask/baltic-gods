## Žaltvykslės — The Snake in the Corner and the Light in the Marsh

The 1612 Jesuit report from Lithuania contains one of the most striking passages in the documentation of Baltic folk religion: almost every peasant household, the missionaries found, maintained a *žaltys* — a grass snake — in the house. These snakes were not wild visitors tolerated out of superstition; they were members of the household in a meaningful sense. They lived in specific corners, under the bed, behind the hearth, or beneath the floorboards. Each morning, the woman of the house set out a small bowl of milk for it. If the snake drank, the household would prosper that year. If it refused to eat, something was wrong. To kill one was the most serious sacrilege imaginable — thought to bring the death of the household patriarch or the burning of the farm within the year.

The *žaltvykslės* of the title are the plural form — literally 'snake-flickers' — which refers both to the collective spirit of household snakes and to a different phenomenon: the flickering blue lights sometimes seen moving through marshes and damp fields at night. Vėlius argued that these two phenomena were linked in the Baltic imagination because grass snakes were considered messengers of the earth world, the chthonic realm connected to fertility and the dead. The marsh lights were interpreted as the eyes or souls of sacred serpents traveling through the spirit world — their physical form invisible, only their luminous essence showing through the darkness.

The household snake cult has deep archaeological roots: serpentine ornaments and snake figurines appear in Baltic Bronze Age and Iron Age burial contexts across the region. In Latvia, the same tradition was documented under different names — the *zalkšu kults* — with the same milk offering, the same dire consequences for killing. Latvian dainas (folk songs) include laments when the family snake departs: 'The snake has left our house, our luck has gone with it.' In both traditions, the snake was not worshipped as a god but honored as a presence — the visible form of the earth's living goodwill toward the people who properly maintained it.
