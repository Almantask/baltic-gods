## Lopu māte — Mother of Livestock

Lopu māte is the Latvian guardian of domestic animals, the divine figure who walked through barns in the night hours counting and blessing the herds. She governed cattle, sheep, pigs, and horses — the entire livestock economy on which Latvian farming communities depended — and her favor meant the difference between a prosperous year and catastrophic loss.

Scholars identify Lopu māte as an aspect of Māra, the great goddess of the earth, cattle, and the material world. Where Māra’s domain was universal, Lopu māte was her specific manifestation in the barn. Farmers invoked her at the great seasonal turning points: at Jurgi (St. George’s Day, April 23) when livestock was first driven to pasture in spring, and at Mikēļi (Michaelmas, September 29) when they returned for winter housing.

The dainas associated with Lopu māte show her as a tall, silver-clad woman who moves silently between the sleeping animals, laying her hands on those who are sick and counting those who are healthy. If she found disorder — hungry animals, dirty stalls, unattended injuries — she could withdraw her protection, leaving the herd vulnerable to disease and predation.

Ritual practices connected to Lopu māte included keeping a clean barn with fresh bedding, providing adequate food and water, and marking the barn door at Jurgi with protective signs. Special milk offerings and the first-morning milking given without commercial purpose were traditional acts of acknowledgment. In this way Lopu māte expressed a theology of care: divine favor flowed toward those who extended that same care to the animals entrusted to them.
