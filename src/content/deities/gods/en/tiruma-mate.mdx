## Tīruma māte — Mother of the Plowed Field

Tīruma māte is the Latvian goddess of the open arable land, presiding over the plowed field from the first turning of soil in spring to the last harvested furrow in autumn. Her domain is the cultivated earth in its most vulnerable and most productive state — exposed, worked, seeded, and waiting. She governs the soil’s fertility, the evenness of germination, and the quality of whatever the farmer chose to entrust to her care.

The spring plowing was accompanied by ritual acknowledgment of Tīruma māte. The first furrow was particularly important: farmers would stop at the end of the first pass and offer a small portion of bread or seed to the opened earth, speaking words that invited the goddess to be present through the season. The act established a working relationship between the farmer and the divine power of the tilth, a relationship that would need to be honored through the months ahead.

Tīruma māte differs from Zemes māte, who governs the earth as a cosmic whole, by focusing specifically on the humanized soil — the fields that have been cleared, dug, and shaped by human labor. She is a deity of culture as much as nature: her domain exists only because humans made it, and she represents the divine response to that human investment of work.

In the Latvian dainas, Tīruma māte is addressed directly in spring songs asking for her blessing on the seed. She is a generous figure when properly acknowledged, associated with the satisfying sight of even green growth across a dark field, the smell of loam turning in the morning air, and the quiet pride of a farmer looking over well-prepared ground.
