## White Lady — Omen at the Threshold of Death

The White Lady (*Baltoji Ponia*) is one of the most persistent spectral figures in Baltic folklore. Dressed entirely in white and appearing at ancient mounds (*piliakalniai*), crossroads, manor-house windows, or the edges of forest clearings, she is understood as an omen: her appearance signals an imminent death in the community, the passing of a significant era, or a major threshold event in the life of the household.

Norbertas Vėlius noted that white was the color of death in the oldest Baltic symbolic system — the color of the soul's state after separation from the body, the color of bones, and the color of the liminal space between the living world and the realm of the ancestors. This archaic meaning of white-as-death was reversed in the Christian period, when black became the mourning color, but the White Lady retained her older significance in the folk imagination.

Marija Gimbutas interpreted the White Lady as a late folkloric memory of the Goddess of Death and Transition in her benign, dignified aspect. Unlike Giltinė — the stark, skeletal figure who arrives to collect souls — the White Lady is mournful and gentle, associated with the soul's passage rather than its termination. She represents the moment of crossing: the boundary between the world of the living and the domain of the ancestors that every soul must eventually traverse.
