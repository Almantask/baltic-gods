## Saules meitas — Daughters of the Sun

The Saules meitas — Daughters of the Sun — are among the most beautifully documented figures in Baltic mythology. In the Latvian dainas collected by Krisjanis Barons, they appear as golden-haired celestial maidens weaving, washing, or dancing at the edge of the sky-sea, personifying the atmospheric phenomena of dawn, dusk, and the shifting light of the heavens.

In the 'celestial wedding' cycle that forms the backbone of Latvian mythological cosmology, the Saules meitas are the brides sought by the Dieva deli (Sons of Dievs). The cycle describes the courtship, the preparation of the dowry, the journey across the sky, and the transition of light from the sun's household to that of the sky-god — an encoded account of the eternal solar cycle rendered in human terms.

The Saules meitas are not simply decorative figures. They are the patronesses of women's craft work: weaving, spinning, and the preparation of dowries. Latvian women invoked them when beginning important needlework, seeing the celestial maidens' golden weaving as the archetype of their own labor. The 'golden garment' woven from sunrays was the most precious of all dowry items in the mythological world.

Scholar Haralds Biezais established that the Saules meitas belong to a pan-Baltic and wider Indo-European complex of solar maidens, linking them to the Vedic dawn goddesses Ushas's companions and the proto-Slavic solar maidens. Their presence in the dainas is one of the most direct surviving transmissions of an archaic Indo-European mythological theme.
