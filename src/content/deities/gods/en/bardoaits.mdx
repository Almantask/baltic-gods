## Bardoaits — God of Ships

Bardoaits, also known as Gardaitis or Gardoaitis, is the Prussian deity of ships, sailors, and fishermen. He is the specific patron of those who made their living on the Baltic Sea, particularly the fishing communities of the Sambia peninsula — that blade of land thrusting into the Baltic at the westernmost edge of the Prussian coast.

The Sudovian Book (*Sudauer Büchlein*), a 16th-century catalogue of Prussian deities, describes Bardoaits as the god to whom offerings were given for a calm voyage and a successful catch. Sailors would offer a portion of their first catch to ensure his continued favor and protection from the unseen forces of the deep. His name is believed to derive from the Old Prussian word *garda*, meaning "boat" or "ship" — suggesting that the deity's identity was inseparable from the vessel of the sea-going life.

Bardoaits is often interpreted by scholars as a localized or maritime aspect of the fertility god Potrimpas, representing the abundance of the sea rather than the abundance of the earth. Unlike the storm-god Bangputys, who represents the terrifying and destructive power of the sea, Bardoaits' focus is protective: the safety of the vessel, the favorability of the harbor, the loyalty of the wind.

With the Teutonic conquest of Prussia in the 13th century, the coastal religious traditions of the Sambian tribes were suppressed along with all other elements of Prussian paganism. Bardoaits survived only in the German-language documentation of those who had suppressed him — a reminder that entire worlds of devotion can be preserved only in the archives of their destroyers.
