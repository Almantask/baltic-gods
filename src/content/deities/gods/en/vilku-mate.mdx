## Vilku māte — Mother of Wolves

Vilku māte is one of the most ambivalent figures in the Latvian māte tradition, a deity who simultaneously governs the wolves themselves and acts as the protector of livestock against them. This double function reflects a profound feature of Baltic religious thought: the boundary between the domestic and the wild was not a wall but a negotiated threshold, and the deity of that threshold held power on both sides.

Farmers invoked Vilku māte at the beginning of the grazing season, asking her to direct the wolves’ hunger away from their flocks. The request was not a command but a petition, acknowledging her authority over the wolves rather than denying it. She could restrain her wolves or release them; the farmer’s task was to be in correct relationship with her so that her restraint continued.

The dainas associated with Vilku māte present her as a powerful figure moving at the forest edge, communicating with both the wolves in the darkness and the shepherd watching his flock in the last light. She is not cruel and not entirely merciful; she is the law of the boundary, and the law applies equally to the wolf pack hunting and to the sheep that have strayed too far from the fold.

In Latvian folk practice, specific protective charms spoken at twilight called on Vilku māte to bind the wolves’ jaws from the animals in her territory. The language was respectful and formal, acknowledging her sovereign right over the wild creatures while stating the human claim to protection. This negotiated, diplomatic quality marks the Latvian relationship with the wild as one of the most sophisticated in Baltic religious tradition.
