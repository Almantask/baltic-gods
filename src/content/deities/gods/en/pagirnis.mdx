## Pagirnis — Guardian of the Quern

Before metal mills and before windmills, the grain that sustained Baltic life was ground by hand on the stone quern: two heavy circular stones, the lower fixed, the upper rotated by a wooden handle, grinding rye or barley between their faces to produce the coarse flour from which black bread was made. This process was a daily labour for the women of every household, repetitive and physically demanding, and the quern stone was one of the most important objects in the entire home. Beneath it, according to Lithuanian folk belief, lived Pagirnis.

His name is a compound of *pa-* (under, beneath) and *girnos* (quern stones), a straightforward toponym that locates him precisely in the household. Jesuit missionaries in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, working through the Lithuanian countryside and reporting their encounters with folk religion back to Rome, noted Pagirnis among the household spirits that rural people refused to abandon despite Christian instruction. Jan Łasicki also recorded him as a deity invoked *pro mola* — for the mill.

Farmers offered the first handful of flour from each grinding session to Pagirnis, pressing it into a small crack in the base of the lower stone or scattering it near the quern's foundation. To fail to do so was to risk the spirit's displeasure — which manifested as poor-quality flour, stones that jammed and cracked, or flour with a bitter taste that meant Pagirnis had withheld his blessing.

Pagirnis represents the principle that domestic labour is sacred labour. The grinding of grain was not merely a physical task but a collaborative ritual between the human household and its divine guardian, a daily covenant renewed with each handful of grain offered between the stones. He is one of the clearest examples of how deeply Baltic folk religion was woven into the unceasing rhythms of ordinary life.
