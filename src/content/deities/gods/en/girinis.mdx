## Girinis / Miškinis — Lord of the Forest, Master of the Wild Woods

Girinis derives from *giria*, the dense primeval forest of Baltic Lithuania. Miškinis derives from *miškas*, the more generic word for any woodland. The distinction in names reflects a distinction in character: Girinis tends toward the ancient, deep, trackless forest — the primeval wilderness — while Miškinis is more associated with ordinary woods, the forest encountered in daily life on the edge of the village. Together they constitute the divine masculine principle of the Baltic forest.

In folklore, both appear as very old men, their hair and skin textured with moss and lichen, their clothes indistinguishable from the dead light-gray of birch bark. They move silently. They are rarely encountered directly; their presence is felt as a shift in the quality of the forest — a sudden directional confusion, a path that loops back on itself, the sense of being watched from behind the trees.

Hunters knew them as the lords who dispensed or withheld hunting luck. To enter the forest without acknowledgment — without a small offering on a forest-path stone, without a murmured greeting to the master — was to invite the forest's resistance. The deer would flee before the arrow; the path home would vanish; the hunter would walk for hours only to find himself back where he started.

The Latvian counterpart, the Meža vīrs (Forest Man), fills the same role in Latvian folk tradition, confirming that this male forest-lord was a pan-Baltic concept. He stands in contrast to Medeina and Meža māte — the forest goddesses — as the masculine, protective, and occasionally threatening aspect of the wood, the deep intelligence of the forest that both sustains and resists human intrusion.
