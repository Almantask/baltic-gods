## Spindulys — The Sun's Reaching Hand

In Baltic folk belief, a ray of sunlight was not merely a physical phenomenon — it was an act of divine attention. When a shaft of light entered a dark barn and fell on a sick animal, or crossed a threshold and touched a sleeping infant's face, or broke through cloudcover to illuminate a particular field, this was understood as Saulė reaching down to bless that specific place. The Spindulys — the ray, the beam, the thread of light — was her hand extended into the world.

Gintaras Beresnevičius describes this as evidence of a highly granular animism: a theology in which the solar goddess was not a distant abstraction but a constantly active presence, able to touch any point on earth through her rays. Latvian dainas speak of Saule's golden threads, her silver needles, the paths of light across the water — each a personified emanation rather than a figure of speech. The rays wake up seeds in frozen ground, carry healing into wounds, and mark the points where heaven and earth are in communication.

Pranė Dundulienė documented the solar ray's visual legacy in Lithuanian folk art: the radiating wheel motif, the eight-pointed star (sold to the sun), the swastika spinning on woven textiles, embroidered linens, and carved wooden implements. These are not decorative choices but theological statements — each ray from a solar center is a Spindulys, an act of divine attention frozen in thread or wood. The ornamental tradition preserved what the official religious vocabulary could no longer say after Christianization, keeping the theology of the reaching sun alive in the material world for centuries.
