## Sietynas — The Pleiades, Celestial Sieve of the Seasons

Of all the stars in the Lithuanian sky, none were watched more carefully than Sietynas, the Pleiades — the tight cluster of seven stars whose rising and setting governed the agricultural year with the precision of a divine calendar. The name comes from *sietas*, the Lithuanian word for a sieve, because the cluster's shape reminded ancient farmers of the perforated wooden bowl used to sift flour or fine grain: the heavens themselves were imagined as filtering blessings down onto the land below.

Jan Łasicki's sixteenth-century record notes celestial bodies venerated for their role in seasonal timing, and folk astronomers across Lithuania and Latvia tracked Sietynas with systematic care. When the cluster appeared above the eastern horizon in the evening, it signalled the time to begin ploughing the stubble fields; when it vanished before dawn, rye sowing season was open. Missing this window meant risking your grain against early frost, so Sietynas was not an abstract mythological figure but a practical survival tool cloaked in divine authority.

In mythology, Sietynas was sometimes said to be the cosmic sieve through which Perkūnas filtered rain — the perforations of the heavenly bowl turned destructive downpours into gentle nurturing showers that fed the grain without drowning it. Other traditions told how the seven stars were seven brothers or seven maidens transformed into the sky to escape danger, their constellation preserved as a reminder of divine mercy.

Latvian folk songs, the *dainas*, celebrated Sietiņš as the "heavenly gardener" who watched over sowing from above. Both traditions share the same core idea: the Pleiades are not merely beautiful lights but active participants in the drama of human agriculture, their position in the sky a direct message from the divine world to the farmers who depended on reading it correctly.
