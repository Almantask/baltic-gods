## The Spring Opener

Ūsiņš governs the moment winter breaks: the first warm morning when gates can be opened and horses led to spring pasture. Dionysius Fabricius, writing in 1610, recorded that Latvians sacrificed cocks to Ūsiņš at plowing time. By the eighteenth century, Ūsiņdiena (Ūsiņš Day, April 23) was the most important spring festival in much of Latvia — horses were decorated with oak wreaths, candles were lit before dawn, and the first pasture was sung open.

## Horses and Light

Ūsiņš rides white horses. He is also associated with light — the growing light of late April that warms the fields after winter. Barons's dainas contain hundreds of verses to Ūsiņš, asking him to bless a specific horse by name, to keep wolves from the pasture, and to bring abundant clover. He is addressed as a noble guest who visits each farm in turn on his feast day.

## Bees and the Spring Calendar

Beyond horses, Ūsiņš also governed bees. The spring opening of hives was his second sacred duty, and beekeeping families sang to him when uncovering the hives for the first time after winter. Scholars note a possible connection to the Dioscuri — twin horse-gods of Indo-European tradition — particularly through the parallel to Lithuanian Ašvieniai.

## The Ūsiņš Candles

A distinctive ritual of Ūsiņdiena involved lighting a cluster of candles before dawn — one candle per horse in the household, plus one for the farm itself. The candles were lit in the stable, then carried to the threshold, then placed at the field's edge. As light rises with Ūsiņš, so the farm's life rises into the growing season. This careful movement of light from enclosed to open space mirrors Ūsiņš's mythic role as door-opener between winter and summer.
