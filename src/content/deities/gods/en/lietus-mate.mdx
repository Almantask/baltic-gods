## Lietus māte — When the Rain Does Not Come

The agricultural calendar of Latvia runs on rain. Not uniform rain, not any rain — specific rain at specific moments. Too much in harvest season destroys grain. Too little in June means the hay is thin and the cattle will starve in January. The rain that comes exactly when the young wheat needs it is not luck; it requires solicitation. Lietus māte is the one who must be asked.

The dainas contain rain-solicitation songs sung in procession: women walking to a spring or a river, singing to Lietus māte, sometimes pouring water themselves in demonstration, mimicking what was needed. The etymology is explicit — *lietus* is simply the Latvian word for rain, and this Mother is its sovereign. She is sometimes depicted as weeping — her tears are the rain — or as pouring water from a great vessel held in the clouds. Either way, the logic is the same: rain is not automatic. It is her decision. And she responds to petition.

The relationship between Lietus māte and Pērkons is structurally important: Pērkons brings the thunder, the lightning, the violent summer storm. But the gentle persistent agricultural rain — the fine drizzle that soaks the soil for three days without flooding — is Lietus māte's alone. She is not a storm deity; she is a nourishment deity. Her concern is the grain, not the drama. In years of drought, when Pērkons gave lightning without rain, Lietus māte had withheld her gift. The question was always: what had been neglected, and how could it be repaired?
