## Milda — Goddess of Love (Disputed)

Milda is the figure most often cited when Baltic mythology is asked for its goddess of love — a role that sits elegantly beside the Norse Freyja or the Roman Venus. Her name is graceful, her domain is appealing, and her story is widely repeated. There is, however, a scholarly problem: Milda almost certainly never existed as a pre-Christian Baltic deity.

The source of the difficulty is Teodor Narbutt, a nineteenth-century Polish-Lithuanian Romantic historian who published his *Dzieje starożytne narodu litewskiego* (Ancient History of the Lithuanian Nation) in 1835. Narbutt was a passionate patriot who believed that Lithuania deserved a mythology as rich and systematized as the Greek or Norse traditions, and he supplied one — partly by elaborating authentic sources and partly, it seems, by invention. Milda appears in his work as a goddess of love and freedom, complete with temples, priestesses, and a white dove as her sacred animal.

No earlier source — not Łasicki, not the Jesuit chronicles, not any diplomatic or ecclesiastical record — names Milda. The Lithuanian academic authority VLE (Visuotinė lietuvių enciklopedija) and leading scholars including Norbertas Vėlius and Rimantas Balsys all conclude that Milda belongs to Romantic literary mythology rather than authentic pre-Christian religion.

She remains, however, a beloved cultural figure: the name Milda became popular in Lithuania precisely because of Narbutt's work, and it is still given to daughters today. Whether historical or invented, she has achieved a kind of mythological life through the affection of the people who have carried her name forward through two centuries of national consciousness.
