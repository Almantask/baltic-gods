## Lazdona — Goddess of Hazel Trees

The hazel held a special place in Baltic sacred botany that sets it apart from even the celebrated oak. While the oak was the domain of Perkūnas, struck by his lightning as a punishment for the serpent-souls lurking in its roots, the hazel was the one tree the thunder god specifically avoided. Lightning does not strike the hazel — this is an old folk-belief found across northern Europe — and in Lithuanian tradition this immunity was attributed to the protection of Lazdona, the goddess who made the hazel grove her home and kept it safe.

Jan Łasicki recorded *Lasdona* among the Samogitian deities, and the identification is clear: *lazdynas* is the Lithuanian word for the hazel tree, and the goddess's name is simply the tree made divine. Her gift was the hazelnut, one of the most nutritious and storable foods in the pre-agricultural Baltic forest, a nut that could be gathered in autumn and kept through the winter months, providing fat and protein during the season when the land gave little else.

Beyond nourishment, the hazel was a tool of divination: forked hazel branches were used to find underground water and buried objects, and hazel rods were cut for use in ritual and protective hedging. To sleep under a hazel on Midsummer Eve was to receive prophetic dreams; to carry a hazel staff was to walk with Lazdona's protection. Snakes, which in Baltic folk belief could be both sacred and dangerous, were said to avoid hazel groves — another protective feature attributed to the goddess.

Latvian folk songs honour *Lazdu māte*, the Hazel Mother, with garlands of praise that match the Lithuanian tradition: she is the generous forest spirit who fills her groves with rich nuts, asks only for reverence in return, and wraps her branches around those who walk beneath her canopy with quiet, constant protection.
