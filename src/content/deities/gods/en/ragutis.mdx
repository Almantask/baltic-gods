## God of the Sacred Cup

Ragutis and his consort Ragutienė preside over one of the most communally important acts in Baltic life: the brewing and shared drinking of ale and mead. Recorded by Jan Łasicki among the Samogitian gods, Ragutis was invoked at every major communal feast — harvest celebrations, wedding banquets, and the great autumn ale-making that saw entire villages come together after the fields were cleared. No barrel could be tapped without a libation poured for him.

## Fermentation as Sacred Act

To brew was to perform a sacred act in Lithuanian traditional culture. The transformation of grain and water into ale mirrored the broader Baltic understanding of natural processes as spiritually charged: just as seeds became grain through Vaižgantas's blessing, grain became ale through Ragutis's blessing. Brewing vessels were treated with ceremonial reverence, and the first foam skimmed from a fermenting batch was offered to the fire as Ragutis's share.

## Community and the Ale-Feast

Ragutis and Ragutienė were not solitary deities of private pleasure — they were gods of community. The communal feast (*puota*) that they presided over was the occasion when debts were settled, marriages announced, disputes resolved, and the dead were remembered with a shared cup. Songs were sung in their honor at these gatherings, some preserved in the Lithuanian sutartinių polyphonic tradition as refrains invoking festive spirit.
