## Curcho — Spirit of the Harvest

Curcho is the Old Prussian personification of the harvest’s vital force, a deity or sacred power to whom the first-fruits of every sowing were ritually offered. The Sudovian Book records his cult among the Old Prussian communities of the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, describing ceremony in which grain, beer, and bread fashioned from the new harvest were presented before the community could consume the remainder.

The ritual structure around Curcho closely parallels the Lithuanian figure of Jumis — the sacred double-ear of grain that embodies agricultural abundance. In both traditions the first abundance is understood as a divine loan rather than a human achievement: the spirit of fertility lends his power to the grain through the growing season, and the first-fruits offering is the repayment of that loan, ensuring that the same generosity will be available the following year.

Christoph Hartknoch’s seventeenth-century account mentions a ritual effigy or sheaf carried through the fields at harvest’s end, a common Old European practice that Curcho’s cult shared with harvest traditions across the Baltic and beyond. The effigy was the last sheaf cut, believed to contain the spirit of the grain; it was treated with ceremony, brought into the granary with songs and processions, and preserved until the spring sowing when its seeds would carry the divine vitality forward into the new season.

In the religious landscape of Old Prussia, Curcho represents the agricultural dimension of a worldview in which every important process was governed by a divine presence. The harvest was not simply a result of human labor and favorable weather but a gift requiring acknowledgment, repaid through ritual reciprocity that maintained the fertility of the earth across the generations.
