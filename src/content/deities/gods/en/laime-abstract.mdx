## Laimė (Abstract) — The Fortune That Must Be Earned

In Baltic mythology, a distinction exists between the goddess Laima — who determines fate at the moment of birth — and the personified principle of *Laimė* (Fortune), which represents the dynamic luck that accompanies an individual through daily life. Algirdas Julius Greimas, in his semiotic analysis of Lithuanian folk narratives, identified this distinction as fundamental: Laima fixes the arc of a life; Laimė is the quality of prosperity within that arc.

Laimė (abstract) appears in folk tales as a golden bird, a bright maiden, or an invisible presence that inhabits the corner of a diligent household. She rewards those who rise early, work honestly, and maintain a clean and generous home. If the household falls into laziness, quarreling, or meanness, Laimė departs — sometimes stolen by a greedy neighbor, sometimes simply gone. Her return requires ritual acts of renewal: cleaning, baking, singing, and welcoming.

The dynamic quality of *Laimė* made her a subject of intense folk attention. Rural communities monitored signs of her presence or absence: an unusually good harvest, a neighbor's unexpected prosperity, or a run of misfortune were all read as evidence of Laimė's movements between households. She represents the belief that good fortune is not purely written in fate but is also a result of individual virtue and communal harmony.
