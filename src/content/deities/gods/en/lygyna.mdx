## Lygyna — Goddess of Field Purity

Lygyna is the Lithuanian goddess of agricultural order, specifically the guardian against weeds intruding in the grain fields. Łasicki records her as *Lyginitem*, the deity invoked "for the cockle" (*pro lolio*) — darnel, cockle, and other weeds that compete with grain and, if harvested with it, can contaminate the flour and make it toxic. In the Baltic worldview, the presence of weeds was not merely a farming inconvenience but a sign of disrupted divine order requiring spiritual intervention.

Her name connects to *lyginti* — to level, smooth, make equal. The implication is an act of restoration: weeds are an unevenness in the field’s spiritual surface, and Lygyna smooths the field back to its proper, undivided state. She was invoked at planting, asking that what the farmer sowed be what the farmer harvested, without chaotic admixture.

The practical dimension of Lygyna’s cult reflects how seriously Baltic farmers took the purity of their grain. Mixed grain meant mixed flour; mixed flour in the wrong proportions could cause illness. The small religious act of invoking Lygyna was farmers’ acknowledgment that they could not control every biological process in a field, that something beyond their labor was needed to keep the harvest clean.

Lygyna is one of numerous minor Lithuanian deities recorded by Łasicki who governed specific agricultural problems. Together they form a theology of specialized divine attention: the Baltic understanding that every type of harm had its corresponding supernatural guardian, and that correct relationship with these powers was the foundation of a stable harvest.
