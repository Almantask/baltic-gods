# Vaivora — The Rainbow Maiden

After the storm, after the thunder of **Perkūnas** had rolled across the sky and the last lightning had struck, the world was washed and expectant. And then came **Vaivora** — the Rainbow — arching across the clearing sky in all her seven-colored splendor. To the Baltic peoples, she was not merely an optical phenomenon. She was *Łaimos juosta* — Laima's Belt — the celestial band worn by the goddess of fate, let down from the heavens as a signal that order had been restored and that the gods were still watching over the earth below.

The personification of the rainbow as a divine figure or attribute appears across many world mythologies, but in the Baltic tradition it carries a specific weight of theological meaning. Vaivora follows Perkūnas's storm as a herald of completion — the battle between the thunder god and his chthonic adversary has been fought, the serpent has been driven back into the earth, and now the rainbow appears as the victory banner. Her colors, stretching from one end of the sky to the other, trace the full arc of the divine hierarchy: the red of Perkūnas's fire, the blue of the sky god's domain, the green of Zemyna's earth.

In folk mythology, Vaivora acquired a secondary role as a "water-drinker" (*straublys*): a being who bends down to the rivers and lakes and drinks their water up into the clouds, replenishing the sky's supply of rain for the next cycle of growth. This image — the rainbow as a vast straw connecting heaven and earth through the medium of water — captures the Baltic sense of the cosmos as a circulatory system, where what falls as rain is lifted back by Vaivora and what shines as sun is extinguished and re-forged by Kalvelis.

Jan Łasicki mentioned Vaivora among the celestial figures whose names were invoked in connection with weather and fate. The Latvian equivalent, *Varavīksne*, appears in dozens of folk songs (*dainas*) as a sign of divine presence after turbulence — the most beautiful signature of the gods written in light across the sky.
