## Drebkulys — Spirit of the Shivering Fever

In the archaic Baltic religious imagination, illness was not an impersonal misfortune but a divine visitation: every fever, every cough, every shivering fit arrived because some spirit willed it, and the only cure was to negotiate with that spirit or find someone who knew how. Drebkulys — the Lord of the Shivering — was one of these illness deities, the personification of the fever that made a person tremble uncontrollably and sweat through the long hours of the night.

His name is immediately transparent to any Lithuanian speaker: *drebėti* means to tremble, to shake, to shiver, and *-kulys* is a suffix indicating an agent or force. The name does not merely describe the spirit; it enacts him — to say the name is to evoke the sensation. Jan Łasicki, cataloguing Samogitian deities circa 1582, noted *Drebkulum pro febre* — Drebkulys, for the fever — in his list, one of several illness spirits whose worship he documented.

The existence of Drebkulys reflects a theology of granular divine responsibility: rather than attributing illness to sin, bad luck, or miasma, Baltic folk religion assigned each disease its own presiding spirit, which meant that healing was a matter of the correct ritual rather than moral reformation. The *žyniai* (wise ones) and *burtininkai* (charm-casters) who served as village healers understood which spirit caused which affliction and what offerings or charms could persuade it to depart.

Drebkulys was typically propitiated with crossroads offerings — food and drink left at the boundary between the village road and the forest path, the place where spirits were understood to travel. The sufferer would sometimes carry a small bundle of herbs tied with red thread to the crossroads at dusk, leave it there with a whispered appeal, and depart without looking back, trusting that the spirit would accept the offering and release its grip.
