# Perkūnas Tikras — The True Thunderer

Not every crash of thunder is equal. The Lithuanians and Prussians understood this with a theological precision that modern meteorology has entirely abandoned. There were small thunders, local thunders, the rumbles of **Perkūnas's** sons attending to peripheral matters at the edges of the sky. But there was also *Perkūnas Tikras* — the **True Perkūnas** — and when he moved, the earth itself responded.

Jan Malecki recorded this epithet in his 16th-century *Libellus*, distinguishing the supreme Thunder God from lesser atmospheric phenomena. The title *tikras* (true, authentic, real) in Lithuanian carries a weight that exceeds mere linguistic qualification. To call something *tikras* is to invoke its deepest, most essential nature — its origin rather than its surface manifestation. Perkūnas Tikras was not the thunder you heard on a summer afternoon; he was the primordial storm that had existed before the world was shaped and would exist when the gods finally sealed the order of things at the end of the age.

Vėlius analyzed this epithet as evidence for a developed theological hierarchy within Baltic paganism — a recognition that divine figures could be stratified, that there was a 'lower' folk version of Perkūnas in the form of the accessible thunder grandfather (*Perkūnas senelis*) who helped with small matters, and a 'higher' version whose sovereignty was total and whose justice was absolute. This distinction — between the accessible face of the divine and its ultimate depth — represents a sophisticated theological intuition comparable to the distinctions made in many high-level religious traditions.

The first thunder of spring was Perkūnas Tikras at work: the sound that woke the frozen earth, announced the defeat of winter's underworld forces, and cracked the ice on the rivers. In that sound, the Lithuanians heard not just weather but the voice of the True One — the reassurance that the cosmic order was holding, that justice still governed the sky, and that another year of life would be permitted to unfold.
