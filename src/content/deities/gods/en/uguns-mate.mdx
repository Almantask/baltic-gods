## Uguns māte — Mother of Fire and the Hearth

Uguns māte, the Mother of Fire, is the Latvian guardian of the sacred hearth flame. In the Latvian worldview the hearth fire was not merely a practical tool but a living presence: a gift passed down from God and maintained by the family across generations. The fire’s health was a mirror of the household’s spiritual health, and Uguns māte was the divine custodian of that living link.

Each evening the fire was banked with care, never extinguished carelessly. A small offering of salt grains cast into the flames was a common act of acknowledgment — a feeding of the sacred presence. If the fire sputtered or refused to light, it was a sign that Uguns māte was displeased, often requiring an apology or a small ritual act of renewal. In Latvia as in Lithuania, the hearth fire was never to be urinated upon, stepped over, or treated with disrespect.

Uguns māte is functionally parallel to the Lithuanian Gabija, the sacred flame goddess. Both represent the same ancient Indo-European concept of the household fire as a living divine presence descended from the cosmic fire of heaven. The Latvian dainas (folk songs collected by Krīšjānis Barons) contain numerous references to the fire’s personality, moods, and requirements, confirming the goddess’s deep roots in Latvian spiritual life.

At weddings and major transitions, Uguns māte was invoked to bless the new household. A flame carried from the parental hearth to the new home was a common custom, symbolically transferring the family’s protective divine fire. In this way the goddess linked generations and maintained the continuity of the sacred family line.
