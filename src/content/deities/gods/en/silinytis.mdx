## Šilinytis — Guardian of the Pine Forest

Šilinytis is the Lithuanian spirit of the pine forest, the *šilas* — a sandy-soiled, open woodland dominated by Scots pine. Recorded by Łasicki as *Silinicius*, he presides over the dry, resinous groves that cover much of the Lithuanian and Prussian coastal lowlands. He governs all life within these heaths: the lichen and heather underfoot, the blueberry and crowberry, the resin weeping from bark wounds, and the amber light that filters through pine needles.

Workers who harvested forest products — resin-tappers, berry-pickers, firewood gatherers — recognized Šilinytis as the owner of the grove’s gifts. Before taking resin or cutting branches, a brief acknowledgment was made: a few words spoken aloud, a small portion of bread left at the base of the oldest tree in the grove. Taking without acknowledgment brought illness, lost tools, or paths that seemed to lead endlessly in circles.

Šilinytis stands in contrast to Mikintas and Giraitis, who govern the denser mixed forest and the sacred grove respectively. The *šilas* was a more approachable, sunlit space — the edge-world between the inviolable deep forest and human settlement. His temperament was therefore considered more yielding than that of deeper forest spirits, though still demanding of respect.

The Latvian equivalent, Sila māte, fulfills the same protective role in the Latvian tradition, confirming the pan-Baltic importance of the pine grove as a distinct sacred landscape. Both figures remind us that every microhabitat in the Baltic world had its own keeper, and that the natural world was governed by a layered system of divine guardianship.
