## Jievaras — Spirit of the Last Sheaf

At the end of every harvest in old Lithuania, when the reapers had worked their way across the field from boundary to boundary, a small patch of grain was deliberately left standing in the corner. This uncut rectangle of stalks — usually rye, the staple grain of the Baltic world — was not an oversight or laziness but a sacred act: it was Jievaras, the portion of the field that belonged to the spirit of the harvest itself, and it could not be cut without ceremony.

The ritual that followed was called the Jievaro tiltas, the Jievaras Bridge. The last stalks were carefully braided together by the eldest woman in the harvesting party, woven into an intricate plait that sometimes ran the full width of the standing patch. Into the braid were tucked offerings: bread, salt, flowers, and small bundles of herbs. The finished structure lay across the stubble like a woven bridge connecting the harvested earth to the sky — and in Lithuanian folk songs that are among the most archaic surviving examples of Baltic lyric tradition, Jievaras is addressed as exactly this: a golden bridge, a mountain of grain, a cosmic connector between the mortal and eternal.

The theology of the ritual is precise: by leaving the last stalks standing and braiding their life-force back into the earth rather than taking it away in the sheaf, the farmer returned the *jievar* energy — the generative vitality of the field — to the soil where it could overwinter and rise again with the spring sowing. The harvest was not a taking but an exchange, and Jievaras was the spirit who held the farmers to their side of the bargain.

Modern ethnographers recorded living versions of the Jievaras ritual in Lithuanian villages well into the twentieth century, sung with polyphonic *sutartinės* whose melodic patterns suggest origins far older than any written record.
