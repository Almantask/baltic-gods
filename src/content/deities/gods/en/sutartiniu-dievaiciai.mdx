## Sutartinių Dievaičiai — The Little Gods Who Live in the Sound

In the north-eastern highlands of Lithuania — Aukštaitija, land of polyphonic song — the ancient *sutartinės* were not merely music. They were ritual infrastructure: two or three voices singing in deliberate dissonance, each voice carrying a repeating refrain that, when interlocked, created something greater than any single part. And woven through the refrains were words: *Lado, Tatato, Laduola, Čiūto, Rūta-rūta*. These were the sutartinių dievaičiai, the Little Gods of the Refrains — called into the room each time the singers opened their mouths.

Algirdas Julius Greimas, in his structural study of Baltic mythology, argued that these refrain words were not merely rhythmic filler. *Lado* connects to a root meaning harmony, order, fitting together — possibly the name of a goddess of concordance, possibly an invocation of divine alignment itself. *Tatato* echoes with the percussive energy of Perkūnas's domain, the rhythm of thunder reduced to a sung pattern. Zenonas Slaviūnas's monumental collection of sutartinės — three volumes cataloguing thousands of songs — showed that these refrains appeared in specific seasonal and ceremonial contexts: haymaking, flax retting, wedding preparations, the turning of the year. The refrains were not decoration; they were the operative element.

What made sutartinės unique among Baltic musical forms was their dissonance: voices that should clash according to Western harmonic theory, but that created, in lived performance, a trance-like resonance that performers described as 'the sound that is between.' The little gods did not live in any single voice; they lived in the space where voices collided and merged. To sing a sutartinė was to temporarily open that space — to stand in the between place where the divine could enter. The gods of the refrains were, in this sense, less entities to be addressed than phenomena to be generated: you did not summon them by name, you summoned them by creating the conditions in which they could arise.
