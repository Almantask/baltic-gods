## Bubilas — Bee-Master and Honey God

In the bee-keeping culture of ancient Lithuania and Samogitia, where honey was currency, medicine, and sacred offering, Bubilas reigned as the divine master of the hive. His name derives from the Lithuanian verb *bubėti* — to hum or to buzz — capturing in a single word the living sound of a flourishing colony going about its sacred work. Where Austėja, the bee goddess, was the queen spirit who oversaw the sweetness of honey, Bubilas was the drone-master, the masculine principle of strength and abundance that made the hive prosper.

Jan Łasicki noted a deity invoked *pro apibus* — "for the bees" — among the Samogitian gods of the sixteenth century, a laconic entry that scholarly tradition associates with Bubilas. The brevity of the record belies his importance: bee-keeping was one of the most prestigious and spiritually significant occupations in old Baltic society, and the god who protected it commanded serious devotion.

Before every honey harvest, Lithuanian bee-keepers would leave offerings of bread, salt, and the first spoonful of new mead at the base of the linden trees where hives were kept. They spoke aloud to Bubilas, requesting that he keep the colony strong through winter and help the bees find rich clover in the coming spring. The mead brewed from his bees' honey was itself a sacred drink used in ritual feasts and poured onto fire as an offering to the gods.

Bubilas and Austėja together represent the gendered complementarity at the heart of Baltic divine ecology: each creature, each crop, each sacred activity had both a feminine and a masculine divine patron, their powers working in concert just as the bees themselves work as a community.
