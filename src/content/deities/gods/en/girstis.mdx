## Girstis

Girstis is a minor Lithuanian forest deity recorded by Jan Lasicki in his 16th-century catalogue of Samogitian gods. His name derives from roots associated with the forest (*giria*) and its distinctive rustling sounds, reflecting the deep-rooted Baltic reverence for the living woodland as a sacred space.

In Baltic cosmology, every forest, glade, and thicket was believed to have a presiding divine guardian — a genius loci who ensured the vitality and integrity of his domain. Girstis was the overseer of the undergrowth: the tangled thickets, dense bracken, and shadowed depths where larger paths did not reach. He protected the margins of the sacred grove and ensured that the hidden life of the forest floor remained undisturbed.

Practitioners who entered the forest for timber, herbs, or hunt were expected to acknowledge the local spirits. Girstis received simple offerings — a broken branch placed at the edge of a thicket, a whispered word of respect — to ensure that the hidden paths through the undergrowth would not lead astray. To disregard him was to risk losing oneself in the tangled depths.

Although Girstis is one of the more obscure entries in Lasicki's catalogue, he represents the broader Baltic understanding that the forest is not empty. Every shadow, every creak of branch, every rustling leaf conceals a watching presence — a minor deity performing the quiet, essential work of keeping the woodland alive.
