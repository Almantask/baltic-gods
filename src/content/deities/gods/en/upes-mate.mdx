## Upes māte — Where the River Curves and Time Flows

The distinction between Ūdens māte (Mother of Water) and Upes māte (Mother of Rivers) might seem scholastic until you understand what the distinction is tracking. Ūdens māte governs still water and fresh water in its general form — springs, wells, the water in the bucket. Upes māte governs the *flowing* — the movement itself, the river as a process rather than a resource. She is the spirit of what rivers do, which is go somewhere, relentlessly, without stopping, carrying whatever is placed in them toward the sea.

This made Upes māte specifically relevant to life transitions. Latvian women brought woven ribbons and glass beads to riverbends — not springs, not lakes, but bends in rivers — because the riverbend is where flowing water changes direction, where what was moving in one direction is redirected to another. Life transitions work the same way. Marriage, departure, death — all of these are the riverbend. The offering was not for the water itself but for what the movement could carry: prayers, farewells, the transitions that needed divine witness to complete properly.

Šmits documented the specific gender association: women, who managed most of the household's transitional moments — childbirth, marriage preparations, death vigils — were Upes māte's primary adorants. She was the goddess who understood that nothing stays still, that time moves in one direction, that the soul eventually reaches the sea. Her river was not a resource to be used but a process to be respected — the same process that brings spring floods and carries away what winter leaves behind, the same process that takes the dead where they are going.
