## The Great Mother of Material Existence

In Latvian belief, the universe is divided between Dievs, who governs the celestial and moral order, and Māra, who governs everything material and earthly. Cattle, cloth, earth, the human body, birth, and death — all belong to Māra. When a person dies, their soul passes from Dievs's keeping back into Māra's embrace. She is the ground beneath every foot.

## Cattle and the Farm

Haralds Biezais, in his monumental study of Latvian goddesses (1955), documented Māra's sacred role in cattle-keeping. She is called Lopu māte — Mother of Livestock — in her bovine aspect, and farmers greeted her on Māras diena (March 25) by touching the first unfrozen earth. Sacred cows were her embodiment; a white cow was particularly holy. Milk left unguarded at the threshold was an offering placed for Māra's night passage.

## The Sacred Cross

Māra's symbol — a hooked or branching cross older than the Christian cross in Latvia — appears on textiles, carved gate posts, and amber pendants. This Māras krusts (Māra's Cross) likely pre-dates recorded history and connects to the sun-wheel symbolism of pre-Christian Baltic culture. It marks a boundary between Dievs's sky and Māra's earth.

## Death and Rebirth

Māra is also Veļu māte — Mother of the Dead — in her deepest aspect. The dead return to her dark womb in the earth, and the grain that grows from that same earth is her gift back to the living. This cycle of material dissolution and renewal is Māra's fundamental law. She does not judge; she receives and returns.
