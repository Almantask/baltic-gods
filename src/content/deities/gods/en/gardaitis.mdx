## Gardaitis

Gardaitis is the Lithuanian guardian deity of boats, sailors, and maritime voyages. His name is etymologically connected to *garda* (ship / boat), marking him as the divine protector of the vessel itself — its planks, its keel, its rigging, and the humans who entrust their lives to it.

Recorded by Jan Lasicki in his late 16th-century catalogue of Samogitian gods (*pro nautis* — for sailors), Gardaitis occupied the functional niche of the seafarer's patron deity. Along the Baltic littoral, the coast of Samogitia, and the territories of the Curonians, maritime trade and fishing were central to community survival. The sea was beautiful but merciless: sudden storms, rock-lined coasts, and unpredictable currents demanded not only skill but divine intercession.

Before setting out on a voyage, sailors would offer prayers and small gifts to Gardaitis. After a successful return, thanksgiving offerings — food, carved wooden tokens, or the first catch of fish — would be dedicated to him. He was distinguished from the great sea-storm deity Bangputys, who ruled over the wild, destructive face of the ocean. Gardaitis was more intimate: the protector of the specific boat under your feet, the guardian of the crew you sailed with.

His presence was felt in the wood of the hull that did not crack, the rope that held in the gale, and the navigator's instincts that found the harbor before the darkness fell. For the Baltic sailor, Gardaitis was the invisible hand that steadied the oar when all seemed lost at sea.
