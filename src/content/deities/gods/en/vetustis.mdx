## Vetušis — The Destructive Wind and the Limits of Vėjopatis

The Baltic theological relationship with wind was nuanced enough to require two separate deities. Vėjopatis — the Wind Lord — ruled the benevolent aspects of atmospheric movement: the wind that drove the clouds bringing needed rain, the wind that powdered the miller's wheel, the wind that carried the boat, the wind that dried the harvested grain. Vetušis was the other wind: the one that flattened the standing rye three days before harvest, that capsized boats, that drove sleet horizontally into travelers' faces, that brought the killing frost in early spring. Łasicki recorded him as *Vetuszim pro tempestate* — for the tempest.

The distinction matters theologically. In a system where Vėjopatis represents the legitimate, useful, morally coherent aspect of wind, Vetušis represents the wind as sheer destructive force that exceeds any useful purpose — the wind that is simply dangerous. He is not necessarily evil in the cosmic sense — Baltic cosmology does not organize around good versus evil in the way Christian cosmology does — but he is adversarial to human interests in the way that a storm is adversarial to human interests: not because of intention but because of nature. To invoke Vetušis was not to worship destruction but to acknowledge it, to demonstrate that you understood what you were dealing with.

The ritual logic would have been propitiatory rather than worshipful: you did not ask Vetušis for his favor; you asked him not to direct his attention toward your farm. A brief offering, a muttered acknowledgment of his power, an implicit negotiation — leave my roof intact and I will remember you. Vėlius's comparative material shows similar atmospheric demons across the Baltic world: the Latvian *Vējas pērts* (Wind Bathed), the destructive aspect of the same elemental force. Every farming culture that depended on weather had to reckon with the gap between weather-as-useful and weather-as-catastrophic. Vetušis named that gap and made it addressable.
