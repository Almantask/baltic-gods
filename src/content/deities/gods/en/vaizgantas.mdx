## God of the Growing Stalk

Vaižgantas is the Lithuanian deity of flax and hemp, recorded by Jan Łasicki as one of the Samogitian gods invoked in agricultural ceremonies. The two plants under his care — flax (*linas*) and hemp (*kanapė*) — were the foundations of Baltic textile culture. From these fibres women wove the shirts, blankets, nets, and ritual cloths that clothed the body, covered the dead, and adorned sacred spaces.

## Flax and the Sacred Loom

Flax cultivation in Baltic tradition was laden with ritual from first sowing to final weaving. Before seeds were cast into the earth, an offering was made to Vaižgantas — sometimes a small bundle of the previous year's fibre left in the field or hung from a tree at the field's edge. The first linen thread of the year was considered sacred and might be wept into by the weaver, infusing it with protective power. The finished fabric carried the blessing of both Vaižgantas and Austėja, goddess of bees, since beeswax was used to fortify weaving thread.

## Hemp's Protective Power

Hemp, Vaižgantas's second charge, was valued not only for rope and coarse cloth but for its protective properties. Hemp seed scattered on the threshold was believed to repel evil spirits, and hemp rope twisted into specific patterns was worn as an amulet. The harvest of both flax and hemp was celebrated with communal gatherings where young people worked together through the night to ret, dry, and comb the fibres — occasions overseen spiritually by Vaižgantas.
