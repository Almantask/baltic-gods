## Šlaitis — The Divine Resident of Each Individual Slope

In Łasicki's catalogue of Samogitian deities, one of the more evocative entries reads: *Szlaitym pro declivitate* — Šlaitis for the hillside. The Latin *declivitas* means declension, slope, the place where the ground angle changes. In a landscape that is not flat, every hillside is a distinct feature: it has its own drainage patterns, its own micro-climate, its own vegetation community, its own orientation to sun and wind. The Baltic animist tradition recognized each such feature as the address of a distinct spiritual presence, and Šlaitis was the deity whose domain was the slope itself.

Samogitia is not dramatically mountainous — its hills are gentle glacial moraines, rounded and forested — but its terrain is varied enough that hillsides were economically important: south-facing slopes dried earlier in spring and could be plowed first; north-facing slopes retained moisture and were used for haymaking; slopes along rivers provided access to water for livestock. A farmer who understood the spirit of each hillside in his territory was a farmer who understood why that hillside behaved as it did — why the rye failed on the eastern slope but succeeded on the western, why the cattle preferred the lower pasture in early summer. Šlaitis embodied the particular character of each particular slope.

This is the level of theological detail that distinguishes Baltic animism from more generalized nature religion: not 'the forest' as a single entity, not 'water' as a unified principle, but *this* hillside, *this* stream bend, *this* oak grove — each with its own name, its own qualities, its own approach. The minor deities of Łasicki's list — Šlaitis, Vetušis, Šaukstis — collectively represent a complete map of the Samogitian landscape rendered in divine terms. Every feature that mattered economically and practically had a corresponding spiritual address. To live in Samogitia was to live surrounded by named presences, each one requiring its proper attention.
