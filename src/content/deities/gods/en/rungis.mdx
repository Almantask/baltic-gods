## Rungis — Spirit of the Fertile Dung

Of all the divine presences catalogued by Jan Łasicki in his survey of Samogitian religion, Rungis occupies perhaps the most earthy position: he is the deity who lives in the manure heap. His Latin description, *qui in fimo habitat* — "who dwells in the dung" — leaves no room for romantic interpretation. Rungis is the divine guardian of the farmstead's most essential recycled resource.

To the modern reader this may seem an improbable subject for devotion, but the ancient Lithuanian farmer understood immediately why manure required its own presiding spirit. Without the careful management of animal waste — the composting of dung through the winter months, the timely spreading onto ploughed fields before sowing — the thin Baltic soils would exhaust themselves within a generation. Rungis ensured that the dung would truly be dung: that it would decompose properly, retain its fertilising warmth, and carry the vital force of renewed growth into the earth rather than dissipating uselessly.

Farmers prayed to Rungis before spreading the manure in spring, asking him to bless the composted heap with full potency. They left small offerings pressed into the midden — a handful of grain, a crust of bread — acknowledging that the god's domain, though fragrant in a particular way, was genuinely sacred. In the circular theology of Baltic agriculture, waste and abundance were two stages of the same divine process, and Rungis was the spirit who held the turning point.

The existence of Rungis tells us something important about Baltic religious sensibility: it recognised no hierarchy of dignity in nature. The dung heap was as holy as the oak grove, the spirit who watched over composting as worthy of reverence as the spirit of the river. Every process that sustained life had its divine guardian, without exception.
