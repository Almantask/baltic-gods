# Tavalas — God of Strength and Boundaries

**Tavalas** (recorded by Łasicki as *Tavalus*) is the deity who *fortitudini praeest* — who presides over fortitude. In a world where survival depended upon physical labor, communal defense, and the unwavering courage to face adversity, Tavalas was the divine reservoir from which warriors and farmers drew their strength. He was invoked before difficult farm work, before a long journey in harsh weather, and before battle, his name spoken quietly as a man tightened his grip on a plow handle or a sword.

The scholar Gintaras Beresnevičius proposed a compelling identification between Tavalas and **Teliavelis**, the divine smith of the Hypatian Chronicle — the artisan who forged the Sun. Both figures share a concern with concentrated, creative force: the smith must possess not just skill but enormous physical and spiritual power to work the celestial metals. Under this interpretation, Tavalas is the *strength* aspect of the divine smith — the raw muscular and spiritual energy that makes the act of creation possible at all.

Beyond the warrior and the craftsman, Tavalas also guarded boundaries. In Baltic society, the marking of a property's edge — the boundary furrow, the sacred stone, the notched post — was a legal and spiritual act. The strength to maintain one's boundaries, to resist encroachment, and to impose the just limits of ownership was understood as a divine gift. Tavalas was the force behind the boundary stone: it stood firmly not merely by weight but by divine will.

Offerings to Tavalas were typically physical in nature — a stone placed on a boundary marker, a handful of grain pressed into the earth at a crossroads, a moment of silent straining of the muscles in his honor. He was a deity of deeds, not words, and his worshippers honored him best through the discipline of their bodies and the integrity of their boundaries.
