## Pjūties Dievas — The Reaper Who Closes the Cycle

In the agricultural theology of the Balts, the harvest was not merely an economic event — it was a sacred unmaking. To cut the grain was to end the life of the plant; to gather it was to transform living matter into the substance of human survival. Pjūties dievas presided over this moment of transformation, the stern and silent overseer who walked the fields at noon or dusk to ensure that the reaping was done correctly, that the first sheaf was cut with proper words, and that the grain was gathered before the weather could take it.

Gintaras Beresnevičius, in his structural analysis of Baltic religion, identifies the harvest god as the functional complement to Pergrubrijus (the spring sprouting deity): where Pergrubrijus opens the cycle in joy, Pjūties dievas closes it in sobriety. The harvest is the place where agricultural and death mythology converge — the scythe that cuts grain is the same tool carried by death figures across many traditions, and the Balts did not miss this connection. Harvest songs addressed Pjūties dievas with respect but also with anxiety: he could be pleased and bring dry weather for the cutting, or he could be slighted and send rain at the wrong moment.

Pranė Dundulienė documented the ritual elements specific to the harvest's end: the binding of the last sheaf into a figure called Jovara or the Rye Bride, the leaving of a small patch of uncut grain for the field spirit, and the ceremonial bringing of the harvest crown into the farmhouse. These rituals acknowledged Pjūties dievas's claim over the completed cycle and invited his protection for the coming year's planting. The last sheaf was not merely left standing — it was the harvest god's portion, his acknowledgment that something of the field's sacred energy must remain for the cycle to turn again.
