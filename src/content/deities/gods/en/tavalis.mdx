## Tavalis — The Spirit Who Counts the Cattle

In Jan Łasicki's 16th-century record of Samogitian deities, one entry is specific enough to be zoological: *Tavalem pro bobus in stabulo* — for the cattle in the barn. Tavalis is the spirit of the livestock building itself, the invisible presence that watches over the animals through the night, monitoring their breathing, noticing when one is restless or feverish, ensuring that morning brings each one still standing. For a Lithuanian farming family of the 16th century, the barn was not an outbuilding — it was the treasury. Cattle represented wealth, milk, draft power, and the margin between sufficiency and starvation.

The logic of dedicating a deity to the barn space reflects the broader Baltic pattern: every space that mattered had a corresponding guardian. The *dimstipatis* watched the homestead. *Gabija* kept the hearth. Tavalis watched the stall. His invocation — likely a brief formula spoken before closing the barn doors for the night — asked him to stand watch while the family slept, to keep the wolves and the illness spirits from the animals' breath, to ensure each cow woke with full udder. The offering was probably minimal: a handful of grain, a sprinkle of holy water from the local well, a small flame set in a lantern by the door.

Tavalis occupies a tier in the Baltic pantheon that later Christian thinking would have trouble categorizing — not a great god, not a demon, not exactly an ancestor ghost. He was a professional spirit, defined entirely by his function, like a specialist in one discipline of a vast spiritual civil service. His worship would have been entirely practical: you invoke Tavalis if you have cattle, you stop invoking him if you do not. He required no theology, only attention. The same pragmatic relationship with minor protective spirits appears in contemporaneous records from Latvia, where specific spirits were assigned to specific animals, specific buildings, and specific craft activities — a distributed divine administration of domestic life in precise and detailed form.
