## Perkūno Kulka — The Thunderstone in the Earth

When the summer storms passed and the rain drained away, people sometimes found long, pointed objects in the freshly turned soil — belemnite fossils, the preserved internal structures of ancient cephalopods. To the scientific mind, these are paleontological specimens; to the Baltic villager, they were something else entirely: the physical residue of Perkūnas's battle against Velnias. The Thunder God had thrown these projectiles at his chthonic enemy during the storm, and those that missed had buried themselves in the earth, re-emerging after the flood.

Nijolė Laurinkienė and Jonas Balys both documented the extensive ritual life of these objects. Perkūno kulkos were kept in homes as protective amulets, placed under thresholds, stored in granaries to prevent fire from striking. They were placed in cradles to protect infants and hung near beehives to protect the bees. Water in which a thunderstone had been soaked was used medicinally, believed to cure various ailments ranging from skin conditions to cattle disease. The object was not merely lucky — it retained an active charge of Perkūnas's power, a fragment of the divine lightning translated into mineral form.

Archaeological evidence confirms that belemnites and polished stone axes were deposited in early Baltic graves and sacred sites, suggesting that this belief complex predates any written records by millennia. The Latvian Pērkona lode occupied the same role in northern Baltic culture. For communities that lived under the constant ecological pressure of fire, flood, and storm, possessing a piece of the Thunder God's weapon was not superstition but a rational engagement with the available theology of protection.
