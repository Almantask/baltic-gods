## The Apportioner of Life

Not everything that Laima decides is equal for everyone. The goddess who governs individual fortune's material dimension is Dalia — her name taken directly from the Lithuanian word *dalis*, meaning share or portion. At a person's birth Dalia arrives alongside Laima and Giltinė to preside over the allotment: this household will have seven cows and a good wheat field; this one will be poor but long-lived. Dalia distributes not cruelty but inevitability.

## Laima's Companion

Scholars of Baltic religion, particularly Algirdas Julius Greimas and Norbertas Vėlius, distinguish Laima as the weaver of life's overall pattern and Dalia as the accountant of its material substance. The two work together at the loom of fate: Laima holds the thread of days, Dalia measures out the weight of what each thread will carry. The distinction reflects an ancient Baltic preoccupation with social justice — a society where everyone has a fair portion, even if unequal, is a cosmos in order.

## Scissors and the Cloth of Fortune

Dalia's ritual symbols include woven cloth, measuring rods, and scissors. The act of cutting fabric at a certain length stands for the exact amount of fortune allotted — no more, no less. Women's weaving in Baltic culture was therefore a sacred act in which the material reality of household prosperity was created with textile skill mirroring the divine work of Dalia. Offerings of woven cloth at springs and sacred sites were addressed to her.

## The Portion Returns

Dalia's governance does not end at the grave. Folk belief holds that the portion she assigned must be fully used up before a person may die peacefully. Wasted abundance — grain left to rot, cattle sold carelessly, cloth cut short — offended Dalia and brought misfortune. The goddess demanded not wealth but stewardship: the responsible use of every scrap of the share she placed in your hands at birth.
