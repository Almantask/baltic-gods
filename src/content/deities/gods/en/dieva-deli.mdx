## Dieva deli — Divine Twins of the Baltic Sky

Dieva deli, the Sons of Dievs, are among the most archaic figures in Baltic mythology and one of the clearest points of convergence with wider Indo-European religion. They are the divine twins who ride across the celestial sea on gray or golden horses, equivalent to the Greek Dioscuri, the Vedic Ashvins, and the Lithuanian Asvieniai.

In Latvian folk songs (dainas) catalogued by Krisjanis Barons, the Dieva deli appear primarily in the context of the 'celestial wedding' — the mythological cycle in which they court the Saules meitas (Daughters of the Sun). They ride to the edge of the world, cross the sea at dawn or dusk, and meet the solar maidens weaving among the stars. This cosmic romance encodes the relationship between the sky and sun, the masculine and feminine principles of the upper world.

Beyond their romantic role, the Dieva deli have an intensely practical function. They are the guardians of horses and are invoked by farmers before the spring plowing season. Offerings were left in stables to ensure the health and strength of the household's draft animals. They are the divine embodiment of youthful energy: swift, helpful, and ever present at the turning points of the agricultural year.

The twinship motif is deeply significant. In many Indo-European traditions, divine twins represent the duality at the heart of existence — day and night, land and sea, strength and wisdom. The Dieva deli carry this archaic meaning into Baltic culture, making them one of the most richly documented figures in the dainas.
