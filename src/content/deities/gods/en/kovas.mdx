## Kovas

Kovas is the Lithuanian god of war and combat, whose very name means "struggle" and is also the Lithuanian word for the month of March — that liminal season when the ice breaks, the first rooks return, and the earth awakens for another cycle of conflict and cultivation. His identity is therefore inseparable from the rhythms of nature and the agricultural-military calendar.

Jan Lasicki recorded *Kovas* among the Samogitian deities in the late 16th century, noting him as the divine patron of the warrior's martial endeavors (*deus belli*). In the same catalogue, the obscure figure *Kauriraris* appears, associated specifically with the heat of battle and the warhorse — a companion spirit that inflames the passion and endurance of the fighter at the moment of greatest need.

The month of March was the traditional start of the campaigning season in the Baltic world. After the winter's frozen stillness, rivers became passable, roads firmed up, and armies began to move. Kovas presided over this awakening military energy: the sharpening of blades, the tending of warhorses, the gathering of warriors under their tribal banners.

For Baltic soldiers, invoking Kovas meant aligning oneself with an archetypal force — the primal drive to defend, to conquer, and to prevail. He is the god of the spear thrust and the disciplined shield wall, a divine witness to the sacrifices that the warrior makes in service of his people.
