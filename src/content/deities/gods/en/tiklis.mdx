## Tiklis — The Fortunate Outcome That Cannot Be Forced

In Łasicki's list of Samogitian deities, Tiklis appears with a Latin gloss that is unusually precise: *Tiklium pro fortunato successu* — Tiklius for a fortunate success. Not luck in general, not good fortune broadly, but the specific favorable outcome of a specific endeavor. Tiklis is invoked when the outcome is not yet determined — when the grain has been planted but not yet sprouted, when the messenger has been sent but not yet returned, when the net has been cast but not yet hauled up. He is the deity of the exact moment when possibility becomes actuality.

The name likely derives from the same root as *tikėjimas* (belief, faith, expectation) and *tikti* (to fit, to be right, to suit) — reflecting a Baltic concept that good outcomes are things that *fit* correctly into their context, not random windfalls. To invoke Tiklis was to express the belief that the right outcome was possible, that the gods recognized it, and that with proper attention and proper ritual the favorable possibility would be selected from among the others. This is not passive fate-acceptance but active participation in the formation of what happens.

Tiklis represents a category of deity common in polytheistic systems but often overlooked in later retellings focused on great narrative gods: the functional deity who addresses a specific human need without mythology, without drama, without personality. He has no stories because his role requires no story — only a brief invocation, a small offering, and the proper attitude. Vėlius noted that these minor functional deities of Łasicki's list are often more historically reliable evidence than the great narrative divinities, because they are too small and too specific to have been invented or embellished. Someone actually invoked Tiklis. The specificity of his domain — fortunate success — proves it.
