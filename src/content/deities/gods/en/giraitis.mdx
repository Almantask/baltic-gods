## Giraitis — Guardian of the Sacred Grove

Giraitis is the Lithuanian spirit of sacred groves, the *giraitė* — a small cluster of ancient trees set apart from the surrounding forest and considered inviolable. Such groves were the oldest form of Baltic sanctuary: no tree within them could be cut, no animal hunted, and no fire lit without the approval of the spirit who dwelt there.

Recorded in variant forms as Girstis and Girinaitis, Giraitis received offerings at the base of the grove’s most prominent tree — typically an oak or linden. Bread, woven wool, and small carved figurines were placed there at seasonal transitions. Communities brought their disputes to sacred groves, trusting that the spirit’s presence would compel honest speech and fair judgment.

Giraitis occupies a distinct position in the hierarchy of Lithuanian forest spirits. He is not the wild, threatening power of the deep forest, but the sanctified, civic spirit of a specific, bounded place. The grove he inhabits is already marked and known; his role is to maintain its inviolability against encroachment. Łasicki and later Praetorius both record offerings and rituals connected to such grove spirits across the Samogitian and Prussian borderlands.

The destruction of sacred groves during the Christianization of Lithuania was experienced as a profound spiritual rupture. Many communities attempted to preserve the tradition by associating their groves with Christian saints, but the underlying figure of Giraitis persisted in folklore: the righteous old man of the grove who appears to those who disturb his trees, demanding an account of their intentions.
