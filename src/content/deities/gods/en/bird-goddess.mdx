## Bird Goddess — Messenger Between Worlds

The Bird Goddess (*Paukščių deivė*) is one of the earliest identifiable religious figures in the Baltic region. Reconstructed by Marija Gimbutas from Neolithic clay figurines, bird-shaped ritual vessels, and bone ornaments, she predates the arrival of Indo-European tribes by millennia. Her essential function was mediation — between sky and earth, between the living and the dead, between the human community and the cycles of the natural world.

The bird was the primary vehicle of divine communication in ancient Baltic belief. The cuckoo's call was the voice of fate itself, counting the years remaining to a listener. The stork's arrival marked the beginning of the agricultural year. The swan's seasonal departure signaled the approach of winter. These were not merely observed phenomena: they were messages, carrying the speech of the Bird Goddess through the acoustic space of the landscape.

Gimbutas noted that the Bird Goddess survived the Indo-European transformation of Baltic religion by merging with later goddesses: Laima took the form of the cuckoo; the Saulės meitas (daughters of the Sun) assumed swan form; the morning star Aūsrinė was pictured as a bird preparing the sky for the Sun's daily journey. The original goddess dissolved into these inheritors while her core function — winged mediation between the divine and the human — persisted without interruption into modern folklore.
