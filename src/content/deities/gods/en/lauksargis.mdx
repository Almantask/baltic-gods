## Lauksargis — Guardian of the Fields

Before a plough blade broke the earth in spring, before a single seed was committed to the soil, the Lithuanian farmer would stop at the field's edge and speak to Lauksargis — the field guardian, the watchful divine presence who lived in the land itself and had to be acknowledged before the agricultural cycle could safely begin. His name is a compound of *laukas* (field, open land) and *sargas* (guard, warden), a straightforward title that left no ambiguity about his function.

Among the earliest documentary evidence for his cult is Martynas Mažvydas's 1547 Lithuanian catechism, the first book ever printed in the Lithuanian language. Mažvydas, writing to combat exactly the kind of folk religion that Lauksargis represented, listed field-guardian deities among the pagan practices he urged his readers to abandon — thereby preserving for posterity the evidence of how widespread and tenacious their worship was, even in the mid-sixteenth century.

Lauksargis was invoked at the beginning of plowing with offerings of bread and salt left at the four corners of the field. Farmers asked for three things: that the soil remain unharmed by flooding or drought, that the harvest be bountiful enough to feed the family through winter, and that no hostile spirit disturb the land's peace between sowing and reaping. His Latvian counterpart, Lauku māte (Mother of the Fields), performed the same function in the parallel folk religion of neighbouring Latvia.

At harvest time, the last sheaf of grain — the *rūpintojėlis* sheaf — was sometimes left standing in the corner of the field as a gift for Lauksargis, acknowledging that no matter how much the farmer took, the guardian of the land retained his portion.
