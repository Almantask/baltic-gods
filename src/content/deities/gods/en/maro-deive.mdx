## Maro Deivė — Goddess of the Sweeping Plague

Maro deivė distinguishes herself from Giltinė, the death goddess who takes individuals one by one, through her scale: she does not visit households but regions, does not select particular souls but sweeps entire villages into the underworld with the wave of her red cloth. In Baltic folk legends documented through the 19th century, she appears as a tall woman in white, riding or walking from the direction of unknown lands, waving her red cloth as she goes. Wherever the cloth passes, illness breaks out.

Gintaras Beresnevičius analyses Maro deivė as a distinct theological concept from generic death: the collective killing event, the plague that exceeds individual fate and forces communities to confront their fragility as a whole. When Giltinė comes, it is someone's appointed time; when Maro deivė comes, it is no one's appointed time — or everyone's simultaneously. The Old Prussian records document a figure called Diedewaythe (Grandmother-Goddess) who could both send and withhold the plague at community request, suggesting that Maro deivė was not purely destructive but could be propitiated.

The terror of Maro deivė shaped deep behavioral patterns in Baltic communities: the practice of burying the dead quickly at the plague-season boundary, the fires lit at village edges to drive her back, the prayers to Perkūnas whose thunderstorms could cleanse the air. Basanavičius collected dozens of legends in which individuals attempt to outsmart her — hiding in chimneys, impersonating her own kin, solving impossible riddles — but these stories largely end in failure, reinforcing the theological message: against the collective death, individual cleverness offers no reliable shield.
