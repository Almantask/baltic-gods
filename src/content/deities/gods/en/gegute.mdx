## Gegute

Gegute is the sacred cuckoo bird of Baltic mythology — the living oracle and avatar of Laima, the goddess of fate. Her name is the Lithuanian word for cuckoo, and her distinctive call, floating across the spring woodland, was one of the most charged and meaningful sounds in the Baltic sacred soundscape.

The cuckoo was believed to be no ordinary bird. According to numerous *sakmes* (legends) recorded by Jonas Basanavicius and later scholars, the cuckoo was once a grieving woman — a widow, a mother, or a sister consumed by sorrow — who was transformed into a bird by the gods so that she might continue to speak her grief and her wisdom across the seasons. This origin myth gives Gegute her dual nature: she is both a voice of prophecy and a vessel of mourning.

Her call carried specific divinatory power. To hear the cuckoo call for the first time in spring while your pockets were full of coin was an omen of prosperity for the year; to hear her call while empty-handed foretold financial hardship. The number of calls counted before she fell silent predicted the number of years remaining in one's life. Latvian *dainas* collected by Krishjanis Barons include hundreds of songs in which the *dzeguze* (cuckoo) sits in the birch tree and calls out Laima's judgments on the fates of the living.

Gegute is the bridge between the human world and the predetermined order of fate. She does not judge — she only reports. Her call in the spring forest is the voice of destiny itself, arriving on the wind to remind the living that their stories are already written in the sky.
