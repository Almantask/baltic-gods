## Snake Goddess — Guardian of Life's Perpetual Renewal

The Snake Goddess (*Gyvatės deivė*) is the primordial Old European deity of regeneration and the life-force, identified by Marija Gimbutas in the archaeological record of the Baltic and surrounding regions. Dating to the Neolithic and earlier, she embodied the principle that life does not end but transforms — a theology expressed in the snake's most visible behavior: the shedding and renewal of its skin.

In the Baltic context, the Snake Goddess's deepest legacy is the household grass-snake (*Žaltys*) cult. The non-venomous grass snake was believed to live under the threshold or behind the hearth of every healthy household, and killing one was a grave sin that would bring sickness and death to the family. The snake was fed milk and addressed respectfully. This practice, documented into the 20th century, represents the unbroken continuity of the Snake Goddess's protective function from Neolithic times through the Lithuanian and Latvian present.

Gimbutas traced the Snake Goddess's iconography — spirals, meanders, the coiled serpent motif, and the "eye-goddess" design — across thousands of artifacts from the Baltic to the Aegean. In Baltic folk art, these same motifs appear on weaving patterns, ceramics, amber carvings, and wooden objects, suggesting that the visual language of the Snake Goddess survived not as conscious theology but as an aesthetic inheritance embedded in everyday craft.
