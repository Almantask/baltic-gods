## Salna māte — The Frost That Tests What Is Worth Keeping

Salna māte appears twice in the agricultural year, and both times she is the same spirit but in a different emotional register. In early spring, she is the destroyer: she comes at night after the apple trees have bloomed, after the wheat has already emerged a centimeter from the ground, and she burns the blossoms, blackens the new growth, and sets the farmer back weeks. This Salna māte is dreaded. In late autumn, she is the finisher: the first hard frost that kills the last insects, hardens the soil, and locks in the crops already stored. This Salna māte is almost welcome.

Šmits documented the folk attitude toward her with precision: farmers did not pray to Salna māte for her absence. They understood that frost was necessary — that a winter without proper frost meant disease in the grain, weak ice on the rivers, unpredictable springs. What they prayed for was timing: that she would come after harvest, not before. That she would spare the orchard through May. That she would hold off until the cucumbers were in. Salna māte's power was not the problem; her timing was the variable.

In the Latvian māte complex, she represents the principle of testing — the cold wind that separates what is strong from what is not yet strong. A blossom that survives the late frost becomes a fruit worth harvesting. A grain that survives the May frost produces stock that can be planted the next year. Salna māte does not hate what she destroys; she simply represents the reality that the world does not protect vulnerable things simply because they are new. Her cruelty, from one angle, is the same as her usefulness: she reveals, with absolute honesty, what was ready and what was not.
