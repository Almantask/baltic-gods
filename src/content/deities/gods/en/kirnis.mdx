## Kirnis — Keeper of the Cherry Trees

Kirnis is the orchard deity who watches over cherry trees (vyšnios) and their sweet harvest. Recorded by Jan Lasicki as deus cerasorum, he is one of the most specific entries in the Lithuanian pantheon — a deity responsible not for a broad domain like the sea or the storm but for the particular grace of a single fruit-bearing tree.

In the Baltic agricultural world, fruit trees occupied a special ritual category distinct from grain crops. They required years of patient tending before they bore fruit, and a single late frost or pest outbreak could destroy an entire harvest. Kirnis was the divine guarantor of this patient investment. Offerings to him were made at the first blossom and again at the first ripe fruit: a gesture of acknowledgment that the orchard's abundance was not merely earned but given.

The cherry held symbolic significance beyond its fruit. Cherry wood was used in small ritual objects, and the flowering cherry was one of the first signs of spring. In some Baltic folk traditions, cherry blossom is associated with the transition of souls and the return of the ancestral dead in spring, connecting Kirnis to a wider complex of seasonal spirits.

Kirnis represents the democratic theology of Baltic nature religion: the principle that no living thing is too small or too specific to have its own guardian. Every grove, every orchard, every specialized niche in the natural world had a name and a deity watching over it.
