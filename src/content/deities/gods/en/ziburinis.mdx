## Žiburinis — The Wandering Light and the Sins of the Land

Žiburinis appears at the edge of marshes and in the middle of forest tracks on dark nights: a flickering flame, or sometimes the silhouette of a figure with fire burning in its chest, drifting without apparent direction across terrain no traveler should enter alone. Jonas Balys's extensive catalogue of Lithuanian folk legends is clear about what this figure represents: the ghost of a person who moved a boundary stone while alive, stealing a neighbor's land by shifting the markers that defined the legitimate boundaries of the fields. This sin — against both the neighboring farmer and against the ancestral order that the boundaries encoded — was considered too serious to be forgotten after death.

The Žiburinis cannot rest because it cannot undo its action. It is condemned to haunt the territory it violated, and its one action now is to mislead travelers the way it misled the system of property and justice when alive. Lead them into the bog. Confuse the path they know. Make them lose their bearings in a landscape that should be navigable. This is both punishment and continuation of the original crime — the Žiburinis is still moving things in the wrong direction.

The moral content of the Žiburinis tradition is specifically about land and boundaries — not about generalized wickedness but about the particular sacredness of demarcated territory that ancestors had established and passed down. Pranė Dundulienė documents the connection between boundary stones and the dead in Baltic ritual: the dead were buried at or near boundaries, and their spiritual authority was one of the reasons the boundaries held. To move a stone was to violate both the living and the dead. The Žiburinis is the form that violation takes in the landscape afterward.
