## Jods — The Dark One Beyond the Light of Dievs

In the Latvian dainas, common curses call on Jods to take the one who wronged you. Jods appears in these moments not as a comic devil to be outwitted — that is Velns's role, the trickster who loses to humans and is fooled by clever girls — but as something more absolute and unsmiling. When the worst names are needed, Jods supplies them. He is the darkness that precedes divine order, the chaos that Dievs must hold back, the sickness that cannot be explained as bad luck.

Haralds Biezais distinguishes Jods carefully from Velns in his structural analysis of Latvian mythology. Velns is the underworld lord who appears in agricultural myths, steals cattle, flirts with Laima, loses games, and generally loses. He is part of the cosmic order even as he opposes it. Jods is not part of the order at all. He personifies the absolute negative — the zone of existence where divine light does not reach, where the organizing principle of Dievs simply fails. His name is connected etymologically to the root for blackness, *juod-*, the same root that gives Lithuanian *juodas* (black).

Pēteris Šmits documented the ways Jods appears differently than Velns in folk belief: he doesn't make deals, doesn't lose, doesn't have a farm or a wife or a court. He brings disease, misfortune, and the terror of pure darkness. Protective rituals against him were not negotiating tools — they were barriers. Pērkons could defeat him, but only through violent opposition. In the structuring of Latvian mythological space, Jods represents the limit of what the divine system can contain: necessary to acknowledge, impossible to incorporate.
