## Ganiklis

Ganiklis is the Lithuanian deity of shepherds and the grazing of livestock. His name derives directly from *ganyti* (to pasture / to herd), and he was the divine patron of every herdsman who led animals into the open fields and forests from spring to autumn.

Recorded by Jan Lasicki with the note *pro pastoribus* — for the sake of shepherds — Ganiklis was part of the extensive Baltic pantheon of agricultural and pastoral deities who governed the daily tasks of the farming community. In a world before fencing was widespread, livestock routinely grazed in shared fields, forest margins, and river meadows. The shepherd's responsibility was immense: a single wolf attack, a moment of inattention, or a sudden storm could mean the loss of animals that represented a family's entire wealth.

Ganiklis was invoked at the beginning of the grazing season — typically after the first spring thaw, when livestock were brought out from the winter barns. Offerings of bread, salt, and sometimes beer were made at the field's edge, accompanied by words asking Ganiklis to watch over the animals and protect them from predators, illness, and getting lost in the forest undergrowth.

The shepherd's horn — the instrument used to call animals and signal danger — was the symbol of his craft and a tool of communication with the divine. Ganiklis embodies the patient, watchful spirit of the herding life: the long days in open air, the intimate bond between human and animal, and the quiet courage required to face whatever the pasture might bring.
