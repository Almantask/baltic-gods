## Indraja — Goddess of Jupiter

In the Lithuanian celestial pantheon, Indraja is the divine personification of Jupiter, the great wandering star that traces a slow and majestic arc through the night sky. A daughter of Saulė, the Sun goddess, she belongs to the family of planetary deities that the ancient Lithuanians imagined as luminous children accompanying their radiant mother on her daily journey across the heavens.

Jan Łasicki, the Polish chronicler who compiled his survey of Samogitian deities circa 1582, recorded the name *Indrai* among the gods worshipped by the Lithuanians of the north. Scholars interpret Indraja as a feminisation linked to the Sanskrit cosmic power *Indra*, suggesting ancient Proto-Indo-European connections that place Baltic astronomical religion within a broader Eurasian tradition of sky worship.

As a planetary deity, Indraja embodied the principle of celestial order: Jupiter's reliable orbit served as a calendar marker for farmers and a sign of divine constancy. Her bright light in the eastern sky before dawn was read as a promise that the cosmic family was intact and that the seasons would turn in their proper rhythm.

Offerings to Indraja were made before important journeys or at the start of agricultural seasons, when the favour of the sky was most needed. Priests and wise women would observe Jupiter's position to divine the coming year's fortune, treating Indraja's glow as a direct communication from the daughters of Saulė to their people below.
