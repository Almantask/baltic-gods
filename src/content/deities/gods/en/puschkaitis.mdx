## Puschkaitis — Earth Lord beneath the Elder Tree

Puschkaitis is the Old Prussian deity of the earth beneath trees, particularly the elder tree (*sambucus*), whose roots were believed to reach the realm he inhabited. He is recorded in the Sudovian Book as a god who commanded the Kaukai and Markopoles, the small household spirits who carried agricultural abundance from the fields into the granary. Puschkaitis thus stood at the head of a hierarchy of beneficial spirits whose work sustained Prussian farming communities.

Stone altars under elder trees received offerings of milk, beer, and porridge brought in small clay vessels. Communities approached these altars especially at harvest time, when Puschkaitis’s subordinate spirits were most active, carrying grain homeward. The elder tree was considered his most important emblem: its hollow stems connected the earthly surface to the underground realm, and the tree’s own tendency toward abundance — masses of white flowers followed by heavy berry clusters — made it a natural symbol of the deity’s gifts.

Matthäus Praetorius, writing in the late seventeenth century, provides the most detailed account of Puschkaitis’s cult. He describes community gatherings at grove altars, where head-of-households presented offerings on behalf of the entire family group. The rituals centered on gratitude for past abundance and petition for continued favor in the coming season.

Puschkaitis occupies an important position in Baltic comparative mythology as a deity who integrates the underground earth, the world tree (elder as axis mundi), and the network of helpful spirits who make the agricultural cycle function. His cult reflects the Prussian understanding that the bounty of the earth was not passive but actively governed — and actively given — by divine forces that required acknowledgment and reciprocity.
