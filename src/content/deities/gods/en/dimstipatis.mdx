## Guardian of the Farmstead

Dimstipatis — the Lord of the Homestead — is the Lithuanian protective deity of the farmstead and the yard surrounding the dwelling. Recorded by Łasicki among the Baltic gods of the Samogitians, he presides over the threshold, the gate, and the entire enclosed domestic space where a family lived its ordered life. Where Gabija guards the hearth fire within the house, Dimstipatis guards everything outside: the yard, the outbuildings, the animals in pens, and the invisible boundary that separates home from the dangers of the forest and the road.

## The Sacred Yard

The farmstead yard in Baltic tradition was not merely functional — it was a cosmological space. The gate was a liminal threshold between the ordered world of the family and the unpredictable world beyond, and Dimstipatis ruled that transition. New brides were traditionally carried over the threshold to his blessing, and families moving into a new home would greet Dimstipatis first by placing a small offering of salt and bread near the gate-post. Carved wooden pillars at farm gates throughout Lithuania and Latvia bore protective symbols — suns, snakes, and interlaced patterns — that were his physical markers.

## Domestic Protection

Dimstipatis was also invoked against invisible threats: wandering spirits, evil eyes, and the envy of neighbors. A horseshoe nailed above the stable door was one of his markers; bundles of herbs hung in the rafters another. If livestock fell sick or crops near the farmyard boundary showed signs of blight, an offering would be made at the gate-post to ask Dimstipatis to strengthen his watch.
