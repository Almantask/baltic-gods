## The Laughing Youth

At Romuva's sacred oak, the idol in the middle position belonged to Patrimpas — a beardless young man, cheerful of face, with a crown of ripe grain woven around his head. Where Perkūns crackled and Patollo brooded, Patrimpas laughed. He is the fertility half of the divine triad: spring planting, the laughter of rivers, the first green after a hard winter, and the joy of the young living into the world he promises will be good to them.

## Rivers and Fields

Patrimpas's domain encompasses all flowing water and germinating grain. Old Prussian farmers appealed to him before the planting season with dances and songs at riverbanks. Young men and women wound grain wreaths and carried them to the water's edge, releasing them downstream as the spring current carried their fortune out to sea and back as abundance. His connection to rivers also linked him to fertility in the older, pre-agricultural sense — rivers as conduits of life itself, sources of fish, irrigation, and renewal.

## The Festival of the Young

Patrimpas was a deity of youth in the social as well as agricultural sense. The Sudovian Book records that his spring rites were occasions for communal joy, when the normal hierarchies of Prussian society were relaxed and young people celebrated their bond to the land with games and feasting. Marriage negotiations are thought to have taken place at his festivals. His domain therefore crossed from agriculture into the human cycle of generation: birth, courtship, and the continuation of society.

## The Balanced Cosmos

The triumph of the Romuva triad as a theological concept is its completeness. Perkūns strikes, Patollo takes, and Patrimpas gives back. Seen this way, Patrimpas is not merely a harvest god but a cosmological argument: despite death, despite storms, the world renews itself. Spring returns. The grain germinates. The river fills. His idol at Romuva stood as a perpetual reassurance that the cold face of Patollo was not the final word, that the white shroud would give way each year to the grain crown of the beardless youth.
