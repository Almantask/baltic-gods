## Perkūno Oželis — The Bleating Voice of the Thunder God

The Common Snipe (*Gallinago gallinago*) is a small, cryptically patterned bird that performs one of the most dramatic displays in the natural world: a steep dive in which air rushing through its spread outer tail feathers produces a haunting, goat-like bleating that carries across open marshes and meadows. To the populations of the Baltic, who lived close to the seasonal rhythms of wetland and forest, this sound had a specific and urgent meaning: the Thunder God's celestial goat was calling from the sky, and the earth was about to be unlocked.

Nijolė Laurinkienė, in her extensive study of the Perkūnas complex, identifies the snipe as a key element in the spring rituals of the thunder cult. The first heard call of the "thunder goat" in spring — traditionally in April or May — was the signal that planting could safely begin. It meant that Perkūnas had returned from the cosmic winter, that his power was active again in the atmosphere, and that the rain his storms would bring would be welcomed rather than feared. Killing this bird was strictly forbidden; to do so was to cut the communication line between Perkūnas and the earth.

In some traditions, Perkūnas rides a chariot drawn by goats across the sky, and the snipe is understood as a scout — one of the celestial animals sent ahead to check conditions on the ground before the full force of the spring storms arrives. The bird's arrival thus marked the transition from the dead season to the living one, from the reign of Velnias (who dominated the underworld and winter) to the reign of Perkūnas (who dominated the sky and summer). Every spring, the Baltic peoples listened for this sound at the edge of the marshes, confirming that the cosmic order was turning on schedule.
