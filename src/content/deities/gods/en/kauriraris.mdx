## Kauriraris — Spirit of Battle Heat and Warriors' Rage

Kauriraris is one of the most obscure figures in Łasicki's list, named with no extended commentary, appearing in the war-deity section alongside Kovas. His name has resisted clear etymological resolution, but the most plausible analyses connect it to roots meaning warhorse, battle heat, or the specific physiological-psychological state of combat arousal.

In Baltic warrior culture, battle was not merely physical contest but a sacred undertaking. Warriors entered the battlefield in a state of heightened ritual consciousness — preparation involved sacrifices, special foods, invocations to the gods. Kauriraris seems to have been the divine force that activated this state: not just the protection from death, but the ignition of the fighting rage that makes a man dangerous in combat.

The concept parallels other Indo-European divine aspects of battle frenzy: the Norse berserker's Odin-trance, the Indic *ugra* (fierce heat of the warrior), the Irish ferg. Across these traditions, battle fury was understood as divinely granted, not merely physiological. Kauriraris may have been the Lithuanian personification of this divinely-granted fighting power.

His survival only as a list entry, without narrative myth, means we cannot know his full character. But his inclusion in the Baltic catalogue at all suggests he answered a real religious need: warriors needed to believe their battle rage was both real and sanctioned — that the gods had lit a fire in them that ordinary life could not sustain.
