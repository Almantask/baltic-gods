## Mājas māte — Mother of the House

Mājas māte is the Latvian spirit of the household, the female counterpart to Mājas kungs (the Lord of the House). Where the male spirit guarded the house from external threats, Mājas māte governed the internal life of the home: the harmony between its inhabitants, the health of children, the smooth running of domestic crafts, and the luck that chose to dwell within family walls.

She was believed to inhabit a specific corner of the main room, often the space near the hearth or beneath the threshold. Household luck — good harvests, healthy livestock, children who thrived — was understood as her active gift, something she bestowed on families who maintained a clean, ordered, and respectful home. Disorder, quarreling, and negligence could cause her to depart, taking the household’s fortune with her.

The Latvian dainas frequently address Mājas māte directly, asking her to remain within the home, to watch over sleeping children, and to ensure that the family’s work was blessed. She accepted no grand sacrifices, only the daily acts of care that demonstrated a family’s commitment to its home: bread baked with attention, floors swept before festival days, hospitality extended to travelers.

In her role as keeper of domestic luck, Mājas māte stands at the intersection of household management and religious observance. Her tradition belongs to the same Baltic worldview that populated every space with a guardian spirit, insisting that the divine was not remote but present in the most ordinary activities of daily life.
