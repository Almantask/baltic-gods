## The God Named in the Treaty

Kurke holds the distinction of being the only Old Prussian deity explicitly named in a surviving legal document: the Treaty of Christburg of 1249, in which the Teutonic Knights required newly baptised Prussians to renounce their gods by name. That Kurke appears in this document places him among the most historically attested figures in the entire Baltic pantheon, a grain deity important enough that Christian authorities marked him for specific condemnation.

## Grain, Granary, and the Last Sheaf

Kurke presided over the full arc of the agricultural year: the sowing, the growth, and above all the harvest. His principal ritual moment was the completion of the harvest, when the last sheaf of grain was cut and bound. In Old Prussian tradition this last sheaf was the material dwelling of the grain spirit, and it was treated with extraordinary care: brought into the farmstead with ceremony, hung in the granary through the winter, and burned at the following spring plowing to return its power to the earth. The effigy associated with this rite, called Curche, survived in Prussian folk practice long after formal Christianity.

## Continuity and Memory

Matthäus Praetorius, writing in the late seventeenth century, documented harvest customs in the Baltic region that preserved Curche's name and rites, indicating that despite three centuries of Christianisation, the grain deity's festival endured in folk memory. Christoph Hartknoch described wooden idols still being venerated as late as 1418, over 150 years after the Treaty of Christburg. Kurke represents the resilience of pre-Christian Baltic practice in the face of political and religious pressure.
