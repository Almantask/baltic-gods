## Erketus — Spirit of Thorns and Protective Barriers

The old Lithuanian farmstead was defined by its edges: the fence line separating cultivated land from the forest, the thorn hedge that discouraged wolves and thieves, the prickled boundary that said *this is ours, and beyond is wild*. Watching over every hawthorn, briar patch, and bramble-woven hedge-row was Erketus, the divine spirit of thorns.

Jan Łasicki's sixteenth-century survey of Samogitian deities records a god invoked *pro spinis* — for the thorns — the characteristic brevity of his catalogue leaving the spirit's function clear even when his mythology is thin. The Lithuanian root *erškėtis* (thorn, briar) is preserved intact in the deity's name, the divine and the vernacular occupying the same linguistic space in the way that older Baltic religion often allowed.

As a boundary deity, Erketus embodied the principle that transitions between spaces required divine sanction and protection. The hedge was not merely a physical barrier but a sacred threshold: beyond it lay the forest, which in Baltic thought was the domain of wood spirits, wild animals, and ambiguous powers. To plant a thorn hedge and invoke Erketus was to ask for a divine fortification against everything the wilderness might send.

Farmers who trimmed their boundary hedges or wove new sections of briar fence would leave small offerings — a piece of bread, a handful of salt, or a drop of beer — pressed into the hedge itself, a gift to Erketus for his continued service. Trees and shrubs with natural thorns, especially hawthorn and blackthorn, were considered sacred to him and were planted preferentially at the corners of farmsteads, where protective power was most needed.
