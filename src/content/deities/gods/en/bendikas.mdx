## Bendikas — The Deity You Invoke When You Leave Home

Lasicki recorded *Bendim pro peregrinatione* — Bendikas for a journey. The Latin word *peregrinatio* carries significant weight: it means not just travel but travel into foreign territory, pilgrimage, the condition of being a stranger in a strange place. Bendikas is not invoked for a walk to the next village; he is invoked when someone crosses a real threshold, leaving the known for the unknown, trading the protection of familiar spirits for the uncertainty of roads where those spirits do not follow.

The domestic Baltic world was comprehensively protected: the *dimstipatis* watched the homestead, the barn spirit watched the animals, Gabija kept the hearth, the *aitvaras* brought luck to the house. All of this protection was specific to the home space. Once a person stepped beyond their known territory — onto a road leading to a foreign market, a journey to a relative several days away, a trade expedition into a different region — that protection ceased. Bendikas filled this gap. Invoking him before departure was a ritual acknowledgment that the traveler was about to enter unsponsored territory, and a request that he extend oversight into those uncharted spaces.

Some scholars have noted the similarity between Bendikas and the Thracian goddess Bendis, worshipped in Ancient Greece and documented in Athens from the 5th century BCE. The connection, if it exists, would indicate a very ancient tradition stretching across the ancient world — the need to have a divine guardian for those in transit. More pragmatically, the Baltic Bendikas reflects the real danger of travel in 16th-century Samogitia: roads were unmarked, forests were extensive, bandits were real, weather was changeable. A traveler who left without proper invocation was leaving without proper armor. Bendikas was that armor in its divine form.
