## Kelukis / Gužė / Čiužė — Guardians of Roads and Smooth Travel

Kelukis, recorded by Łasicki as *Keluki, qui viis praeest* — "Kelukis, who presides over roads" — is the Lithuanian divine master of the road itself. His name derives directly from *kelias* (road, way, path), making him perhaps the most etymologically transparent deity in the Samogitian list. Roads mattered enormously in medieval Baltic country, where villages were separated by dense forest, and where a false turn or a washed-out ford could mean death from exposure.

Gužė and Čiužė, recorded by later lexicographers including Philipp Ruhig in his 1747 Lithuanian-German dictionary, represent more specialized aspects of travel. Čiužė's name comes from *čiuožti* — to glide, to slide — making her specifically the goddess of smooth movement through space. Her domain was winter sledge travel: the long runs across frozen fields and rivers when the sledge runners whispered over the snow and the horses found an easy rhythm. Smooth travel was not a luxury in a Baltic winter; it was the difference between reaching the market and freezing on the open road.

Crossroads held special religious significance as Kelukis's active site. Offerings were left at forks and intersections — small deposits of food, a coin, a pinch of salt — acknowledging that the choice of road was partly a divine matter and that the spirits needed to be consulted before one committed to a direction.

Together, Kelukis, Gužė, and Čiužė offer a portrait of Baltic road-religion: the personification of a technology (the road network) that was simultaneously physical, dangerous, social, and sacred.
