## Debesų Kareivis — The Warrior in the Storm Clouds

When the great summer cumulonimbus towers rose above the Baltic horizon — dark columns of cloud reaching from the earth to the upper atmosphere, flickering with lightning from their deepest layers — the people looking up from their fields did not see meteorology. They saw battalions. They saw the army of Perkūnas deployed across the sky, cavalry on white horses moving between the cloud-towers, fiery swords raised, hunting Velnias through every hiding place in the atmospheric world. Jonas Balys's collections are full of observations like "look — there, a soldier on a horse" pointing at the shapes of charged cloud columns above the horizon.

The Debesų Kareivis is the functional extension of Perkūnas's agency into the full scale of a storm system. Where the rykštė (lightning bolt) is the instrument, the cloud soldier is the wielder — the active intelligence that directs the strike, that recognizes Velnias wherever he hides and pursues him through the cloud structures. Thunder is the sound of chariot wheels on the celestial road, the crash of armies meeting. Rain is the aftermath of the battle — the blood of the sky, but also the healing fluid that the purified atmosphere releases onto the earth.

Pranė Dundulienė discusses the "war of the gods" as a recurring motif in Baltic visual culture: fighting figures in the clouds depicted in folk art, the warrior-god imagery that recurs in ceremonial objects. To witness the Debesų Kareivis riding his storm was reassuring rather than terrifying — it meant that the divine order was functioning, that Perkūnas was active and vigilant, that the chthonic forces would be struck back down before they could do serious damage, and that the rains that followed would be clean.
