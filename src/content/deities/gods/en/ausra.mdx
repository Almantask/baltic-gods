## Aušra — The Light That Opens the East

Aušra is the personified light of the breaking day — not the morning star (that is Aušrinė's domain), but the atmospheric glow itself: the rosy wash of light that spreads across the eastern horizon before the sun appears. Jan Łasicki recorded her in 1615 as a distinct deity in a celestial triad alongside Bežlėja (bright noon light) and Brėkšta (twilight), showing that the Lithuanians of his time maintained precise sacred classifications for each phase of daily light. These were not metaphors but separate presences with separate roles.

Norbertas Vėlius located Aušra within a broader Indo-European pattern in which dawn goddesses open gates for the solar chariot — Sanskrit Ushas, Greek Eos, Roman Aurora. What is specifically Baltic in Aušra's character is the emphasis on purification and beginning: her light cleanses what the night has touched and prepares the world to receive the sun's blessing. Folk practice associated dawn with healing, with the gathering of medicinal dew, and with first prayers spoken facing east. These practices invoked Aušra even when her name was not spoken.

Gintaras Beresnevičius analyses Aušra as part of a sophisticated hierarchy of light in Baltic thought — a hierarchy in which the quality of light at each hour of the day carries distinct sacred meaning. Aušra's light is the most liminal: the threshold between dark and day, between the world of spirits and the world of the living. In some traditions, the souls of the recently dead are said to ride Aušra's light toward the heavens, carried on her robe into the company of Saulė's daughters who tend the skies.
