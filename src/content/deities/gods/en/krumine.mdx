## Krūminė — Goddess of Bushes and the First Grain Ears

At the edge of every Lithuanian field lay a zone of ambiguity: the scrubby boundary of hawthorn, elder, and wild rose that separated the cultivated earth from the free forest. Neither fully tamed nor fully wild, this borderland was one of the most spiritually charged spaces in the Baltic landscape, and its divine guardian was Krūminė, the goddess of bushes and thickets.

Jan Łasicki recorded her with the expanded name *Kruminie Pradziamuarpu* — a Latinised rendering of *Krūminė Pradžių Varpų* — the Goddess of Bushes of the First Grain Ears. The second part of her name is crucial: she was not merely a nature spirit confined to the wilderness margin but a deity who crossed into the agricultural world at its most vulnerable moment, the very beginning of growth. She "cares for all shrubs, thickets, and meadows" according to Łasicki's testimony, and the first ears of grain that pushed up from the newly sown soil were considered to be under her specific protection.

In the Baltic agricultural imagination, this made sense: the boundary zone was where the domestic and the wild exchanged energies. The beneficial insects that pollinated crops came from the scrubland. The birds that ate the grain pests roosted in the hedgerow. The medicinal herbs that the village healer gathered grew thickest at the field's edge. Krūminė presided over all these exchanges, ensuring that the wild border served the farm rather than threatening it.

At the moment of first sprouting — when the tender green shoots of the new sowing broke the soil surface for the first time — farmers would walk the field boundary and leave offerings at the bushiest corners, acknowledging Krūminė's role in coaxing the first life from the earth.
