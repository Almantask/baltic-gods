# Žemininkas — Divine Steward of the Soil

The earth goddess **Žemyna** was the great universal principle of the earth — vast, impersonal, and encompassing. But the Lithuanian farmer who walked his fields each morning needed something more specific: a relationship not with the earth as a cosmic abstraction but with *this* soil, *this* particular plot of land that his grandfather had broken and his father had plowed and that he himself would one day be buried beneath. For this intensely local relationship, there was **Žemininkas** — the Divine Steward of the Soil.

Jan Łasicki recorded the spirit under the name *Ziemiennik*, describing offerings of the first harvest made to him and prayers for both prosperity and health. Crucially, Łasicki's account implies that each farmer had *their own* Žemininkas — that the spirit was not a single universal deity but a localized presence tied to a specific farm, a specific family, and a specific relationship with the land. This personalization of the agricultural covenant is distinctively Baltic: the earth was not an impersonal resource but an ongoing relationship requiring specific acknowledgment and respect.

Žemininkas was also connected to the ancestral dead. The soil that grew grain was the same soil that received the bodies of the farmer's forebears, and the fertility of the land was in some sense the accumulated blessing of all who had worked and died there before. Norbertas Vėlius analyzed this connection between the earth cult and the ancestor cult as a core feature of Baltic agrarian religion: to honor Žemininkas was simultaneously to honor the chain of the dead who had made the land what it was.

The sacred grass snake (**Žaltys**), often seen in fields and gardens, was understood as Žemininkas's visible representative — the point where the invisible spirit of the soil made itself perceptible to human eyes. To respect the snake in one's field was to maintain a proper relationship with the earth itself.
