## Samanis — Spirit of the Moss

In the ancient forests of Lithuania, where oak and ash and linden rose in cathedral columns above a floor carpeted with soft green moss, every layer of the woodland had its governing spirit. The canopy was the domain of the grove-spirits and the tree-souls; the roots reached toward Velnias in the underworld; and the floor itself, that dense living mat of mosses and lichens that held the forest's moisture and sheltered its smallest creatures, was the kingdom of Samanis.

Jan Łasicki's sixteenth-century survey records Samanis as the deity *qui in musco habitant* — "who dwells in the moss" — a characteristically concise description that captures the spirit's essence perfectly. Samanis was not a distant celestial deity but an intimate one: he lived underfoot, in the soft yielding ground that a barefoot person could feel sinking slightly with each step. To walk through a mossy forest was to walk through his presence.

For the Lithuanian woodsman, moss was not merely a ground cover. It was a water reservoir that kept streams flowing in dry seasons by releasing stored moisture slowly. It was an insulator that prevented the forest floor from freezing solid in winter, protecting the dormant seeds that would germinate in spring. It was a soft bed for the forest's animals, a hiding place for the small creatures who formed the base of the woodland food web. All of this was Samanis's work — the careful, patient, invisible maintenance of the forest's most fundamental layer.

Offerings to Samanis were modest: a few grains of rye or barley pressed into the moss when entering the forest for gathering expeditions, a quiet acknowledgment that the floor you walked upon was alive and cared for. He was not a god you prayed to for dramatic help but a presence you acknowledged with the respect due to any creature going quietly about its essential work.
