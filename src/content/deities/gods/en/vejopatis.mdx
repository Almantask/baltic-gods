## Lord of the Winds

Vėjopatis — whose name means simply *Lord of Wind* — is one of the most ancient Lithuanian atmospheric deities, catalogued by Jan Łasicki in 1582 among the gods of the Samogitians. He governs every breath of wind from the gentlest summer breeze stirring flax fields to the winter gale bending pines flat on the Žemaitija highlands. Where the wind moves, Vėjopatis is present.

## Guide of Souls

In Lithuanian cosmology the wind carries more than weather — it carries the souls of the dead. Vėjopatis is closely associated with the afterlife realm of Dausos, the bright sky-world to which righteous souls ascend. Believers tied strips of linen to pines on hilltops so the wind could carry their prayers to him, and funerary poems speak of the wind sweeping the road clean for departing souls. His counterpart in the underworld journey is Velnias, but where Velnias rules the destination, Vėjopatis governs the passage.

## Ritual and Worship

Offerings to Vėjopatis were simple and wind-delivered: feathers released from hilltops, grain scattered into a strong breeze, and linen strips tied to exposed trees in high places. The Samogitian tradition held that no sailor should set out without first invoking him, and that the direction of the first gust after dawn revealed his mood for the day. Spring equinox bonfires on hilltop ridges were also said to honor him, their smoke rising straight upward as a sign of his approval.
