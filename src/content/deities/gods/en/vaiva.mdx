## Vaiva — The Rainbow, Belt of Laima

Whenever a summer storm rolled across the Lithuanian plains and the sky cracked with Perkūnas's thunder, the people watched the horizon for the arc of colours that signalled peace: Vaiva, the living rainbow, stretching her luminous belt from earth to sky and back again. She was understood not merely as an atmospheric phenomenon but as the personal adornment of Laima, the fate goddess, whose cosmic sash bridged the gap between the divine realm and the mortal world.

In folk tradition, the rainbow was said to drink from rivers and lakes, bending her great arc down to touch the water's surface. This mythological act connected Vaiva to the water cycle — she drew up moisture from the earth and returned it later as life-giving rain, making her an intermediary in the agricultural drama of drought and abundance that governed rural Lithuanian life.

Some regional traditions depicted Vaiva as a weaving maiden who stretched coloured threads across the sky in the hours after a storm, her loom being the arch of heaven itself. The seven bands of colour corresponded to different blessings: red for health, yellow for harvest, blue for water, green for the forest, and so on in a cosmological key known to the *žyniai* (wise ones) of each village.

Lithuanian women sometimes wore woven sashes patterned with rainbow motifs as protective amulets, linking their clothing to Vaiva's celestial garment. The dual identity — as Laima's belt and as an independent rain spirit — reflects the layered quality of Baltic folk religion, where a single natural phenomenon could carry multiple sacred meanings simultaneously.
