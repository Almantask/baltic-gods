# Gulbi — The Fire-Swan of Długosz

In the 15th-century chronicle of **Jan Długosz**, there appears a curious figure: **Gulbi**, whom the chronicler mapped to the Roman god **Vulcan** as the Lithuanian deity of fire and smithing. The name is unmistakably related to *gulbė* (swan), and the identification has puzzled mythologists ever since. How does a swan become a smith? What is the connection between the graceful white bird of fate and the fire-wielding craftsman of the forge?

The most likely explanation, proposed by Gintaras Beresnevičius and Norbertas Vėlius, is that Długosz was using his *interpretatio romana* method — fitting native Baltic beliefs into Roman categories without perfect correspondence. He may have heard a name related to fire or smithing that sounded to his ear like the Lithuanian word for swan, or he may have encountered an epithet for the celestial smith **Teliavelis** or **Kalvelis** that did not survive in other sources. Alternatively, "Gulbi" may have been a localized fire-spirit whose name happened to coincide with the swan-word, creating a false etymological connection that misled the chronicler.

In Slavic folklore adjacent to the Baltic world, the firebird (*Zhar-Ptitsa*) represents the swan's transformation into a creature of flame. It is not impossible that a cognate tradition existed in Lithuanian mythology — the swan that ascends into fire, the graceful creature that carries the sun's heat in its wings. If Gulbi carries any genuine mythological content, it may be this: the swan as a solar and luminous being, whose purity of white feathers contains a hidden kinship with the purifying fire that the smith commands at his forge.

Gulbi thus occupies a fascinating liminal position in the historical record — a name that may encode a genuine but obscure mythological tradition, or may be the product of a chronicler's creative confusion, or both at once.
