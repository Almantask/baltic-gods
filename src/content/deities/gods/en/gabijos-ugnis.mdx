## Gabijos Ugnis — The Fire That Is Never Allowed to Go Out

When Jesuit missionaries arrived in Lithuania in the 1580s, they expected to find paganism. What they did not expect was how ordinary it would look. One report from 1583 describes the discovery, in farmhouse after farmhouse, of a fire burning in the hearth that the family did not allow to go out — not a ceremonial fire, not a fire at a special time, but the continuous household fire, fed carefully, covered in ash at night to preserve it, reopened in the morning with prayer. The Jesuits noted that families spoke to this fire, offered it the first pinch of salt from each meal, and treated it as a living presence. They called it by a name: *Gabija*.

Gabija was the goddess of the sacred hearth; Gabijos ugnis (Gabija's fire) was her embodiment in the physical flame. Norbertas Vėlius documented the specific protocol: when the fire needed to be transported to a new house, a woman carried it in a covered vessel speaking formal words of invitation, asking the fire to come willingly. To spit into the fire was among the most serious ritual offenses — equivalent to spitting in a deity's face. To let the fire go out entirely was to invite disaster: illness, poverty, death. The fire was the family's continuity made visible. As long as it burned, the household was spiritually alive.

Vėlius connects this practice to the ancient Indo-European sacred hearth tradition — the same fire preserved in the Roman Temple of Vesta, the same fire invoked in the Vedic *Agni* hymns. In Lithuania it persisted not as archaism but as living practice, so woven into daily domestic life that Christianization could not simply abolish it. Jesuit records note that even after formal conversion, many families maintained their hearth practices in modified form — keeping a candle burning before a saint's icon in place of an open fire, but following the same protocols of address, feeding, and protection. Gabija was renamed, but the fire was still there.
