## The Fire-Serpent of the Loft

In the farmsteads of Kurzeme and Zemgale, the pūķis announces itself at night: a trail of fire crossing the sky above the barn, then a red glow in the hayloft window. If you are the one it has chosen, it lands on the roof beam and coils there, a glittering serpent with ember eyes. By morning, your grain bins are full.

## The Pact

The pūķis demands a contract. Its master must never speak of it, never beat it, never neglect to leave cream and black bread on the barn step each evening. The pūķis serves willingly — stealing grain from neighbours, bringing coins found on roads, protecting cattle from sickness. But when the master dies, the pūķis claims the soul. This is the price Pēteris Šmits documented from dozens of Latvian farm communities in the nineteenth century.

## Forms and Appearances

The pūķis is a shape-shifter. Most commonly it appears as a flying fire-serpent — a great snake whose belly glows red like forge-coals. But it may also take the form of a black rooster whose crow wakes no one, or a glossy black cat that appears without footprints in the snow. In each form it leaves the same sign: a scorch-mark where it lands.

## The Threshold of the Contract

Those who wished to summon a pūķis were said to hatch one from a seven-year-old rooster's egg, kept warm under the arm for nine days. Folk magicians warned that the spirit would test its new master three times before settling — appearing in increasingly frightening forms — and that only the truly resolute would complete the bargain. Most who tried fled after the second test.
