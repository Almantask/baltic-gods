## The White-Shrouded Elder

At the sacred grove of Romuva, three idols stood beneath an eternal oak where fire burned without ceasing. The rightmost idol, draped in a white linen shroud and carved with a face as pale as a week-old corpse, belonged to Patollo — the Old Prussian master of death and the underworld. Simon Grunau's chronicle of 1529 describes this figure in detail: an aged man with sunken eyes, his head wrapped in white cloth, standing watch over the shadow realm the way a sentinel watches a gate that no one may pass without permission.

## The Romuva Triad

Patollo shares the sacred oak at Romuva with two fellow gods: the thundering Perkūns and the youthful Patrimpas. The three represent a complete vision of the cosmos — sky-power, life-force, and death. Patollo holds the third station: winter, ending, and the space beneath the roots. He is not evil in Old Prussian religion so much as necessary. The eternal fire that burns before his idol was tended by virgin priests called Vaidilutes, and its extinction was considered a catastrophe for the entire community.

## Fear and Appeasement

Patollo's displeasure manifested as plague, famine, and cattle sickness. Medieval chronicles record that when a Prussian community suffered misfortune, the Criwe — the high priest of Romuva — divined whether Patollo had been neglected. Offerings of black animals, carved skulls, and ash from the sacred fire were presented. The goal was never to destroy death but to negotiate with it, to convince the pale elder to turn his gaze elsewhere for another season.

## Legacy in the Baltic Night

With the Christianisation of Prussia in the 13th and 14th centuries, Patollo's image fragmented. His pale face merged with the Christian devil in popular imagination, his white shroud with the burial cloths of the church. Yet the folk memory of a powerful lord of winter who must be honoured persisted in Baltic peasant tradition well into the 18th century. Pātalos — demons of sickness and shadow — appear in Latvian folklore as distant echoes of the Old Prussian death-god who once occupied one of three sacred thrones beneath the world-oak.
