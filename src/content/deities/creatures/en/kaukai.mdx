## Kaukai — Earth Spirits of the Deep

Kaukai are small, industrious subterranean spirits of Lithuanian mythology, intimately associated with the deep earth and the accumulated fertility it contains. Where the great gods governed storms, fate, and the celestial vault, the Kaukai belonged to the intimate world of the farmstead and the field — busy, practical beings who helped sustain the agricultural life from below.

In physical descriptions, Kaukai are small, hairless or lightly furred beings, rarely seen but often sensed through unexplained warmth in the soil, sudden abundance in a grain store, or tasks mysteriously completed overnight. They respond to offerings of milk and simple food left near the threshold or in root cellars. In return, they aid in farm labor — threshing grain, tending livestock, carrying burdens during the harvest season.

The Kaukai are closely related to the Barzdukai, small "bearded" dwarfs who live specifically under elder trees (*sambucus*) or in deep forests. The Barzdukai are often described as servants of the forest-earth deity Puschkaitis, suggesting that the Kaukai form part of a broader network of subterranean helping-spirits who mediate between the human world and the hidden forces of the earth.

The 17th-century chronicler Matthaeus Praetorius documented the Kaukai and Puschkaitis cult as still active among Lithuanian peasants of his time, recording the offering rituals and describing their role in agricultural prosperity. This documentation captures the final independent expressions of a spirit-belief that stretched back through the entire Baltic Bronze and Iron Ages.
