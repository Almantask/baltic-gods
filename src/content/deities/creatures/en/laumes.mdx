## The Spinners in the Clearing

The Laumes are one of the most complex and mythologically rich figures in Lithuanian tradition: beautiful supernatural women who dwell in forests and at the edges of water, appearing at night in moonlit clearings to spin, weave, and dance. Their origins go deep into Baltic prehistory: Marija Gimbutas saw them as survivals of a Neolithic goddess complex, and their connection to spinning and weaving links them to the pan-Baltic tradition of fate goddesses who literally weave the threads of human destiny.

## Protectors and Thieves

The Laumes are ambivalent. They care for orphaned and abandoned children, nursing them in the forest with supernatural tenderness. But they also steal newborn infants from unwatched cribs, leaving in their place a changeling, a wailing malformed substitute that will drain a family's resources. To protect against this, mothers kept constant watch over newborns, and specific protective charms, salt under the pillow, iron near the cradle, were employed. The spinning and weaving Laumes could also bless or curse human weaving: a woman who left her work unfinished overnight might find it completed by morning, or completely ruined.

## Forest Presence

When the Laumes were seen in the forest, they were most often described as tall, pale, naked or in white, their hair loose and wet. Meeting them could mean enchantment: men who followed them disappeared for years, or returned with no memory of where they had been. Their spindles and weaving equipment, left behind at the site of their work, were considered powerfully charged objects: to take them was dangerous, to leave them an offering was prudent.
