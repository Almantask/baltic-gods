## Lietuvēns — The Nightmare Rider

Lietuvēns is the Latvian personification of sleep paralysis and the nightmare — a spirit believed to enter sleeping rooms through a keyhole or a small crack in the wall, then settle with crushing weight on the chest of the sleeper, making it impossible to breathe or call out. He was also said to ride horses in the night, leaving them lathered and exhausted in the stable by morning.

According to Latvian folk belief, Lietuvēns is typically the soul of a person who died an unnatural or untimely death: an unbaptized infant, a suicide, someone murdered and left unburied. These souls, unable to proceed to the realm of the dead by normal channels, remained in the world of the living and expressed their agitation by afflicting the living with their weight. The connection to the unquiet dead gives Lietuvēns a character distinct from purely demonic entities — he is a tragedy as much as a threat.

Protection against Lietuvēns required both practical and ritual measures. Marking the doorframe and window with a cross at night was considered essential. Iron objects — a knife, a sickle, scissors — placed under the pillow or beside the bed would repel him, as iron was believed to cut the spiritual bonds that such creatures used to enter. Saying a prayer before sleep or placing blessed herbs under the mattress also offered defense.

Pēteris Šmits, the foundational scholar of Latvian mythology, documented Lietuvēns extensively in his 1918 survey of Latvian folk beliefs. His accounts preserve dozens of individual testimonies: farmers who woke to find their horses ruined, villagers who described feeling pinned to their beds by an invisible weight, women who reported a cold presence in the room just before the paralysis began. In each case, the folk logic was clear: the boundary between the living and the dead was permeable, and Lietuvēns was what crossed it in the hours of deepest sleep.
