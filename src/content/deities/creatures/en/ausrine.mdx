## First Light

Before Saulė wakes, Aušrinė is already at work. She kindles the fire of the sky, spreads the white cloth of dawn across the horizon, and ties the horses to the celestial stable post. In Baltic folk songs — the dainos — she is called Saulė's handmaiden, though in the oldest layers she may be a celestial goddess in her own right, the Morning Star identified with the planet Venus.

## The Dawn Preparations

Łasicki's 1582 list of Samogitian deities names her alongside Saulė. Folk poetry elaborates her role vividly: she rises while her mistress still sleeps, sweeps the sky-bridge, and lays pine branches for Saulė's sledge to cross without catching. When sparrows chirp before dawn, they do so in Aušrinė's service.

## The Rivalry and the Song

In some dainos, Aušrinė is Saulė's daughter rather than servant, and her relationship with Mėnulis (the Moon) is as complex as her mother's. She is wooed by the Evening Star and the Morning Star trade places in the sky. This astronomical reality — Venus as both morning and evening star — gives rise to mythic sequences where Aušrinė is separated from her celestial kin and must find her way back before sunrise.

## Ritual at Dawn

Girls in Lithuania and Samogitia rose before dawn on certain feast days to wash their faces in dew still cold on the grass — dew considered Aušrinė's tears of longing as she waited for Saulė. The first person to see the morning star would whisper a wish before speaking to anyone else, for Aušrinė heard wishes made in the liminal space between dark and light.
