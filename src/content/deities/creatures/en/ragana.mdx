## Ragana — Seer and Witch

Ragana is one of the most prominent and complex figures in Baltic tradition. Her name derives from the Lithuanian verb *regėti*, meaning "to see" — a reminder that she was originally not a figure of evil but of profound vision. In her archaic form she was a seer, a mistress of the threshold between the living and the dead, a wise woman who possessed knowledge of herbs, fate, and the hidden structures of the world.

Over time, and especially under the influence of Christian demonology, the Ragana became the classical witch: a powerful, often elderly woman who lived in the forest and had mastered the arts of shape-shifting. She could transform into a cat, a bird, or a sow. She was known to fly — on a broom or on the back of a demon — to great nocturnal gatherings at Šatrija Hill in Samogitia, where the witches danced and plotted mischief until dawn.

In folk belief, Raganos were blamed for "stealing" milk from livestock, for bringing diseases to neighbors' fields, and for hexing marriages. But the same community that feared them also sought them out: a Ragana, properly approached, could heal as well as harm. She knew which herbs cured fevers, which roots ended pregnancies, which words drove away a curse. She represented the dangerous complementarity of knowledge — that it cannot be divided cleanly into good and evil.

The trial records of the 16th and 17th centuries document widespread belief in Raganos across Lithuania and the Baltic region. These records, compiled by the same Christian authorities that persecuted witches, paradoxically preserve the richest primary documentation of folk magical belief in the region. Through their distorting lens, we can still see the outlines of the original seer — the woman who had looked too deeply into the hidden world and could not look away.
