## The Fiery Serpent of the Roof Ridge

The Aitvaras is one of the most vivid figures in Lithuanian folk belief, a household spirit that can appear as a rooster with a fiery tail, a black cat, or most famously as a streak of fire or a flying snake arcing across the night sky. When it descends to a particular roof, the household is claimed as its domain. It brings grain and gold to its masters, but never freely: everything it delivers has been taken from somewhere else, from a neighbour's granary, from a passing stranger's purse, from the cosmic supply of wealth that moves between households in the night.

## Making a Pact

Acquiring an Aitvaras was a serious undertaking that Jan Lasicki documented among the Samogitians, and later sources elaborate the ritual: one could purchase one from a sorcerer, hatch one from the egg of a seven-year-old rooster, or make a pact with a spirit already circling a new farmstead. Once bound, the Aitvaras required regular feeding, typically with omelets, milk, and grain, and could not be easily dismissed. Failing to feed it could turn it destructive; and a dead Aitvaras meant misfortune for the household it had served.

## Ambivalence and Guilt

The Aitvaras embodies the deep ambivalence at the heart of desires for material wealth. The household that prospers through an Aitvaras knows that its prosperity comes at a cost to others, and this moral weight appears in the folklore: neighbours whisper, the Aitvaras is seen, and eventually the family that sheltered it faces judgement. Witch trial records from the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries document fearful confessions of Aitvaras ownership, reflecting how deeply the figure was integrated into the lived anxieties of Baltic rural life.
