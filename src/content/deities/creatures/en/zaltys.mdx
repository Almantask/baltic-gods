## The Holy Snake of the Hearth

The Zaltys is the sacred grass snake of Baltic tradition, one of the most consistently documented figures across Lithuanian and Latvian sources. Jan Lasicki described Lithuanian families keeping grass snakes in their homes, beneath the hearth or in a special corner, fed on milk and given a specific domestic status that placed them between family members and household goods. In Latvia the cognate figure is the Zalktis, who appears throughout the dainas as a sacred creature whose care is both a practical act of protection and a spiritual duty.

## The Snake as Mediator

In the Baltic religious worldview, the grass snake occupied a unique position: it moved between the underworld and the domestic surface, between the realm of Velnias and the realm of human household order. As such it was a mediator, a living bridge between the cosmic zones. To maintain a Zaltys in the home was to maintain a channel to the powers below, which sent protection and fertility upward through the snake's body. The snake that drank from the household milk bowl was drawing on the household's stored vitality and returning it as blessing.

## The Sacrilege of Killing

The killing of a domestic Zaltys was the gravest imaginable domestic sacrilege. Jesuit missionaries noted families in seventeenth-century Samogitia who wept when ordered to kill their household snakes as part of Christian conversion, and who hid them rather than comply. The death of a Zaltys was believed to bring illness and death to the household, and folklore records many stories of people who killed such snakes and suffered immediate punishment: sickness, accident, the death of cattle.
