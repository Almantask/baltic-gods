## Slogutė — The Night Presser

Slogutė (also called *Sloga* or *Marė*) is the Lithuanian personification of sleep paralysis and nightmares. She is described in folk tradition as a heavy, invisible weight or a small, pale creature that sits on the chest of a sleeping person, making it impossible to breathe or move — pressing all air from the lungs and all will from the body.

The experience she embodies is physiologically precise: sleep paralysis, the state in which the mind awakens but the body remains locked in the muscle-atonia of deep sleep. In this condition, the sleeper may feel crushing pressure on the chest, hear sounds, and experience vivid, often terrifying visions. Baltic folk knowledge interpreted these experiences as the visit of a spiritual being and developed elaborate protective practices in response.

To drive Slogutė away, folk practitioners recommended keeping iron objects near the bed — a knife, a pair of scissors, or an iron ring. Iron was universally believed in Baltic tradition to repel spirits and break magical paralysis. Moving a single toe was said to suffice to break her grip. The placing of blessed herbs under the pillow or marking the bedpost with a cross were additional remedies.

Slogutė belongs to a class of Slavic and Baltic nightmare-spirits found across northern Europe: the Latvian Lietuvēns, the Slavic *Mora*, the Germanic *Alp*. Their widespread presence in folklore represents the universal human encounter with hypnagogic and hypnopompic hallucination — and the extraordinary variety of spiritual explanations that different cultures developed to account for the same terrifying experience.
