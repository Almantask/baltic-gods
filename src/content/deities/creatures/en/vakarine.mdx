## The Lamp of Dusk

When Saulė's chariot reaches the western sea and the great sun-wheel tips toward the horizon, it is Vakarinė who lights the way. As the Evening Star, the planet Venus glowing in the dimming sky, she is the last light of the day — a silver lamp held aloft to guide the Sun to her rest. Folk songs describe her laying out linen sheets on the floor of the sea, arranging the silver basin and comb, smoothing the great bed where Saulė will sleep until Aušrinė wakes her again.

## Sister to Aušrinė

Vakarinė and her sister Aušrinė form the celestial pair that encloses each day. Aušrinė kindles the Morning Star to greet Saulė's return; Vakarinė tends the evening threshold where she departs. In Lithuanian dainas, their voices echo: one sings the opening of the sky-gate, the other sings its closing. Together they represent the full arc of the sun's daily journey, performing the domestic rites of a heavenly household that keeps the cosmos in order.

## Twilight Offerings

On hills facing west, Lithuanian women gathered at harvest's end to sing to Vakarinė as the first star appeared. Candles of beeswax were lit and set in west-facing windows, silver-threaded cloths unfolded from chests where they had been woven through winter. The songs asked Vakarinė to carry the warmth of summer into the coming darkness and return it with light when Aušrinė rose again in spring.

## The Eternal Rhythm

Vakarinė embodies rest as sacred cycle. She does not mourn the sun's departure: she prepares for its return. Her astronomy is peace — the planet Venus steady in the dusk speaks of order, of faithfulness, of the promise that morning will come. In the Latvian tradition she is called Vakara zvaigzne and serves the same function for Saule, one of the daughters of the sky who maintains the household of the heavens through the quiet work of the twilight hour.
