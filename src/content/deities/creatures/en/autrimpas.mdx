## Lord of the Baltic Deep

The Prussian shore is not gentle. The Baltic in winter heaves grey and merciless, and the amber-carrying currents that make the coast valuable also drown the fishermen who depend on them. Autrimpas — the Old Prussian god of the sea and all open water — governed this dual nature. Coastal Sambians invoked him before every significant voyage, offering the first fish of the season cast back into the waves, asking that the Baltic's depths remain generous and its storms remain short.

## The Neptune Comparison

Medieval chronicles, seeking Roman parallels to organise their descriptions of Prussian religion, consistently compared Autrimpas to Neptune. The comparison is imprecise but illuminating: like Neptune, Autrimpas commanded the sea's depths, held power over storms, and accepted offerings from sailors. Unlike the Roman god's majestic distance, Autrimpas was worshipped with an intimacy appropriate to the subsistence fishermen of the Sambian coast, who needed him to be present and responsive rather than olympian and removed.

## Amber as Sacred Currency

The Baltic coastline produces the most amber in the world, and amber was the sacred material of the Prussian coast. Before a voyage, amber was cast into the sea — returning to the water one of the shore's most prized treasures as an act of reciprocity. Autrimpas received this offering as acknowledgment that the sea owned the amber first and was only lending it to those who collected it. The theology of the Baltic fisherman was one of careful balance: take from the sea proportionally; give back before taking more.

## Kinship with Bangpūtys

Autrimpas and Bangpūtys — the Lithuanian sea and storm deity — reflect the same basic mythological structure from different tribal traditions. Where Bangpūtys is the blower-up-of-waves who personifies the storm's fury, Autrimpas is more broadly cast as the ruler of the deep, encompassing both calm and tempest. Their relationship in reconstructed Baltic mythology suggests a common Proto-Baltic sea deity whose identity split along tribal lines as Prussians, Lithuanians, and Latvians developed distinct mythological traditions.
