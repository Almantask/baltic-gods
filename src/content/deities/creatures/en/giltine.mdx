## The Pale Goddess

Giltinė does not announce herself with thunder or fire. She appears at dusk beside the sickbed — a tall, pale woman in white linen, her long tongue flickering over the dying to taste the last breath. In Lithuanian folk belief she is Laima's sister, the balance to every fate: where Laima assigns life, Giltinė completes it.

## Sister of Fate

The chronicles of Jan Łasicki (1582) record her among the Samogitian deities as the goddess who governs death. She was once a gentle healer, but after being imprisoned in a pit by a mortal for seven years, she emerged emaciated and consumed by spite. From that day her touch became lethal and her tongue, long from years of licking tree roots for sustenance, could extract the soul with a single lick.

## The Death-Watch

Families in Samogitia knew the signs: a dog howling three times, an owl landing at the roof's peak, or the sudden smell of cold resin with no fire burning. These were Giltinė's heralds. When she arrived, no door could be locked against her. The wise left a small bowl of milk and white bread for her by the hearth — not to bribe her away, but to honour the passage she provided.

## Offerings and Appeasement

On Vėlinės — All Souls Night — fires were kept bright and food set out for both the returning dead and the goddess who watched over them. Giltinė never took those she was not meant to take. Her cold mercy was considered just: every death she presided over was a return, not a destruction.
