## Eglė — Queen of Serpents

Eglė žalčių karalienė — Eglė, Queen of the Serpents — is the heroine of Lithuania's most profound and archaic myth. In the story, a young girl named Eglė agrees to marry the serpent king Žilvinas after he emerges from the sea and takes one of her braids as a pledge. She goes to live in his underwater palace, where she discovers that Žilvinas is handsome and noble in his own realm, and with him she has three sons and a daughter.

Her brothers eventually find her and persuade her to reveal, through cunning, the secret word that calls Žilvinas from the depths. Armed with this knowledge, they lure the serpent king out and kill him. When Eglė learns of the betrayal, grief and rage overwhelm her, and she transforms herself and her children into trees. Eglė becomes a spruce — *eglė* in Lithuanian — while her three sons become the oak (*ąžuolas*), the ash (*uosis*), and the birch (*beržas*), and her daughter becomes the trembling aspen (*drebulė*).\n\nThe myth is at once a nature-origin tale, a meditation on the permanence of an oath, and a theogonic narrative about the relationship between the human world and the chthonic serpent realm. The sacred serpent *Žaltys* was a revered household spirit in Baltic religion, and Žilvinas represents its royal dimension. Eglė's transformation is not only grief but also a form of permanence and triumph: unlike her husband, she cannot be killed. She becomes part of the forest that endures.

The tale has been collected in hundreds of variants across Lithuania, Belarus, and the Lithuanian diaspora, making it one of the most widely distributed Baltic myth-narratives in the scholarly record. Jonas Basanavičius, the father of Lithuanian ethnography, collected many of these variants in the 19th century, recognizing in the story the most archaic stratum of Lithuanian spiritual memory.
