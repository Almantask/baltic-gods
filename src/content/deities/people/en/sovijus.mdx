## The Man Who Demanded Fire

Sovijus appears in the Hypatian Chronicle of 1261 in an embedded legend explaining the origin of the Lithuanian practice of cremation. He is described as a hunter who killed nine wild boars and, after cooking and eating them, could not sleep. His sons, failing to understand what was wrong with him, first buried him, then placed him on a tree, and finally burned him on a pyre. Only at that moment did Sovijus find rest, and the practice of cremating the dead became established.

## Nine Underworld Gates

The Hypatian version of the story is more elaborate: Sovijus descends through nine gates of the underworld, and at each gate he is asked by the guardian what he has brought. At the ninth gate he is finally accepted and his body can be properly disposed of. Some interpretations see the nine gates as the nine layers of the Baltic afterlife cosmology, through which the soul must pass in sequence.

## Historical Significance

The Sovijus legend is one of the most historically important mythological texts from the Baltic world because it survives in a medieval chronicle rather than a nineteenth-century folklore collection. It confirms that cremation was practiced by Lithuanians in the thirteenth century, supported by archaeological evidence. The story also encodes a theological argument: cremation, by releasing the body to fire, sets the soul on the correct path.
