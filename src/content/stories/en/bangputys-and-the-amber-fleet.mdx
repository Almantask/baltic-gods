The Curonians were a seafaring people, bold and proud. Their longships cut through the grey Baltic waters with the confidence of those who believed themselves masters of the sea. But the sea has only one master, and his name is Bangpūtys.

Bangpūtys, god of waves and storms, dwelled in the depths beneath Jūrmala where the currents are coldest and the pressure crushes light itself. He was not cruel, but he demanded respect. Every Curonian captain knew to pour a cup of ale into the waves before setting sail and to speak Bangpūtys's name with reverence.

One summer, a fleet of twelve ships set out from the Curonian Spit, laden with amber bound for distant markets. The fleet's admiral, a man named Gintaras, was the wealthiest trader on the coast. He laughed at the old customs and poured no ale. "The sea is water," he said, "not a god."

For three days the fleet sailed under clear skies, and Gintaras boasted louder with each passing mile. On the fourth day, the horizon turned the color of iron. A wind came from nowhere — not from east or west, but from below, as if the sea itself were exhaling.

Bangpūtys rose from the waves in a column of spray and foam. His voice was the sound of breakers on rock. "You carry the tears of Saulė in your holds," he thundered, "and you think to cross my domain without a word?"

The storm lasted three days and three nights. Eleven of the twelve ships were driven back to shore, their cargo scattered across the waves. Only one ship survived the open sea — the smallest vessel, whose captain, a young woman named Jūratė, had quietly poured her offering before departure.

Jūratė's ship arrived safely, and the amber she carried was said to glow with an inner light, as though Bangpūtys himself had blessed it. Gintaras was found clinging to a spar off the coast of Nida, humbled and half-drowned.

He never sailed without an offering again, and he carved Bangpūtys's likeness into the prow of every ship he built thereafter.
