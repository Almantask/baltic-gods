Before the first breath is drawn, before a mother's cry of joy, Laima is already at work. In her dwelling beyond the mist — where the rivers of time converge — she sits at a loom of silver birch and moonlight, weaving the thread of each new life.

The Latvians say that Laima arrives at every birth in the form of a cuckoo, perching on the rooftop and calling out the number of years the child will live. The Lithuanians whisper that she appears as an old woman at the crossroads, spinning thread from a distaff made of fate itself.

Once, a young couple in Kurzeme prayed for a child. Laima heard their plea and wove a thread of extraordinary brightness — golden as amber, strong as iron. But Laima's sister, Giltinė, the bringer of death, coveted the thread. She crept to the loom in the dark hours and tried to cut it with her bone shears.

Laima awoke and seized Giltinė's wrist. The two struggled until dawn, and when the first sunlight touched the loom, Giltinė fled. But she had nicked the thread, and so the child born that morning carried a small scar above her heart — a mark that reminded her, all her days, that life is precious precisely because it can be cut short.

The child grew to become a great healer, and at every birth she attended, she would leave a small woven band of linen at the threshold — an offering to Laima, asking the goddess to weave long and brightly.
