Every Lithuanian home once had a guardian — not a lock, not a wall, but a flame. Gabija, the spirit of the sacred fire, dwelled in every hearth. She was not merely warmth; she was the living memory of the household, the keeper of its luck and its honor.

The rules of Gabija were simple and ancient. Never spit into the fire. Never throw refuse upon the coals. And above all, never let the fire die completely — for if the last ember went cold, Gabija would leave, and misfortune would follow.

In a village near Kernavė, a young bride named Danutė moved into her husband's home. She was from the city and did not know the old ways. One night, tired and careless, she let the hearth fire go out entirely.

The next morning, the milk curdled. The bread would not rise. The cat hissed at the cold hearth and fled. Danutė's mother-in-law wept and said, "Gabija has left us."

Danutė, determined to set things right, walked to the oldest house in the village — the home of a wise woman named Morta. Morta's hearth had burned without interruption for forty years. She gave Danutė a single coal wrapped in birch bark.

"Carry it home without speaking," Morta said. "Place it in the hearth and feed it with dried oak. Then sit beside it through the night and tell Gabija your name, your mother's name, and your grandmother's name. She will know you are worthy."

Danutė did as she was told. As the flame grew from the coal, she felt a warmth that was more than heat — it was welcome. The cat returned and curled by the fire. The milk was sweet again by morning.

Danutė never let the fire die again, and she taught her own daughters the same: Gabija lives in the flame, and the flame lives in the family.
