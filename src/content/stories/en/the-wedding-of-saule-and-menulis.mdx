In the beginning, Saulė the Sun and Mėnulis the Moon were wed in a ceremony attended by all the gods. Dievas himself blessed their union, and Perkūnas forged their wedding rings from thunderbolts. The stars were their bridesmaids, and Aušrinė, the Morning Star, carried the train of Saulė's golden gown.

For a time, all was radiant. Saulė warmed the earth by day, and Mėnulis lit the paths of travelers by night. Together they kept the wheel of time turning in perfect balance.

But Mėnulis grew restless. He began to court Aušrinė, drawn by her shimmering beauty at the edge of dawn. When Saulė discovered the betrayal, her grief shook the heavens. She wept tears of amber that fell into the Baltic Sea, where they can still be found along the shores of Palanga and Jūrmala.

Perkūnas, keeper of justice, was furious. He struck Mėnulis with his great axe, splitting the Moon's face. That is why the Moon waxes and wanes — he is forever healing from the blow, only to be struck again when he grows too bold.

Dievas decreed that Saulė and Mėnulis would never again share the sky at the same time. One rules the day, the other the night, and only at dawn and dusk do they briefly see each other across the horizon — a fleeting, sorrowful glance between two who were once one.

The Lithuanian mothers still tell their children: when you find amber on the beach, hold it to the light. You will see the golden tears of Saulė, and if you listen closely, the sea will hum the melody of the wedding that ended the first age of the world.
