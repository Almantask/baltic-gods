Before the plough, before the sickle, the people of the Baltic lands lived by gathering what the forests offered — mushrooms, berries, honey, and roots. They were grateful, but they were hungry, for the forests could not feed everyone.

Žemyna, the Earth Mother, watched her children struggle. She loved them as the soil loves the seed, and she resolved to teach them the secret of cultivation. She appeared to a woman named Birutė who was digging for roots near the river Neris.

"Daughter," Žemyna said, her voice like the sound of rain on leaves, "the earth will give you more than roots if you learn to ask properly."

She showed Birutė how to turn the soil, how to plant grain, and how to wait with patience through the seasons. But she gave one warning: "Before you break the earth each spring, you must kiss the ground and pour a libation of milk and honey. For I feel every cut of the plough, and only love makes the wound fruitful."

Birutė taught her village, and the village taught its neighbors, and soon the fields of Lithuania were golden with barley and rye. Each spring, the farmers would kneel and kiss the earth before the first furrow, whispering thanks to Žemyna.

But one generation grew careless. They ploughed without prayer, took without thanks, and the soil turned grey and barren. Žemyna withdrew her blessing, and famine crept across the land.

An elder remembered the old ways. She walked barefoot to the center of the largest field, knelt down, and pressed her lips to the cracked earth. She poured the last of her milk into the dust. The ground trembled, and green shoots broke through the grey crust.

From that day, no Baltic farmer forgets: the earth is alive, she listens, and she remembers who honors her.
