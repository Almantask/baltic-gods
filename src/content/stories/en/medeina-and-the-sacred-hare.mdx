In the age when Mindaugas was forming the first kingdom of the Lithuanians, the forests were not merely places of timber and game, but the living court of Medeina. She was the mistress of the hares, the one who rode upon them through the thickets of Anykščiai, her hair the color of autumn leaves.

Legend tells of a great hunt led by the king's men. They sought a prize for a royal feast, but every time they drew their bows, a white hare with ears as long as a man's arm would dart across their path. The hounds would howl and break formation, following the spectral creature deep into the marshes where the ground turned to treachery.

This was no ordinary animal, but Medeina's sentinel. The hare did not flee in fear; it danced in defiance, leading the hunters in circles until the sun dipped below the pines and the first stars—the eyes of the night—appeared in the firmament. 

The hunters returned empty-handed but filled with a new-found awe. They realized that some groves were not for the taking, and some creatures were protected by a bond older than any crown. To this day, in the deep woods of the Aukštaitija, hunters still pause when they see a hare at the edge of a clearing, honoring the silent boundary set by the goddess of the forest.
