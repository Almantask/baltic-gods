Among all the daughters of the farmer, Eglė was the youngest and the loveliest. One summer evening, when the girls went to bathe in the river, Eglė found a grass snake coiled in the sleeve of her linen shirt. She stepped back in alarm, but the snake spoke: "Marry me, Eglė," it said, "and I will not leave." She refused three times, but the snake would not go. The farmer consulted the elders, who said there was nothing to be done: what enters the shirt has entered the household.

So Eglė was wed to the grass snake, and he led her into the sea. Once beneath the waves, he shed his serpent skin and stood before her as Žilvinas, the King of Grass Snakes — a tall young man with dark eyes and a voice like river current. They lived in a palace of amber and coral, and Eglė bore him three sons and a daughter: Auzas, Ābelas, Ąžuolas, and Drebulė.

Years passed, and Eglė grew homesick. Žilvinas allowed her to visit her family on condition she return in nine days. But her brothers had conspired. They forced her sons to reveal Žilvinas's secret name, which he had hidden in a walnut, in a duck's egg, at the bottom of the sea. When the brothers found it, they called him to shore with the name and killed him. The sea ran red.

Eglė knew what had happened before she reached the shore. She cursed her children one by one — the treacherous ones became trees of their names: Auzas became an oat stalk, Ābelas an apple tree, Drebulė a trembling aspen. And Eglė herself, who had loved and lost and would grieve forever, became a spruce tree: straight, dark, and evergreen, mourning beside the sea where Žilvinas's palace lay scattered in amber shards along the shore.
