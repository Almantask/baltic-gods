In the beginning the sky was clean and the air was still. Then Velnias crept up from the underworld, wearing a cloak of borrowed cloud, and stole something that did not belong to him — accounts vary: a cow, the sun, a soul, a year's harvest. The theft was not the point. The point was that he had the insolence to rise into Perkūnas's domain and take.

Perkūnas woke to the absence and knew immediately who was responsible. He took his axe of iron, his belt of cloud, and began to hunt. Velnias fled across the sky in shapes that should have hidden him: first a cloud himself, then a man, then a bull, then a stone in a field. Perkūnas hurled lightning at every shape, and each one cracked or burned or scattered. Velnias dove into the earth.

He hid in a tree root. Perkūnas split the tree with thunder — which is why trees struck by lightning are holy; Perkūnas was hunting through them. Velnias slid into a stone. Perkūnas shattered the stone. Velnias hid in a man's house, then in the man himself. Perkūnas stood outside and hurled lightning at the man's heels, or at the threshold, or at the chimney smoke — accounts differ by village. The man was not permanently harmed: Perkūnas was surgical, not cruel.

In the end, Velnias retreated to the underworld, the stolen thing was returned or abandoned, and the rain fell — because every thunderstorm is that pursuit, and the rain is what remains when the hunt ends and equilibrium is restored. This is why after lightning strikes, the earth smells washed clean. Perkūnas has passed through and order has been renewed.
