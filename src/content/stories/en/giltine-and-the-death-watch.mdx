In the moonlit burial mounds of Salantai, where the ground is ancient and heavy with memory, the Samogitians once whispered of a woman so thin she cast no shadow. Giltinė, the sister of Laima, did not weave the threads of life; she held the silver shears that brought the loom to rest.

She appeared not as a terrifying specter, but as a silent watcher, her skin as pale as the birch bark in winter. Her presence was a cold wind through the cracks of a farmhouse, a sign that the threshold between the living and the ancestors was thinning.

A woodcutter once met her at a crossroads near the holy mounds. "Your fire is burnt down to the ash," she spoke, her voice like the dry rattle of leaves. The woodcutter, though afraid, bowed as one does to a forgotten queen. He understood that she was not an enemy, but a necessary shadow, the darkness that gave the sun its meaning.

To this day, in the villages of Samogitia, a single candle is kept in the window when a loved one is fading, a light so Giltinė might find the path without startling the household's soul. For the goddess of death is not a thief; she is the one who carries the tired home.
