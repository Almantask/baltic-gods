In the fertile fields of Kurzeme, the sky often turned a deep copper-red when the day was dying. It was a sign of the fire-drake's flight. Pūķis, the dragon of the hearth, was a creature of a thousand facets—the giver of golden grain and stolen silver, the fire that cooked the feast and burned the unwary house.

A prosperous farmer once made a covenant with the Pūķis. Each night, a bowl of hot porridge was left by the hearth, and a single lock of wool was placed at the farm's gate. In return, the farmer's barn was always full, his fields were the first to sprout, and the wheat was heavy with ears like coins.

But the Pūķis was not a servant; he was a guest with a temper. When the farmer's son once forgot the porridge, the dragon's scales sparked with a cold, green flame. The sky over the farm turned to smoke, a warning that the spirit of the home could just as easily be the spirit of ruin.

To this day, the people of the Latvian plains speak of the "Pūķa pēda"— the dragon's foot—the mark in the frost or the dust that shows where the protector has walked. For in the Baltic mind, fortune is never a gift from above; it is a shared breath between the hearth and the world beyond the walls.
