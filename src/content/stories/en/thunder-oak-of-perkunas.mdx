In the oldest days, when the sky was still young and the earth still remembered its own making, there stood upon a hill in Lithuania an oak so vast that its crown pierced the clouds. The people called it the Thunder Oak, for Perkūnas — god of storms, guardian of justice — had chosen it as his earthly throne.

Each spring, when the first thunder rolled across the Baltic plains, the elders would gather beneath its canopy. They brought offerings of bread and mead, laying them among the roots that coiled deep into the sacred earth. It was said that Perkūnas would descend along the trunk in a flash of lightning, striking the stone altar at the oak's base to purify the land from the deceits of Velnias.

One year, a terrible drought gripped the land. The rivers shrank to trickles, and the fields cracked under a merciless sun. The people begged Perkūnas for rain, but the sky remained silent. An old priestess named Eglė climbed the hill alone and pressed her forehead against the bark of the Thunder Oak.

She whispered the oldest prayer — one that had no words, only the rhythm of the wind and the heartbeat of the earth. The oak groaned, and from the highest branch a single acorn fell, splitting open upon the altar stone. Thunder cracked the sky, and rain poured down in silver sheets. The drought was broken.

From that day, the people carved a spiral into the altar stone — the mark of the thunder's descent — and no one felled an oak in that region again, for every oak was a lesser child of the Thunder Oak, and in each one, a sliver of Perkūnas watched over the land.
