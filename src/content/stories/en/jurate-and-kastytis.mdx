In the ancient days before memory, the Baltic Sea was governed by Jūratė the sea goddess, who lived in a palace of amber at the ocean's floor. Her halls were lit by the slow glow of golden walls, and the fish came to her court as subjects come to a queen. No mortal had ever seen the palace, because no mortal had ever been permitted to approach it.

Then came Kastytis, a young fisherman of the Curonian coast. He was not reckless or greedy, only skilled — his nets caught more than other men's, and the fish seemed to swim toward him. But his very success began to draw fish away from Jūratė's most carefully tended schools. She sent her sea spirits to warn him. He ignored them. She sent storms. He waited them out and returned. At last, curious and furious in equal measure, she rose to the surface herself to confront him.

Jūratė looked at the fisherman, and the fisherman looked at the goddess, and both fell silent. What happened next is told in a hundred amber-coloured words by the Curonian fishermen's wives: Jūratė fell in love. She was immortal, she was divine, and she was forbidden by Perkūnas from loving a mortal. But she brought Kastytis down to her amber palace and showed him the floor of the sea.

Perkūnas learned of it. His thunder split the sea's surface and his lightning struck the amber palace, shattering it into ten thousand pieces along the Baltic shore. Kastytis was drowned, chained to a rock in the deep water. Jūratė survived but was condemned to weep forever on the ruins of what she had loved. And the amber that washes up on the shores of Lithuania and Latvia to this day — the resinous golden stones that children collect at the tideline — those are the broken walls of Jūratė's palace, and each piece is soaked in the tears she has shed across the ages.
