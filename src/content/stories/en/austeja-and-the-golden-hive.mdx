In the deep forests of Dzūkija, where the pines grow so tall they seem to hold up the sky, there lived a beekeeper named Rūta. She kept thirty hives, and her honey was the sweetest in all the land — golden as sunrise, fragrant as linden blossoms.

But Rūta was greedy. She took too much honey and left too little for the bees to survive the winter. Year after year, her hives dwindled, and the bees grew weak.

Austėja, goddess of bees and patron of beekeepers, appeared to Rūta in a dream. She came in the form of a queen bee the size of a woman, her wings shimmering with the light of a thousand candles.

"You take from my children as a thief takes from the unwary," Austėja said. "The hive is a covenant, not a treasury. You must give as much as you take."

Rūta awoke ashamed. That spring, she planted wildflowers around every hive — clover, thyme, and meadowsweet. She left twice as much honey as before and sang to the bees each morning, as the old grandmothers had done.

By midsummer, her thirty hives had become sixty. The bees were strong and vigorous, and the honey they produced was so remarkable that people said it could heal fevers and mend broken hearts.

At the Joninės festival, Rūta placed a bowl of her finest honey at the forest shrine and thanked Austėja. That night, a column of golden light rose from the hives and danced among the treetops — the goddess acknowledging the restored bond.

To this day, Lithuanian beekeepers greet their hives each morning with a kind word, for Austėja listens through the hum of every bee.
