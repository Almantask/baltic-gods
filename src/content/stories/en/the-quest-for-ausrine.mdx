In the ancient Baltic worldview, the heavens were not empty space, but a parallel geography mirroring the earth, separated only by a great sea. One of the most enigmatic tales from this mythic space—extensively analyzed by the semiotician Algirdas Julien Greimas—describes a protagonist, sometimes called Joseph in later syncretic folklore, who sets out to find a "second sun". The sky was perceived to be missing a crucial source of light, and the young hero began a long, arduous journey across the mythical sea to find it.

After traversing the watery expanse, he reaches a distant island (often described as a glass mountain or a mansion beyond the sea). Here, instead of a burning orb, he finds Aušrinė—the divine Morning Star maiden. She possesses radiant beauty and glowing hair that illuminates the surrounding realm. This discovery reveals the "double origin" of the myth: the second sun is not a celestial object in the ordinary sense, but a living goddess whose presence brings the dawn and prepares the way for Saulė (the Sun).

The tale also introduces Tarnaitis, the devoted servant (associated with the planet Mercury), who aids Aušrinė. This narrative is more than a fairy tale; Greimas identified it as a structural myth reconciling the transition from darkness to light. It explains how Aušrinė ascended to the sky to become the Morning Star, forever spinning the threads of light that pull the sun's chariot over the horizon and bringing hope, youth, and renewal to the world each morning.
