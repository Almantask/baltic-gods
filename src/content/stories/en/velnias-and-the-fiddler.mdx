Velnias, the lord of the underworld and master of trickery, loved music above all things. On moonless nights he would emerge from the marshes near Kernavė, his eyes gleaming like foxfire, and listen to the songs the farmhands sang in their barns.

One autumn, a fiddler named Jonas traveled the roads of Samogitia, playing at weddings and fairs. His music was so sweet that birds would land on his bow arm, and even the pines would lean closer to listen. Word of his skill reached Velnias, who appeared at a crossroads in the guise of a well-dressed gentleman.

"Play me a tune," Velnias said, "and if your music moves me to tears, I will give you a pouch of silver. But if it does not, you must play for me in the marsh for seven years."

Jonas was clever. He knew the old stories. He played not a mournful song but a dance tune — the kind that makes the feet stamp and the heart race. Velnias began to dance. He could not stop, for the rhythm was woven with the pattern of running water, and Velnias, being a creature of still pools and stagnant bogs, had no power over moving streams.

Velnias danced until his fine boots wore through and his gentleman's coat tore at the seams. When dawn broke and the rooster crowed, Velnias vanished with a howl, leaving behind the pouch of silver — and a scorched ring in the earth where he had danced.

Jonas picked up the silver and walked on, and ever after he played a quick reel at every crossroads he passed, just to keep the old trickster honest.
