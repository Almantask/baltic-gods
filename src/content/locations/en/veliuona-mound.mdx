Veliuona sits on the high right bank of the Nemunas River in central Lithuania, its name an almost transparent window into its sacred function: "vėlės" in Lithuanian are the souls of the dead, and Veliuona was their place — a hill where the boundary between the living world and the realm of ancestors grew permeable at the right seasons.

The mound at Veliuona was a site of the autumn festival of Vėlinės, the Lithuanian feast of the dead (related to the Latvian Veļi and ancestral traditions across the Baltic world). On these nights, the families of the community gathered at the mound to share a meal with their departed relatives, setting out food and drink on specially prepared tables, speaking the names of the dead, and listening for signs of their presence in the wind and the crackling of the fire.

The connection between Veliuona and Velnias, the god of the underworld, was structural rather than incidental. Velnias ruled the realm beneath the earth where the souls of the dead gathered, and the mound at Veliuona was understood as a vertical axis connecting the living community above with the ancestral realm below. The roots of the trees on the mound were thought to reach down to where the vėlės dwelled.

Archaeological excavations have confirmed that Veliuona and its surroundings were used for cremation burials spanning the first millennium CE. The ash and bone of the community's dead were returned to this hill through many generations, accumulating a density of ancestral presence that was itself a sacred resource — a concentration of protective power that the living could draw upon through the right ritual approaches.
