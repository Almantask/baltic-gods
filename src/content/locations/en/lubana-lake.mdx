Lake Lubāns is the largest lake in Latvia — a vast, shallow body of water set in the low wetlands of Latgale, surrounded by reed beds and migratory bird routes. In Latvian folk tradition, large lakes were considered the eyes of the earth, places where the sky and the deep communicated and where the boundary between worlds grew thin in certain seasons.

Before setting out to fish, Latgalian fishermen addressed the lake with formulaic words that were invocations as much as requests. The "lake mother" (ezera māte) was a powerful spirit who could calm or agitate the surface, and her goodwill was courted with offerings of the first catch, bread, and beer left at the water's edge.

On Midsummer night, fires were lit on the ridges of the marshy land surrounding the lake, and young people leapt through the flames before wading into the shallow water at dawn to wash away illness and bad fortune. The reed beds along the shore were gathering places for women performing rites of purification, and the floating pollen of reed grasses in spring was associated with the dispersal of fate's blessings.

Lubāns was also a landscape of the dead. In Latgalian tradition, the souls of the departed journeyed to a watery afterlife, and large lakes were considered the gathering places of ancestral souls. Offerings of food and drink were left at the lake shore during Veļi (the Latvian festival of the dead), and the mist that rose from the water on autumn mornings was understood as the breath of those who had gone before.
