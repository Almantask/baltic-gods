# Grobiņa Archeological Site: Seeburg and the Curonian Kings

**Grobiņa**, or **Seeburg** as the Vikings named it, is one of the most remarkable archaeological landscapes in the eastern Baltic. Located in western **Latvia** (Kurzeme), it is a UNESCO-listed testimony to the symbiosis between the **Curonians** (Kurši) and the **Scandinavian** (Norse-Viking) traders and warriors.

## The Ship-Setting Burials

A highlight of the site is the **Priediens** cemetery, where unique **ship-setting** burials (stones arranged in the shape of a ship) are found—a ritual typically Norse, yet here shared with Curonian ancestors. This synthesis of ritual death-paths points to a deep, maritime spiritual connection.

## The Spirit of the Amber Sea

Grobiņa was a center for **amber** trade and craftsmanship. The Curonians, known as the "vultures of the sea," were fierce sailors and practitioners of the **maritime cults** of the Baltic:
- **Sea Spirits**: Sacred offerings made at the shore to ensure calm voyages.
- **Amber Rituals**: Small carved amber figures were used to ward off the **Velnias** (Devil/Spirits) during the winter months.
- **Hill Fort Defense**: The Grobiņa hill fort was a spiritual and military bastion that stood strong for centuries against the expansion of the northern crusades.

## Historical Legacy

Mentioned in the **Life of Ansgar** (9th century), Grobiņa is a rare point where the written word and the archaeological record of the "pagan North" meet.
