Before the Catholic Basilica of Aglona rose on this Latgalian hill, the site was already ancient and sacred. The hill commands a view over the surrounding lake district, and its spring-fed waters were considered to carry healing and prophetic powers long before any Christian missionaries arrived. Ethnographic records document ribbon-tying, night vigils, and ritual washing at the spring that ethnologists identify as survivals of pre-Christian Latvian spiritual practice.

The sacred spring at the base of the hill is the most tangible remnant of the older cult. Women brought sick children to bathe in its waters on midsummer morning, repeating words that were not prayers to Christian saints but invocations of the fate goddess Laima. The ribbons tied to the nearby birch branches were offerings — material threads of intention left for the goddess to weave into the fabric of a family's fortune.

The Latgalian landscape around Aglona is dotted with sacred hills, springs, and stone circles that predate Christianity. The area was one of the last in Latvia to accept the new faith, and the Latgalians blended the old and new with particular skill, preserving water cult practices under a thin Christian veneer for generations.

Today the Basilica of the Assumption draws pilgrims from across Latvia and Lithuania, especially for the Feast of the Assumption on August 15. Yet on the eve of the feast, when the crowds have gone to sleep, the old spring at the hill's base still glitters in moonlight — an older sacred presence that the centuries have not erased.
