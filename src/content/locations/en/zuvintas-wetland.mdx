Lake Žuvintas and the surrounding wetlands in Dzūkija form one of the most important bird habitats in Lithuania — a vast expanse of shallow water, reed beds, and bog mosses that turns amber-gold in autumn and silver-grey in winter fog. In Lithuanian folk tradition, this landscape was not simply natural wilderness but a living sacred space, a place where Laima, the goddess of fate and fortune, made herself visible through the flight patterns of birds.

The Dzūkian tradition of reading bird augury was particularly rich in this region. The arrival of specific migratory species in spring was carefully observed: the call of the first cuckoo of the year was the most important omen (the number of calls predicting the years remaining to the listener), but the flight of cranes, the song of the reed warbler, and the hovering of marsh harriers were all readable as messages from the invisible powers that governed human fate.

Women gathered at the lake shore in spring to observe the returning birds, performing rites of greeting that included song, the offering of bread crumbs on the water surface, and the reading of the patterns of ripples that a bird's landing created. These were understood as letters from Laima, written in the medium of water and wing.

The bog water of Žuvintas is naturally amber-colored from the tannins of decomposing plant matter — a quality that folk tradition associated with sacred significance. Water that looked like liquid amber was considered particularly powerful, charged with the essence of the sun goddess Saulė's tears, and collecting such water for healing purposes was a traditional practice. The wetland's isolation and its surreal visual character made it a site of persistent veneration that outlasted the official religious changes of the 16th century.
