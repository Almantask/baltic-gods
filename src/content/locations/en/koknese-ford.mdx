Koknese stands on a dramatic promontory at the confluence of the Perse stream and the Daugava River, its red sandstone cliffs rising sheer from the water below. Long before the Livonian Order built their castle here in the 13th century, this strategic high point was a Semigallian and Selonian sacred site — a natural altar overlooking the most important river in the Baltic interior.

The ford below the promontory was a sacred boundary as much as a practical crossing. Travelers who crossed here were moving from one territory to another, from one spiritual jurisdiction to another, and the crossing demanded acknowledgment of the powers that governed the waters. Small offerings — a handful of grain, a piece of bread, a drop of mead — were cast into the river before wading across, addresses to the river deity who controlled whether the crossing would be safe or treacherous.

On the high promontory itself, seasonal fire festivals were held at midsummer and winter solstice. The elevated position meant the fires could be seen across a wide landscape, signaling the turning of the solar year and gathering communities from a broad radius for the ceremonial season. Perkūnas, the thunder god, was the principal deity honored on the cliff top, appropriate to the dramatic vertical geography of the site.

The sandstone cliffs at Koknese are naturally riddled with small caves and niches, which served as offering repositories. Archaeological finds from the 19th century included embedded bronze ornaments, coins, and carved wooden figures pressed into these natural recesses — centuries of accumulated petitions to the powers of rock, river, and sky that converge at this striking promontory.
