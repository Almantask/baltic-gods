At Bauska, the Mūsa and Mēmele rivers flow together to form the Lielupe — the "Great River" that winds westward to the sea. The Semigallian word for this confluence site had ritual significance: it was a place where two living streams became one, a visible enactment of fate's binding that the tribes associated with the weaving work of Laima.

River confluences were among the most sacred geographical features in Baltic religion, representing boundaries, transitions, and the merging of different spiritual streams. The Semigallians who once controlled this landscape performed seasonal sacrifice rites at the water's edge, throwing offerings into the swirling confluence and reading omens in the mixing of currents.

Archaeological excavations in and around Bauska have recovered pre-Christian ritual deposits: amber beads, small bronze ornaments, and animal bones that represent the material residue of these offerings. The hilltop castle built by the Livonian Order in the 13th century sits precisely on the site of an earlier hill fort, a pattern typical of Christianization — sacred high places were seized and repurposed before the surrounding population could resist.

In Semigallian folk tradition, the confluence was the place where fate threads of different origin were joined — a liminal geography that corresponded to the moments of life when separate paths crossed and bound together. Marriage ceremonies in the region historically included a ritual walk to the nearest river confluence, echoing this older cosmology of flowing fates.
