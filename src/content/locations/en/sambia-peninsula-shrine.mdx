The Sambian Peninsula juts into the Baltic Sea like a curved thumb, and along its western and northern coast the sea washes up amber — the "gold of the north" — in quantities unmatched anywhere else on earth. For Baltic peoples, this peninsula was not merely geography: it was the sacred shoreline where Saulė, the sun goddess, wept her crystallized tears, and where the sea returned her gifts to the land each time the waves receded.

Amber was the most sacred material in Baltic religion. Unlike gold or silver, which were dug from the ground or worked by human hands, amber was self-offered — the sea brought it to the shore unbidden, depositing it in the wrack line after storms, a spontaneous gift from divine powers. Priests of the Sambian shore collected the best pieces for ceremonial use, and a portion of every collection was returned to the sea or buried in coastal sanctuaries as an acknowledgment of the gift's divine origin.

The sacred sites along the Sambian coast were not grand stone structures but natural features: particular beaches where amber washed up most abundantly, cliffs where the amber-bearing seams of blue clay were exposed, springs where fresh water met salt water in liminal combination. At these points, seasonal ceremonies were performed that mixed practical amber collection with religious acknowledgment — a harvest of sacred material conducted with the same ritual attention as a grain harvest on land.

The Teutonic Order's conquest of Prussia in the 13th century brought systematic destruction of these coastal sanctuaries and the expropriation of the amber trade by the Order and its allies. The amber monopoly that the Order maintained for centuries was not merely commercial exploitation but a deliberate severing of the religious economy that had sustained Baltic spiritual life on this shore for millennia. Yet the sea continued to deliver its amber, indifferent to human history, each storm restoring to the beach the sacred material that no conquest could ultimately control.
