Lake Burtnieks in northern Latvia carries in its very name the mark of its sacred purpose. The Latvian word "burvs" (wizard, seer) and related forms point to an association with prophecy and divination that was not forgotten even when the lake's cult faded. The name preserved the memory of wise people — burtnīcas — who gathered at its shores to divine the future in the patterns of water, wind, and waterfowl.

The lake sits in a bowl of gentle hills, its surface often mist-covered in the morning hours when the most significant divination was performed. Women would wade into the shallows before sunrise, speaking to the water in a low voice, reading the ripples and eddies for signs of the coming year's fortune. The specific questions — will this marriage be fruitful? will this child survive? will the harvest come? — were the perennial human questions that the oracular landscape was asked to answer.

On Midsummer morning, the lake shore was a gathering place for a different kind of divination: the reading of flower wreaths floated on the water. Each girl placed a wreath of seasonal wildflowers on the surface and watched which direction it drifted, which bank it reached, whether it stayed whole or fell apart — each movement readable as a sign of romantic fate in the coming year.

The surrounding northern landscape of gently rolling farmland and scattered groves was also rich in sacred stones and springs, and Burtnieks formed the centerpiece of a wider ceremonial geography. Its waters, fed by underground sources, maintained a constant temperature that was itself considered significant — the earth's own steady breath, speaking beneath the changing seasons.
