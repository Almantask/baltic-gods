Honeda was a sacred forest sanctuary of the Galindian Old Prussians, recorded in 13th-century Teutonic chronicles as a significant site of pagan worship in the territory south of the main Prussian heartland. The Galindians were among the southernmost Baltic tribes, neighbors to the Mazovian Slavs, and their sacred landscape combined the characteristic Baltic alka traditions with local geographical features of the Masurian lake district.

The Teutonic chronicler Peter of Dusburg described Honeda as a grove with a perpetual sacred fire, tended by hereditary priests who served the deity Curcho — a Prussian god associated with grain, harvest, and the turning of the agricultural year. The fire at Honeda was not merely symbolic: it was a living entity with its own requirements, fed with specific types of wood at specific times, tended with precise protocols that were as much spiritual discipline as practical fire-keeping.

Tribal assemblies at Honeda were seasonal events that combined religious ceremony with political deliberation. The Galindian chiefs gathered under the sacred trees to settle disputes, conclude alliances, and perform the collective rites that renewed the community's relationship with the divine powers governing their land. The same space that was a sanctuary was also a parliament and a market — a concentration of community life in a designated sacred geography.

When the Teutonic Knights moved against the Galindians in the 13th century, destroying Honeda was part of the conquest's religious program. The grove was burned, the fire extinguished, and the priests killed or dispersed. What remained in the oral memory of neighboring populations were fragments: the name of the place, the memory of fire, the knowledge that something irreplaceable had stood here — a node in the network of sacred groves that had once organized Baltic spiritual and political life across a vast territory.
