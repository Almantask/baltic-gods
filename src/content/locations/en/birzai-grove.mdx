The karst landscape around Biržai in northern Lithuania is unlike any other terrain in the Baltic region. The ground here is riddled with sinkholes — sudden depressions where the limestone bedrock has dissolved and the earth has collapsed inward, creating bowl-shaped openings that look like the ground breathing or sighing. To the ancient Lithuanians, these were not geological curiosities; they were portals.

The sinkholes were understood as entrances to the underworld realm of Velnias — places where the boundary between the living surface world and the realm of the dead became physical and navigable. Offerings of dairy products, grain, and small figurines were deposited into the sinkholes as petitions to the dead, messages sent down through the portal to the ancestral realm below. In return, the dead were expected to send up their protection and their accumulated wisdom.

The sacred birch groves that grew around many of these sinkholes were boundary markers in the ritual landscape — the white-barked trees serving as pillars between worlds, their pale trunks visible even at night, their rustling leaves the voice of spirits. Birch was the tree of spiritual transition in Baltic tradition, associated with the souls of the dead and with the liminal periods of the calendar when the living and the dead came closest.

The town of Biržai itself became a center of Protestant Reformation activity in the 16th century, and the old sacred landscape was overlaid with new religious associations. Yet the sinkholes continued to attract folk attention — people threw offerings into them for generations after Christianization, and local stories about underground voices and strange lights persisted in the oral record well into the modern era.
