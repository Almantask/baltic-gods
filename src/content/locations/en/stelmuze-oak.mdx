The Stelmuže Oak stands in a small village in Zarasai district, Aukštaitija — a living colossus estimated to be over 1,500 years old, making it one of the oldest living trees in Europe. Its gnarled trunk is more than three meters in diameter, and its great canopy has sheltered the same ground through the Migration Period, the pagan Lithuanian era, the Christian conversion, and into the present day.

In Baltic religious tradition, ancient oaks were the primary dwelling places of Perkūnas, the thunder god. The Stelmuže Oak was no ordinary tree: it was surrounded by a ring of smaller sacred trees forming an alka — a natural grove sanctuary. Priests and wise men would gather here during storms to read the lightning's path and divine the god's will.

Offerings of iron nails, grain, and cloth were left at the roots of the tree. The iron was particularly significant, as it was the metal most associated with Perkūnas — thunderbolts were sometimes called "Perkūnas's iron." Pilgrims would touch the bark and whisper requests for protection from storms, for good harvests, and for justice in disputes.

Despite centuries of Christian pressure, the oak was never felled. Local communities continued to treat it as sacred, gathering under its canopy for unnamed seasonal observances. It was formally designated a Lithuanian state nature monument, and today it remains a place of quiet pilgrimage for those who still feel the living connection between the oldest trees and the oldest powers of the sky.
