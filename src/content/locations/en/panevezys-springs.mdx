The Nevėžis River valley at Panevėžys in north-central Lithuania is a landscape of multiple spring sources, where underground waters emerge from limestone strata to feed the river and the smaller streams branching from it. In Lithuanian folk tradition, spring water that emerged from the earth spontaneously — unbidden, self-offered — was the most sacred of waters, carrying the blessing of Laima directly from her underground realm.

Different springs in the Panevėžys area were associated with different specific powers. Healers and wise women maintained detailed knowledge of which spring's water was best for treating which illness, which spring's current ran in the right direction for divination, which spring's water was suitable for the ritual washing of newborns. This was not random folk belief but a practical geography of sacred power, mapping the landscape's spiritual resources as carefully as its agricultural ones.

The major calendar festivals brought women to specific springs in organized groups. On the eve of Jorė (the spring festival honoring the earth's awakening), young women gathered at the springs to wash their faces in the first light of dawn and speak the names of the fate spirits over the water. On the morning of Rasos (the midsummer water festival), the same springs were sites of more elaborate rites, with the water collected in special vessels for year-round use in healing and protection.

The association of these springs with Laima, the fate goddess, reflected a theology in which fate was not an abstract force but a flowing, material presence — something that moved through the world in water, that could be invoked at its source, and that could be redirected or gently petitioned by those who knew the right approaches. The Panevėžys springs were nodes in this flowing theology, places where the fate waters were closest to the surface.
