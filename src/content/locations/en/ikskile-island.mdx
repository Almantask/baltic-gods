The island at Ikšķile on the Daugava River is among the most contested sacred spaces in Latvian history. For centuries before German missionaries arrived, this natural promontory in the river was a Livonian and Latvian ritual site — an island bounded by sacred water on all sides, a natural sanctuary set apart from the surrounding world by the river's flowing boundary.

The German Bishop Meinhard chose this island for the first stone church in Latvia, built in 1185, a choice that was not accidental. Missionaries consistently targeted pre-existing sacred sites for their building programs, understanding that the spiritual resonance of a place was transferable — that communities who had gathered at the island for generations would continue to do so. The church was built to redirect that gathering toward the new faith.

What the chronicles do not record, but archaeology preserves, is the material evidence of what came before: offerings of pottery, weapons, and ornaments recovered from the riverbed around the island, deposits left by worshippers addressing the river deity, the water spirits, and the high god Dievas who was believed to look down from the sky upon the shining surface of the great river.

Today the island is partially submerged beneath the Ķegums reservoir, its ancient church ruins visible above waterline as a haunting reminder of layered sacred history. The sunken landscape beneath the water holds the accumulated spiritual deposits of Livonian paganism, the remains of the first Christian mission, and the silence of centuries — a flooded sacred geography that speaks precisely because it can no longer be heard.
