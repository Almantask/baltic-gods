The name Medininkai means "place of the forest people" in Lithuanian — from "medis," meaning tree or forest. Before a medieval castle was built here in the 14th century, the landscape around Medininkai near Vilnius was a sacred forest zone, the eastern edge of the great woodlands that Medeina, the forest goddess, was said to govern.

In Lithuanian cosmology, the forest was not merely a natural feature but a spiritual territory with its own rules, powers, and guardians. Hunters entering the sacred woodland performed propitiation rites before the trees, addressing Medeina and asking permission to take game from her domain. The rituals were practical acknowledgments of dependence on the forest's living resources.

The forest surrounding Medininkai was among the last remnants of the old sacred woodlands that covered much of pre-Christian Lithuania. Its particular sanctity came from age and isolation — these were trees that had never been cut, that had witnessed the movement of the great Lithuanian pagan kingdom, that had sheltered the ritual gatherings of communities who understood the forest as a living being with memory and preference.

The castle built by the Grand Duchy of Lithuania in the 14th century represents the transition — the sacred forest gradually becoming managed territory, the wild woodland boundary pushed back as settlement expanded. Yet the name "Medininkai" endured, as did the folk memory of the forest's special character. Even in later centuries, local people spoke of unusual encounters in these woods — lights among the trees at night, animals behaving strangely, a sense of being watched that was not threatening but attentive, as if the old guardian still dwelt among the ancient trunks.
