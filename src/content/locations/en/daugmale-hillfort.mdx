Daugmale, on the lower Daugava River just upstream from where Riga would later be founded, was one of the most important gathering sites in pre-Christian Latvian life. Its hilltop position gave commanding views of the river and the surrounding flat landscape, making it both a defensive vantage point and a natural focal site for the scattered communities of the region.

Archaeological excavations at Daugmale have revealed the material remains of a site that functioned simultaneously as a market, a ceremonial center, and a political meeting place. Amber artifacts — including worked beads and raw nodules — testify to the amber trade that passed through this point on its journey from the coast to distant markets. But the ritual deposits of animal bones, vessels, and ornaments speak of a different kind of exchange: gifts offered to the invisible powers that governed trade, weather, and the fertility of the land.

The community that gathered at Daugmale in the seasonal assemblies operated under the supervision of priests (vaidilai) who maintained the sacred fires and conducted the ceremonies. Communal feasts followed the major seasonal ceremonies, and the smoke of the fires at Daugmale could be seen across the flat Daugava plain for many kilometers, signaling the times of gathering to communities throughout the region.

When the Teutonic and Livonian Orders began their conquest of the Baltic in the 13th century, Daugmale was one of the centers of resistance — not merely because it was a well-positioned fort, but because it was a sacred place whose defense had religious as well as military meaning. Its eventual fall represented both a military defeat and a spiritual disruption of the ceremonial geography that had organized Baltic life in the lower Daugava basin for centuries.
