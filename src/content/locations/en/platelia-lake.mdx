Lake Plateliai is the largest lake in Samogitia and one of the most sacred bodies of water in Lithuanian folk belief. Stretching across the Žemaitija National Park, its still waters reflect the sky with a depth that local legend interprets as a window into another world. Beneath its surface, so the story goes, lies a sunken city whose church bells still ring on quiet nights.

The lake has been a site of midsummer raft ceremonies since pre-Christian times. On the eve of Joninės (St. John's Eve), young people floated garland-laden rafts out onto the water, their candles reading the currents as messages from fate spirits. Women waded in at dawn to perform ablutions and divine the fates of new marriages.

The small island known as Pilies Sala — Castle Island — served as a refuge during the Teutonic crusades and is believed to have been an earlier cult center. Its isolation from the shore made it a natural liminal space between the mortal world and the realm of water spirits. Offerings of amber, grain, and wreaths were deposited here to honor Žemyna and the lake spirits.

Samogitian resistance to Christianization was especially fierce, and Lake Plateliai remained a center of folk religious practice centuries after the official conversion. Local women continued to venerate the lake as a living entity, greeting it each spring with ritual songs and offering bread crumbs upon its surface as thanks for winter survival.
