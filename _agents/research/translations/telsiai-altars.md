# Telšiai Clay Altars / Telšių Molio Aukurai / Telšu Māla Altāri

## Comprehensive Overview

The **Telšiai Clay Altars** are low clay platforms (*aukurai*) hidden in hazel groves near Telšiai, Samogitia, western Lithuania. They are sites of grain offering to **Žemyna**, specifically for the harvest season. The altars are re-formed each harvest from local clay — an act that is itself a ritual of earth-offering: the altar is made *from* the earth it addresses. The practice of pressing footprints into fresh clay as a purity test, followed by embedding household embers into the clay to "warm the soil," reflects a domestication of the great earth-goddess rite into a community-level harvest observance. Rye crowns left to dry as pledges for the next harvest complete the seasonal cycle. Samogitia is historically marked by tenacious pre-Christian practice, and the hazel grove — a smaller sacred enclosure — is a characteristic Samogitian adaptation of the Lithuanian *alka* tradition.

### English (EN)
**Žemyna's** harvest connection was not merely symbolic: she was the living body of the soil that produced grain, and the offering of grain back to her — as rye crowns and flour prints — was a literal return of surplus to source. The Telšiai altars were re-moulded each year from fresh clay: participants kneaded the clay with their hands and shaped it into flat, knee-height pads. Grain flour was spread over the wet surface so that every footprint left by approachers was recorded — only those with clean soles could leave votive wreaths; muddy or blurred prints were swept away to remove spiritual confusion from the offering. Embers from household stoves were pressed into the cooled clay to transfer Žemyna's indoor and outdoor manifestations: Gabija (the hearth fire) and Žemyna (the earth) were here briefly united in the kiln-hardened altar. The resulting fired bricks were broken after the season and mixed into the fields, returning the altar itself to the earth as a second-order offering. Hazel groves were specifically chosen because the hazel was associated with boundaries and thresholds — suitable for a ritual that moved grain from field to table to earth.

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Žemynos** ryšys su derliumi nebuvo vien simbolinis: ji buvo gyvasis grūdą gaminančio dirvožemio kūnas, ir grūdų grąžinimas jai atgal — kaip rugių vainikai ir miltų atspaudai — buvo tiesioginis pertekliaus grąžinimas šaltiniui. Telšių aukurai kasmet buvo perrengti iš šviežios molio: dalyviai minkydavo molį rankomis ir formuodavo į plokščius, kelio aukščio padėklus. Miltai buvo tepami ant drėgnos paviršiaus, kad kiekvienas prisiartinusio pėdsakas būtų įrašytas — tik tie su švariais padais galėjo palikti votyvines vainikus. Žarijos iš namų krosnelių buvo spaudžiamos į atvėsusį molį Žemynos vidaus ir lauko apraiškoms perteikti. Gautos iškeptos plytelos buvo sudauzytos po sezono ir sumaišytos į laukus.

### Latvian (LV)
**Žemynas** saikne ar ražu nebija tikai simboliska: viņa bija graudu ražojošās augsnes dzīvais ķermenis, un graudu atdošana viņai atpakaļ — kā rudzu vainagi un miltu nospiedumi — bija tiešs pārpalikuma atgriešana pie avota. Telšu altāri katru gadu tika pārveidoti no svaigas māla: dalībnieki mīca mālu ar rokām un veidoja plakanas, ceļgala augstuma paliktņus. Graudu milti tika smidzināti uz mitras virsmas, lai reģistrētu katru tuvojoša cilvēka pēdas nospiedumu — tikai tie ar tīrām zolēm varēja atstāt votīvās vītnes. Ogles no mājas krāsnīm tika iespiesti atdzisušajā mālā Žemynas iekštelpu un āra izpausmju pārsūtīšanai. Iegūtie apdedzinātie ķieģeļi pēc sezonas tika sasisti un sajaukti laukos.

## Primary Source Details

- **Łasicki (Łas):** Documents Samogitian grain offerings to *Žemyna* and the use of clay objects in harvest thanksgiving rites. [Jan Łasicki, *De Diis Samagitarum*, 1615]
- **Dundulienė (Dund):** Studies the ecology of Lithuanian sacred enclosures, including hazel grove (*lazdynas*) sites as boundary-threshold cult spaces. [Pranė Dundulienė, *Medis senovės lietuvių tikėjimuose*, 1979]
- **Vėlius (Vel):** Compiles folk accounts of clay altar construction and its role in Žemyna's seasonal harvest dialogue. [Norbertas Vėlius, *Baltų religijos ir mitologijos šaltiniai*, Vol. 1, 1996]
- **Jesuit mission reports (Jes):** 17th-century Jesuit accounts of persisting harvest rituals in Samogitian villages, including clay altar use. [Lithuanian Jesuit missions, 1569–1650]
- **Beresnevičius (Ber):** Analyses the transition of outdoor earth-goddess rites into hazel-grove enclosures in Samogitia. [Gintaras Beresnevičius, *Lietuvių religija ir mitologija*, 2004]
