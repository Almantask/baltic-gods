# Maro deivė / Marų deivės / Mēra māte

## Comprehensive Overview

"Maro deivė" (The Plague Goddess) is a specialized and formidable deity associated with epidemics and mass death. While distinct from the general goddess of death (**Giltinė**), she embodies the sudden and collective transition to the afterlife during times of disease.

### English (EN)
**Maro deivė** (plural: *Maro deivės*) is the personification of the plague and other catastrophic illnesses. In Baltic folklore, she is often depicted as a tall, thin woman dressed in white, sometimes riding a white horse or flying through the air waving a red cloth. Where she waves her cloth, the plague strikes. In historical accounts and folk tales, the arrival of the Plague Goddess was a time of extreme terror. Communities would bar their doors and pray to **Perkūnas** or **Žemyna** for protection. In West Prussia, a similar figure named **Diedewaythe** (Grandmother-Goddess) was invoked by the plague-stricken. Unlike **Giltinė**, who takes individuals one by one, Maro deivė represents the massive, sweeping power of fate that affects entire villages at once.

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Maro deivė** lietuvių mitologijoje yra mirtį ir epidemijas nešanti būtybė. Ji vaizduojama kaip aukšta, lyg mirtis baltai vilkinti moteris, kuri keliauja iš kaimo į kaimą, darydama ženklus ant durų ar modama raudona skara. Toje vietoje kyla maras. XVI a. prūsų šaltiniuose (pvz., Mato Pretorijaus raštuose) minima **Diedewaythe** (Diedievaitė), kaip viena iš sargybinių deivių, kuri galėjo atsiųsti ar sustabdyti marą. **Gintaras Beresnevičius** nurodo, kad ši deivė yra specifiška Giltinės atmaina, veikianti kolektyviniu lygmeniu. Sakmėse sako, kad Maro deivės bijo net patys drąsiausi vyrai, nes jos kelyje niekas negali atsilaikyti.

### Latvian (LV)
**Mēra māte** latviešu folklorā ir baisa un neizbēgama dabas dusa. Viņa ceļo naktīs, tērptas baltā drānā, un, kur viņa apstājas, tur sēta un ciems izmirst. Mēra māte personificē epdidēmiju nepielūdzamo dabu un nāvi, kas nāk nevis no vecuma, bet gan no slimības. Dainās viņa tiek lūgta apiet māju, un sētās tika veikti dažādi rituāli (rakšana, uguns kūrtuve), lai viņu aizbiedētu. Viņas tēls ir atgādinājums par cilvēka trauslumu un dabas briesmīgo pusi, kas var iznīcināt visu radību vienā mirklī. Viņa ir bargā tiesnese, kurai neviens nevar izbēgt.

## Primary Source Details

- **Beresnevičius (Ber):** Analyzes the role of collective death deities and their iconographic overlap with the Fates. [Gintaras Beresnevičius, 1995]
- **Praetorius (Prae):** Records the name *Diedewaythe* associated with the plague in Old Prussia. [Matthäus Prätorius, 17th c.]
- **VLE:** Describes Maro deivė as an epidemic-personifying figure and its folk depictions.
- **Folklore (Bas):** Records dozens of legends of "Plague visits" and how individuals tried to outsmart the goddess by hiding in chimneys or wells.

## Key Metadata
- **Region**: Lithuania, Latvia, Old Prussia
- **Tribe**: All Baltic tribes
- **Period**: Medieval to 20th-century folklore
- **Category**: Death Deity / Epidemic Personification
- **Symbol**: Red Cloth, White Robe, Bell (Death Knell), Red Scythe
- **Animals**: Pale Horse, Crow, Bat
- **Function**: Delivering epidemics, Collective death, Testing community resilience
- **Etymology**: *mar- (to die/plague)
