# Kaunas Hearth Archive / Kauno Židinio Archyvas / Kauņas Pavarda Arhīvs

## Comprehensive Overview

The **Kaunas Hearth Archive** is an old brick kiln in Kaunas, Lithuania's second city, where communal embers from multiple households are kept together during severe storms. The site is dedicated to **Gabija**, the sacred Lithuanian hearth fire, who is both a divine entity and the protector of each individual household. Gabija was not merely the fire in the hearth — she was the soul of the home, the living warrant of family continuity, and the guardian against harm. When lightning threatened individual home fires, bringing coals to the communal kiln was a way to ensure Gabija's continuity: even if a private hearth went dark in a storm, the shared flame preserved her presence. The kiln's permanent heat made it a reliable guardian-space, and the ritual practice of sifting ash for "bright flecks" — reintegrating them into new firewood bundles — transferred protective energy back into the household.

### English (EN)
**Gabija** was perpetually maintained: a fire allowed to go out entirely was a domestic catastrophe, a sign that the family's guardian had abandoned them. In Lithuanian practice, the household fire was never allowed to fully extinguish; embers were kept alive through the night, and Gabija was fed with offerings of salt, bread, and beer at major transitions. The Kaunas Hearth Archive extended this practice communally: before a severe storm that might extinguish multiple household fires through rain or fallen embers, neighbors collected coals from their stoves and added them to the kiln's common bed. The logic was collective insurance — Gabija could not leave if she was held together in one protected space. After the storm, ash was carefully sifted: bright, sparkling flecks within the ash were understood as pieces of Gabija's still-living spirit, and these were mixed into new firewood bundles carried home. Relighting a stove from these charged bundles was understood as a direct act of welcoming Gabija back to the household rather than starting a fire from scratch. Lightning nights — when Perkūnas was active — were particularly critical because the same divine force that Gabija stood against could directly strike homes.

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Gabija** buvo nuolat palaikoma: ugnis, kuriai leista visiškai užges, buvo namų katastrofa, ženklas, kad šeimos globėja juos apleido. Lietuvių praktikoje namų ugnis niekada nebuvo pilnai leidžiama užges; žarijos buvo palaikomos gyvo per naktį, o Gabija buvo maitinama druskos, duonos ir alaus aukomis didžiuolių perėjimų metu. Kauno Židinio Archyvas išplėtė šią praktiką bendruomeniškai: prieš stiprią audrą kartu su kaimynais buvo surenkamos žarijos iš krosnelių ir dedamos į kilno bendrą lovą. Po audros pelenai buvo kruopščiai išsiurbiami: blizgantys, žirkliuojantys dalelytės pelenų viduje buvo suprantami kaip Gabijos dar gyvo dvasios gabaliukai ir buvo sumaišyti į naujus malkinės ryšulius, vežamus namo.

### Latvian (LV)
**Gabija** tika nepārtraukti uzturēta: uguns, kurai atļāva pilnīgi izdzist, bija mājsaimniecības katastrofa, zīme, ka ģimenes sargs viņus pametis. Lietuviešu praksē mājas uguns nekad netika pilnīgi dzēsta; ogles tika dzīvas turētas naktī, un Gabija tika barota ar sāls, maizes un alus upuriem pie galvenajiem pārejas punktiem. Kauņas Pavarda Arhīvs paplašināja šo praksi kopīgi: pirms smagas vētras kaimiņi savāca ogles no krāsnīm un pievienoja cepļa kopējai gultai. Pēc vētras pelni tika rūpīgi sijāti: mirgojošās, dzirkstošās dzelmes pelnos tika saprasts kā Gabijas vēl dzīvā gara gabali un tika sajaukti jaunās malkas saišķos, kas tika vesti mājās.

## Primary Source Details

- **Stryjkowski (Str):** Documents the sacred fire (*ugnis sventoji*) maintained in Lithuanian noble households and the priests who attended it — the domestic parallel to Vaidilutės-kept temple fires. [Maciej Stryjkowski, *Kronika*, 1582]
- **Vėlius (Vel):** Compiles Lithuanian folk sources on Gabija's nature, the prohibition against extinguishing the hearth fire, and communal ember-sharing practices. [Norbertas Vėlius, *Baltų religijos ir mitologijos šaltiniai*, Vol. 1, 1996]
- **Beresnevičius (Ber):** Analysis of Gabija's status as the soul of the home and the theological implications of fire-death and fire-resurrection. [Gintaras Beresnevičius, *Lietuvių religija ir mitologija*, 2004]
- **Łasicki (Łas):** First records Gabija (*Gabjauja* in grain storage variant) among Lithuanian household deities. [Jan Łasicki, *De Diis Samagitarum*, 1615]
- **Jesuit mission reports (Jes):** Document persistent hearth-fire ritual in 17th-century Lithuanian households, including communal fire-preservation during storms. [Lithuanian Jesuit missions, 1569–1650]
