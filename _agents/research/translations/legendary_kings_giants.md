# Legendary Kings, High Priests, and Giants / Architects of the Baltic Order

## Comprehensive Overview

The Baltic landscape and social order were seen as the creations of primordial giants and legendary sovereign figures. These characters bridge the gap between the divine panteon and the historical survival of the Baltic nations.

### Milžinai (The Giants)
In Pan-Baltic folklore (Lithuania and Latvia), the **Milžinai** (*milžins* in Latvian) were an ancient race that lived before humans. They are characterized by their immense size and their role as the architects of the terrain. Legendary hills, mounds, and rivers were believed to have been formed when a giant shook sand out of his boot or dropped a handful of earth. Some, like the giantess **Neringa**, were protective figures who built the Curonian Spit to protect fishermen from the sea-monsters.
- **Symbol**: Large boulders, Mound-hills (piliakalniai), Giant's boot
- **Role**: Primordial builders of the Baltic landscape.

### Videvutis & Prutenis (The Founding Kings of Prussia)
According to the 16th-century chronicle of **Simon Grunau**, the brothers **Videvutis** and **Prutenis** were the founders of the Prussian social and religious system. 
- **Videvutis**: The secular sovereign who established the code of Eleven Laws that governed Prussian society. He represents the wise, law-giving king.
- **Prutenis**: The spiritual sovereign who founded the central sanctuary of **Romuva** in Nadrovia. He established the cult of the triad (Perkūnas, Patollo, Patrimpas) and became the first **Kriwe** (High Priest).
- **End of Life**: According to legend, the brothers committed ritual self-immolation in their old age at the sanctuary of Romuva to join the gods, symbolizing the ultimate dedication to the divine order.
- **Symbol**: The Three-Headed Oak of Romuva, The 11 Laws, The High Priest's Staff.

### Kriwe / Criwe (The High Priest of Romuva)
The **Kriwe** (or *Krivis*) was the supreme religious authority of the Baltic peoples, described by the 14th-century chronicler **Peter of Dusburg** as a "Pope" of the Baltic faith. Based in the sanctuary of **Romuva** (Nadrovia), the Kriwe was the keeper of the eternal fire and the divine oracles. His authority extended across Lithuania, Prussia, and Latvia, and his staff (*krivūlė*) was a symbol of absolute spiritual power. He mediated between the living and the dead and interpreted the will of the gods through the flight of birds and the burning of sacred wood.
- **Symbol**: The Krivūlė (twisted staff), The Eternal Fire, The Sacred Oak
- **Status**: Mediator between the Gods and the People.

### Herkus Monte (The Tragic Hero)
While a historical chieftain of the Natangians during the Great Prussian Insurrection (1260–1274), **Herkus Monte** has achieved a mythic status. He is the archetype of the noble rebel who, though trained by the Teutonic Order, chose to lead his people in a final, tragic defense of their gods and their land. He represents the "last breath" of Old Prussian identity before its assimilation.
- **Symbol**: Broken sword, Rebel banner, Burning pyre.

## Primary Source Details

- **Simon Grunau (Grun):** The principal (though debated) source for the Videvutis and Prutenis legend. [Preussische Chronik, 1517–1521]
- **Peter of Dusburg (Dur):** Documented the authority of the Kriwe and the sanctuary of Romuva. [Chronicon terrae Prussiae, 1326]
- **Basanavičius (Bas):** Collected hundreds of legends about giants in the Lithuanian landscape.
- **VLE:** Provides the standard historical and mythic context for these figures.
