# Sodnu — Old Prussian Funeral Feast and Cremation Rites

## Comprehensive Overview

**Sodnu** (also written *Sodne*, *Sodin*) is the Old Prussian funeral feast accompanying cremation of the dead. It is one of the earliest documented Prussian religious practices, condemned explicitly in the **Treaty of Christburg (1249)**, which the Teutonic Knights imposed on conquered Prussian tribes. The feast involved horse sacrifice, burning of the deceased's weapons and possessions, communal drinking, lamentation, and specific food offerings — all designed to provision the dead for their journey to the underworld realm of **Patollo**.

### English (EN)
**Sodnu** is the Old Prussian cremation feast — the communal ritual surrounding the death, preparation, and burning of a deceased person, followed by a feast among the living. The **Treaty of Christburg (1249)** specifically and repeatedly condemns the *Sodne* rite, listing its components: the burning of the body, the burning of horses and weapons with the dead, and the communal feasting (*convivia*) held over the bonfire mound. This direct condemnation makes *Sodnu* the most precisely documented Old Prussian ritual in any primary source. A horse — the most prestigious animal sacrifice in Baltic and Indo-European practice — was killed and burned alongside the deceased so that the dead could ride to the underworld. A warrior's weapons, tools, clothing, and personal belongings were likewise burned: the material world was provisioned as a spirit-double in the afterlife. Family and community then gathered around the funeral pyre's remnant mound to eat, drink beer (*alus*), and mourn with ritual lamentation (*klage*). Portions of food were placed on the mound as direct offerings to the deceased. The dead's spirit (*dwassos*, Old Prussian) was understood to remain at the burial-mound for a period before journeying to **Patollo**'s realm. Additional minor periodic feasts (*Ilginen* in related Lithuanian practice) were held at the mound in subsequent years. The Teutonic condemnation was directed at the practice of cremation itself (seen as pagan versus Christian burial) and at the feast as a deliberate act of non-Christian religious expression.

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Sodnu** — senprūsų laidotuvių puota, lydinčianti kūno sudeginimą. **Christburgo sutartis (1249)** aiškiai smerkia *Sodne* apeigą: kūno deginimą, žirgų ir ginklų deginimą kartu su velionis, bei bendruomeninę puotą (*convivia*) prie peleninės dūmų kauburėlio. Žirgas — prestižiausias aukojamas gyvūnas — buvo deginamas, kad mirusysis jotų į **Patollo** požemio karalystę. Šeima ir bendruomenė valgydavo, gerdavo alų ir raudodavo prie kauburėlio. Maisto dalys buvo dedamos ant kauburėlio kaip aukos mirusiajam.

### Latvian (LV)
**Sodnu** ir Senprūšu kremācijas sēru mielasts. **Kristburgas līgums (1249)** tieši nosoda *Sodne* rituālu: miesas sadedzināšanu, zirgu un ieroču sadedzināšanu kopā ar mirušo un kopienas mielastu (*convivia*) pie kaudzes. Zirgs tika upurēts, lai mirušais varētu jāt uz **Patollo** pazemes valstību. Ģimene un kopiena pulcējās ap sēru kaudzi, ēda, dzēra alu un sēroja. Ēdiena daļas tika novietotas uz kaudzes kā tiešas upures mirušajam. Teutoniešu brāļu nosodījums bija vērsts gan pret kremāciju kā tādo, gan pret mielastu kā apzinātu pagānisku reliģisku izpausmi.

## Primary Source Details

- **Treaty of Christburg (Chr):** Primary document — directly names and condemns the *Sodne* funeral feast, listing horse sacrifice and weapon burning. [Treaty of Christburg / *Vertrag von Christburg*, February 7, 1249]
- **Peter of Dusburg (Dur):** Context for Old Prussian funerary practices in the *Chronicon terrae Prussiae*. [Peter of Dusburg, *Chronicon terrae Prussiae*, 1326]
- **Buga, Kazimieras:** Linguistic analysis of the Old Prussian *dwassos* (soul) concept and funerary vocabulary. [Kazimieras Būga, *Rinktiniai raštai*, 1958–1961]

## Key Metadata
- **Region**: Old Prussia — throughout Prussian tribal territories (Sambians, Nadruvians, Bartians, Galindians)
- **Tribe**: Old Prussians (all tribes; explicitly documented for Sambians and Nadruvians)
- **Period**: Pre-Christian; condemned in writing 1249 CE; actual practice likely centuries older; continued clandestinely until 15th century
- **Category**: Funeral Rite / Cremation / Ancestor Feast
- **Date**: At death — no fixed calendar date; periodic revisits to burial mound in following years
- **Symbol**: Funeral pyre, Horse sacrifice, Weapons and tools (burned), Beer (*alus*), Food mound-offerings, Burial mound (*kapas/kauburys*)
- **Deities invoked**: Patollo (death/underworld — destination of the dead), Patrimpas (youth — present at life cycle transitions)
- **Ritual actions**: Cremation of body, horse sacrifice and co-burning, weapon and possession burning, communal feast, lamentation, food-offerings on mound
- **References**:
    - "Treaty of Christburg / Vertrag von Christburg (1249)"
    - "Peter of Dusburg: Chronicon terrae Prussiae (1326)"
    - "Būga, Kazimieras: Rinktiniai raštai (1958–1961)"
