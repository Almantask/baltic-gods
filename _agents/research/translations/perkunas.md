# Perkūnas / Pērkons / Perkūns

## Comprehensive Overview

Perkūnas (Lithuanian), Pērkons (Latvian), and Perkūns (Old Prussian) is the preeminent Baltic deity of thunder, lightning, storms, and the sky. As the most active and socially significant god, he serves as the protector of cosmic order, justice, and fertility.

### English (EN)
**Perkūnas** is the wielder of the celestial axe and hammer, riding a chariot through the heavens to hunt his chthonic rival, **Velnias**. He is the "purifier" of the earth; his first spring thunder is believed to "unlock" the soil and bring life back to vegetation. He resides on high hills and is inseparable from the sacred **Oak** tree (*ąžuolas*). In Latvian tradition, **Pērkons** is often depicted with a large family, including sons who manifest different thunderous sounds and daughters who represent varying types of rain. He is a god of war but also a stern judge who punishes the wicked with his "thunder-bullets" (belemnite fossils). Perkūnas represents the dynamic, masculine power of the storm that fertility depends on.

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Perkūnas** yra galingiausias baltų atmosferos ir teisingumo dievas, dangaus kalvis, kurio kirvis ar kūjis skelia žaibus. Jis nuolatos kovoja su savo priešininku **Velniu**, kuris slepiasi po akmenimis ar medžiais. Perkūnas yra sakralizuoto **ąžuolo** globėjas; tikima, kad pirmasis pavasario griaustinis „atrakina“ žemę ir leidžia jai sužaliuoti. Senovės lietuviu tikėjime jis buvo vaizduojamas kaip rūsčias vyras, riedantis dangumi ugniniu ar žirgų kinkiniu vežimu. Jo vardas tapo bendriniu žodžiu perkūnijai pavadinti, o jo kultas išliko gyvybingas net po oficialaus krikšto.

### Latvian (LV)
**Pērkons** latviešu mitoloģijā ir debesu kalējs un taisnīguma sargs, kurš dzenā **Velnu** un attīra pasauli no ļaunuma. Viņš ir cieši saistīts ar auglību; dainās Pērkons bieži parādās kopā ar savu kuplo ģimeni — **Pērkona dēliem** un **meitām**, kas simbolizē dažādas dabas parādības. Pērkona simbols ir **Pērkona krusts** (ugunskrusts), kas sargā pret ļaunumu. Viņš jāj uz pelēka zirga vai brauc akmens ratiem, dārdinot debesis un metot zibens bultas. Mēdz teikt: „Pērkons grauj, Dievs dod lietu”, uzsverot viņa lomu dabas spēku līdzsvarošanā.

## Primary Source Details

- **Łasicki (Łas):** Describes Perkūnas as the primary god of thunder (*Percunus*) and records goat sacrifices made in his honour. [Jan Łasicki, written c. 1582, published 1615] *(Note: the folk name "Perkūno oželis" for the Common Snipe derives from later Lithuanian ethnographic records — e.g., Laurinkienė (1996), Jonas Balys — not directly from Łasicki's text.)*
- **Stryjkowski (Str):** Mentions Perkūnas as the supreme god of the Lithuanians worshipped with eternal fire in groves. [Maciej Stryjkowski, 1582]
- **Praetorius (Prae):** Details the iconographic representation of Perkūnas as a middle-aged, bearded man with a stern expression. [Matthäus Prätorius, 17th c.]
- **Vėlius (Vel):** Analyzes the dualistic struggle between Perkūnas (celestial) and Velnias (chthonic), noting Perkūnas as the guardian of cosmic stability. For analytical work on the dualism see *Senovės baltų pasaulėžiūra* (1983); *Baltų religijos ir mitologijos šaltiniai* (1996) is a primary source anthology. [Norbertas Vėlius]
- **Barons (Bar):** Cataloged thousands of Latvian *dainas* where Pērkons acts as a family man, blacksmith, and divine judge. [Krišjānis Barons]

## Key Metadata
- **Region**: Lithuania, Latvia, Prussia
- **Tribe**: All Baltic tribes
- **Period**: Proto-Baltic origin to present folklore
- **Category**: Major / Sovereign Deity
- **Symbol**: Oak, Axe, Hammer, Thunderbolt, Thunder Cross (Pērkona krusts), Fire
- **Animals**: Horse (primary chariot-puller; fiery steed in folk songs), Bull, Goat (sacrificial animal; also the origin of the folk name "Perkūno oželis" for the Common Snipe — *not* a chariot-puller; the goat-drawn chariot is a Norse/Thor motif not natively attested in Baltic primary sources)
- **Day**: Thursday (Perkūno diena / Pērkondiena)
- **Function**: Thunder, Justice, Fertility, War, and Purification
- **Etymology**: *perk- (oak/strike)
