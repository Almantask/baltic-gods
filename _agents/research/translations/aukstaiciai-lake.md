# Lake Moon Ladder / Ežero Mėnulio Kopėčios / Ezera Mēness Kāpnes

## Comprehensive Overview

**Lake Moon Ladder** is a still lake in the Aukštaitija lake district with coordinates placing it in the heart of the Lithuanian lake country. The site derives its name from the optical phenomenon of a long, narrow moon-reflection that appears on calm water on clear nights when the moon is young (crescent phase) — a reflection that runs perpendicular to the shore like a shining ladder extending from waterline to horizon. The lake is dedicated to **Mėnulis**, the Baltic moon god, who presides over night travel, the luck of fishermen, and the measurement of sacred time. Young-moon reflections were interpreted across Baltic tradition as the deity's personal path becoming temporarily visible — a moment of direct contact between the world above and the world of living people.

### English (EN)
**Mėnulis** was far more active and present than a modern conception of the moon would suggest: he rode a horse or drove a chariot across the night sky, he visited Saulė (the sun) as a husband who was periodically punished by her or by Perkūnas for his inconstancies, and he regulated the lives of fishermen by controlling the tides, the visibility of underwater life, and the behaviour of schools of fish. At Lake Moon Ladder, this practical-mythological connection was made ritual: fishermen waited for the appearance of the long reflection before casting nets. Pulling before the reflection arrived was believed to tangle the silver path — an offense against the deity that would bring a night of misfortune. Children floated birch-bark boats with silver pins tucked inside; if the boats spun clockwise (following the moon's apparent motion eastward), the trip home was blessed; counterclockwise indicated unresolved business at the lake. The "ladder" image itself was associated with ancestors climbing to or from the moon's region — the Latvian *dainas* have a well-developed motif of souls travelling by moonlight — and families would call the names of recently dead relatives toward the reflection on the anniversary of their death, believing the moonlit path facilitated the soul's continued journey.

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Mėnulis** buvo daug aktyvesnis ir labiau esantis, nei šiuolaikinė mėnulio samprata reikštų: jis jojo arkliu ar vairavo karieta per nakties dangų, lankė Saulę kaip vyras, kurį ji ar Perkūnas periodiškai baudė dėl jo nepastovumų, ir reguliavo žvejų gyvenimą valdydamas potvynius, povandeninio gyvenimo matomumą ir žuvų mokyklų elgesį. Ežero Mėnulio Kopėčiose ši praktinė-mitologinė jungtis buvo paversta ritualu: žvejai laukė ilgojo atspindžio pasirodymo prieš mesdami tinklus. Traukimas prieš atspindžio atėjimą, kaip manyta, susipynė sidabrinį taką — dievaičio įžeidimas, kuris atneštų nelaimingą naktį. Vaikai plukdė beržo žievės valtis su įkictomis sidabrinėmis vinimis; jei valtys sukosi pagal laikrodžio rodyklę, kelionė namo buvo palaiminta.

### Latvian (LV)
**Mēness** bija daudz aktīvāks un klātesošāks, nekā mūsdienu mēness koncepcija norādītu: viņš jāja ar zirgu vai brauca ar karieti pāri nakts debesīm, apmeklēja Sauli kā vīrs, kuru viņa vai Pērkons periodiski sodīja par viņa nepastāvību, un regulēja zvejnieku dzīves, kontrolējot paisumus, zemūdens dzīves redzamību un zivju baru uzvedību. Ezera Mēness Kāpnēs šī praktiskā-mitoloģiskā saikne tika ritualizēta: zvejnieki gaidīja garā atspulga parādīšanos pirms tīklu mešanas. Velšana pirms atspulga ierašanās, tika uzskatīts, samezglotu sudraba ceļu — aizvainojums pret dievību, kas nestu nelaimes nakti. Bērni laida bērza mizas laivas ar sudraba spraudīšu iekšā; ja laivas griezās pulksteņrādītāja virzienā, ceļš mājās bija svētīts.

## Primary Source Details

- **Łasicki (Łas):** Notes *Mėnulis* as a Lithuanian deity counted among the sky family and consulted at night journeys and fishing. [Jan Łasicki, *De Diis Samagitarum*, 1615]
- **Barons (Bar):** Latvian *dainas* with extensive moon-traveler motifs, including the "ladder of moonlight" image and souls ascending by night. [Krišjānis Barons, *Latvju dainas*, 6 vols, 1894–1915]
- **Biezais (Biez):** Studies the moon-god's role in the Latvian sky family; his relationship with sun, pleiades, and the fate of fishermen. [Haralds Biezais, *Die himmlische Götterfamilie*, 1972]
- **VLE:** Encyclopaedia entry on *Mėnulis* and his role in Lithuanian night-travel and fisher's belief (*vle.lt*)
- **Šmits (Šmits):** Folk accounts from Latvian lake regions linking moonlight reflections to deceased souls and fishing luck. [Pēteris Šmits, *Latviešu pasakas un teikas*, Vol. 9, 1930]
