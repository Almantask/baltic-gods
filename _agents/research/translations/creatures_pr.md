# Old Prussian Creatures & Spirits / The Little Gods of the Earth

## Comprehensive Overview

The Old Prussian mythological system, particularly as documented in the 16th-century *Sudovian Book*, features a distinct hierarchy of elemental and household spirits. These "little gods" serve the primary earth deity **Puschkaitis** and are deeply connected to the fertility of the soil and the success of the farmstead.

### Kaukai (Prussian Gnomes / Kaukoi)
In Prussian tradition, the **Kaukai** are small, industrious earth-spirits. They differ from the Lithuanian house-dragon Aitvaras by being social, community-dwelling beings. They were believed to live under elder trees (**sambucus**) or in the shadows of the homestead. The Prussians constructed small stone altars beneath elder trees, where they would leave offerings of milk, beer, and porridge. If treated well, the Kaukai would bring grain to the barns and ensure the prosperity of the animals. If insulted, they would leave, taking the household's "luck" with them.
- **Symbol**: Elder tree (*Sambucus nigra*), Stone altar, Porridge bowl
- **Function**: Bringing harvest-wealth, Industry, Household protection

### Markopoles (Earth spirits / Border spirits)
Mentioned alongside the Kaukai in the *Sudovian Book*, the **Markopoles** (or *Markopole*) are subordinates of Puschkaitis. Their exact nature is debated: they are often seen as the field-based counterparts to the household-based Kaukai. Etymologically, the name may be linked to ground-borders (*marka* - field/limit). They ensure that the crops grow tall and that the soil remains fertile.
- **Symbol**: Plowed furrow, Field-stone
- **Role**: Minions of Puschkaitis, Field fertility

### Barstucke (Bearded Dwarfs / Barstucae)
The **Barstucke** are specifically "bearded" dwarf-spirits who inhabit the forest and the edges of the fields. They are described as small men with long beards who work in secrecy to promote the growth of fruits and wild plants. They are the Prussian version of the Lithuanian **Barzdukai** and are generally seen as benevolent nature-spirits who coexist peacefully with humans as long as their sacred groves are respected.
- **Symbol**: Long beard, Acorn, Moss-covered roots

### Smok (Amber-Serpent / Coastal Dragon)
> ⚠️ **Editorial note:** The term *Smok* is a **Slavic word** (Polish: *smok* = dragon) and does not appear in any identified Old Prussian-language source, the Sudovian Book, or Praetorius. The amber-guardian serpent motif itself has Baltic parallels (cf. *Žaltys*, *Eglė*), but no Old Prussian creature is attested under this specific name. This entry describes a motif found in Prussian coastal folk tradition via Slavic-influenced transmission; it should not be presented as a native Prussian mythological term.

The **Smok** is a legendary serpent or dragon-like being from the Prussian coast. Legend associated these creatures with the protection of the "sea gold" (**amber**). While the term *Smok* is shared with Slavic folklore, in the Prussian coastal context, it personifies the dangerous and treasure-laden nature of the Baltic Sea. Amber was often seen as "tears of a goddess" or "dragon's scales," and the Smok was its fierce guardian.
- **Symbol**: Amber piece, Sea-waves, Serpent-coil

## Primary Source Details

- **Sudovian Book (Sud):** The primary 16th-century source for the Prussian "little gods." [Sudauer Büchlein, 1561]
- **Praetorius (Prae):** Documented the continuing folk belief in Kaukai and elder-tree offerings in the 17th century. [Deliciae Prussicae]
- **Hennenberger (Hen):** Provided detailed descriptions of the Barstucke and their forest habitats.
- **Mannhardt (Mann):** Critically analyzed the hierarchy of Prussian nature-spirits. [Letto-Preussische Götterlehre]
