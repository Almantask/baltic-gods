# Agricultural & Animal Mātes / Mother Spirits of the Land, Herds, and Wildlife

## Comprehensive Overview

In the worldview of the ancient Latvian farmer, every vital aspect of the agricultural cycle and the animal kingdom was presided over by a specific "Mother" (Māte) spirit. These entities were the guardians of the livestock and the fertility of the plowed fields.

### Lopu māte (Mother of Livestock)
**Lopu māte** is the primary guardian of the farm's animals, particularly cattle and sheep. She is often considered a specialized aspect of **Māra** (the Earth Mother). In the dainas, she is depicted as a benevolent presence who walks through the barns, counting the animals and ensuring they are fed and healthy. Farmers made offerings to her during the seasonal transitions (such as **Jurģi** or **Miķeļi**) to ensure the survival of the herds through the winter.
- **Symbol**: Barn-key, Woven hay, Capercaillie (in some livestock motifs)
- **Function**: Protecting the health and fertility of the herd

### Piena māte (Mother of Milk)
A specialized spirit governing the production and quality of milk. **Piena māte** was crucial to the household's survival. Rituals involved pouring the "first milk" or a portion of the cream on the threshold or into the ground as a gift to her. She was believed to protect the cows from "milk-theft" by malevolent sorcerers or spirits.
- **Symbol**: Milk-pail, Creamed bowl, White cloth

### Vilku māte (Mother of Wolves / Vilkamāte)
The mistress of the wild canine world. **Vilku māte** represents the untamed power of the predator. She is the mother of the "hounds of Dievs" (the wolves). While she was feared for her power over the farm's herds, she was also respected as the spirit that maintained the balance of the forest. Folklore motifs include the "locking of the wolf's jaws" through ritual prayer to prevent them from attacking the livestock.
- **Symbol**: Wolf-skin, Iron teeth, Silent forest path

### Tīruma māte (Mother of the Field)
**Tīruma māte** (or **Lauka māte**) is the spirit of the cultivated land. Unlike the general earth, she is the goddess of the *plowed* field and the hard human labor of sowing and reaping. She is invoked at the start of the agricultural year to bless the seeds and at the harvest to receive the "last sheaf."
- **Symbol**: Plow, Seed-bag, Last sheaf of grain

### Water-Landscape Mātes (Avotu, Ezeru, Purva)
- **Avotu māte** (Mother of Springs): The protector of the flowing, pure water of the earth's springs.
- **Ezeru māte** (Mother of Lakes): The mistress of the still, deep waters.
- **Purva māte** (Mother of Bogs): A dangerous figure associated with the treacherous marshes. The bog was seen as a liminal space where one could easily be lost (by the *vadātājs* or spirits). Purva māte is the one who "owns" the swamp and must be treated with caution.
- **Putnu māte** (Mother of Birds): The protectress of the winged creatures, governing their migration and nesting.
