# Velnias / Velns / Vels — Lord of the Underworld

## Comprehensive Overview

Velnias (Lithuanian), Velns (Latvian), and Vels (Old Latvian/Latvian dialect) is the great chthonic deity of the Baltic pantheon — lord of the dead, cattle god, master of magic and wisdom, guardian of oaths, and trickster figure in folk tradition. He is the cosmic antagonist of **Perkūnas** (Thunder God), and their eternal pursuit is the central binary axis of Baltic mythology. In folk fairy tales he appears as a shapeshifting trickster who can be outwitted by a clever mortal.

### English (EN)
**Velnias** inhabits the boundary lands: marshes, crossroads, river bends, the roots of old trees — anywhere the surface of the living world grows thin. He is the keeper of the souls of the dead (the *vėlės*) and the protector of cattle, reflecting a deep cultural link between the underworld, ancestors, and the fertility of livestock. In the great mythological cycle he steals fire, cattle, the sun, or a soul from the celestial realm, provoking **Perkūnas** to chase him across the sky; every thunderstorm re-enacts that pursuit. Velnias flees by shapeshifting into cloud, man, bull, stone, tree root, and finally hides inside a human being. Perkūnas strikes each hiding place with lightning, and the rain that follows is the by-product of restored cosmic order.

In folk narratives — the layer where Velnias becomes less cosmic terror and more cunning trickster — he appears at crossroads in the guise of a well-dressed gentleman, a wandering merchant, or a fiddler. He issues wagers with mortals, he invites the bold to dance or play music, and he can be defeated through clever use of *moving water* patterns (which bind him, as a creature of stagnant bogs and still pools) or through the morning rooster's crow, which drives him back below ground. The Fiddler tale — where a musician named Jonas plays a dance tune that traps Velnias in compulsive motion until dawn — is a classic example: the victory is not of force but of formal trickery, using the old spirit's own nature against him.

As god of oaths and wisdom, Velnias also has a benevolent aspect: he is associated with poetry, magic, music, and the preservation of ancestral memory. In Latvian tradition, **Velns** is specifically invoked in cattle-blessing rites, reflecting his ancient role as protector of the herd.

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Velnias** — viena iš dviejų pagrindinių baltų mitologijos jėgų, priešprieša dangaus dievui Perkūnui. Jis yra mirusiųjų vėlių valdovas, gyvulius saugantis dievas ir kerų bei išminties šaltinis. Mitologiniame cikle Velnias vagia iš dangaus sferų — gyvulius, ugnį, saulę ar sielą — ir Perkūnas jį persekioja per dangų bei žemę, kraipo jį iš vienos formos į kitą: debesis, žmogus, jautis, akmuo, medžio šaknis. Kiekvienas griaustinis yra tas pats kosminės dramos persikartojimas. Daugybė folkloro sakmių vaizduoja Velnią kaip sukčių ir gudruolį, galinčią būti pralaimintį protingo žmogaus. Smukininkas Jonas iš Samogitijos, grojantis šokio melodiją, verčia Velnią šokti iki aušros — laimingas rezultatas, nes gyvieji turi pranašumą prieš žemesniąsias dvasias per rytą ir lakantį vandenį, kuriam Velnias paklūsta. Velnias taip pat turi gerąją pusę: jis globoja priesaikas, saugo protėvių atminimą ir yra susijęs su poezija ir magija.

### Latvian (LV)
**Velns** latviešu mitoloģijā ir Viļņas dvēseļu un lopu aizgādnis — tumšās pasaules saimnieks, kurš mitinās purvos, upju krastos un ceļu krustojumos. Viņš ir **Pērkona** kosmiskais pretinieks; viņu cīņa ir visu pērkona vētru pamatā. Latviešu dainās Velns bieži parādās kā liellopu aizsargs — tēls, kurš skaidro, kāpēc ganāmpulkiem vajadzēja rituāli saņemt viņa aizsardzību. Latviešu pasakās Velns ir viltīgs; jūs varat viņu piekrāpt ar *tekošu ūdeni*, ar gaiļa dziedāšanu vai ar uzjautrinošu dejas melodiju, kas viņu iesloga aklos dejošanas spazmos. Tāpat kā lietuviešu Velnias, arī Velns vienlaicīgi ir drauds un aizsargs — atkarīgs no tā, kas nāk pie viņa un kā.

## Primary Source Details

- **Basanavičius (Bas):** Collected dozens of fairy tales and legends featuring Velnias as a crossroads trickster in Dzūkija and Aukštaitija. *Lietuviškos pasakos ir svietimo dalykai* (1898–1902) contains the primary versions of the fiddler-type encounter. [Jonas Basanavičius, 1898–1902]
- **Vėlius (Vel):** The foremost scholarly analysis of the Perkūnas–Velnias duality as the cosmological axis of Baltic religion. Vėlius demonstrates Velnias's roles as cattle god, oath-keeper, and the guardian of the *vėlės* (souls of the dead) across Lithuanian, Latvian, and Old Prussian material. [Norbertas Vėlius: *Baltų religijos ir mitologijos šaltiniai*, Vol. 1–4, 1996–2005]
- **Ivanov & Toporov (IT):** Foundational structuralist analysis of the Perkūnas-Velnias myth as the universal Indo-European "storm myth," reconstructed from comparative Baltic evidence. [Ivanov, V.V. and Toporov, V.N.: *Issledovaniya v oblasti slavyanskikh drevnostey*, 1974]
- **Barons (Bar):** Krišjānis Barons's *Latvju Dainas* contain numerous references to Velns as a cattle spirit and oath-witness, providing the Latvian counterpart to Lithuanian Velnias traditions. [Krišjānis Barons: *Latvju Dainas*, 1894–1915]
- **Łasicki (Łas):** Notes "Ganyklis" and "Giltine" alongside "Velnias" in his list of Lithuanian spirits; Velnias appears as a figure associated with cattle and the dead. [Jan Łasicki: *De diis Samagitarum*, 1582; publ. 1615]
- **VLE:** Confirms Velnias as the most thoroughly documented deity in the Lithuanian mythological archive after Perkūnas. [Visuotinė lietuvių enciklopedija]

## Key Metadata
- **Region**: Pan-Baltic (Lithuania, Latvia, Old Prussian borderlands)
- **Tribe**: All Baltic tribes
- **Period**: Proto-Baltic origin; attested from 16th century onwards in written sources
- **Category**: Major Deity — Chthonic / Underworld
- **Symbol**: Cattle, gold, serpent, willow, stagnant water, crossroads, music, silver pouch
- **Sacred Animals**: Cattle, snake, toad, fox
- **Locations**: Crossroads, marshes, riverbends, tree roots; Kernavė burial mounds in Lithuanian tradition
- **Related Beings**: Perkūnas (cosmic antagonist); *vėlės* (souls of the dead, under his guardianship)
- **Function**: Lord of the dead; cattle protector; keeper of oaths and buried treasure; trickster and master of magic
- **Etymology**: *vel-* (Proto-Baltic: relating to the dead/underworld; cf. Lithuanian *vėlė* — soul of the dead, *viltis* — hope)
