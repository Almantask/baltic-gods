# Jelgava Sky-Stone / Jelgavos Dangaus Akmuo / Jelgavas Debess Akmens

## Comprehensive Overview

The **Jelgava Sky-Stone** is a glacial erratic boulder on the Zemgale plain near Jelgava (historic Mitau), Latvia. Its surface is marked by spiral grooves that local tradition attributes to "cloud-fire" — lightning strikes over successive nights. Glacial erratics (*akmeņi*) were widely venerated across the Baltic region as objects linked to Pērkons: their alien bulk, having been carried by forces no human could command, made them natural vessels for divine contact. The Jelgava stone is noteworthy for the spiral pattern of its burns, which archaeo-folk accounts indicate were used as a calendar for "dry thunder" — the kind that rolls without rain. Recent ethnographic surveys (Urtāns, 1993) list it among documented Latvian cult stones.

### English (EN)
The **Sky-Stone** is a palimpsest of lightning contact: each major burn spiral is said to correspond to a different cloud-fire event across the long memory of its keepers. Before reading the sky for divination, supplicants would press both palms flat against the stone's grooves. If the stone felt warm, thunder was expected to travel with rain; if cold, it would move silently and strike only isolated trees. The act of "reading warmth" is consistent with animist practices documented by Einhorn in 17th-century Livonia. Children traced the spirals with chalk — not to deface them but to transfer the pattern to paper for family weather records. When rain washed the chalk away, families believed the stone had absorbed the worries carried in those marks and deposited them in the soil. The site served as a practical oracle for Zemgale farmers facing the chronically unpredictable storm fronts that sweep in from the Baltic Sea.

### Lithuanian (LT)
**Dangaus Akmuo** yra palimpsestinis žaibo kontakto paminklas: kiekviena degimo spiralė tariamai atitinka skirtingą debesų ugnies įvykį ilgoje jo saugotojų atmintyje. Prieš skaityti dangų pranašystei, maldininkai glausdavo abi delnus prie akmens griovelio. Jei akmuo atrodė šiltas, tikėtasi griaustinio su lietumi; jei šaltas — jis judės tyliai ir trenksis tik į vienus medžius. Vaikai piešė spirales kreida, ne tam, kad sugadintų, bet kad perkelė raštą ant popieriaus šeimų orų įrašams. Kai lietus nuplaudavo kreidą, šeimos tikėjo, kad akmuo įsiurbė tuose ženkluose nešamas rūpesčius ir padėjo juos dirvoje.

### Latvian (LV)
**Debess Akmens** ir zibens kontakta palimpsests: katrs degšanas spirāles tiek sacīts atbilstošs citam mākoņuguns notikumam garās tā glabātāju atmiņās. Pirms lasīt debesu pareģojumam, lūdzēji spieda abas plaukstas pret akmens rievām. Ja akmens jutās silts, gaidāms pērkons ar lietu; ja auksts — tas dosies klūsumā un skars tikai atsevišķus kokus. Bērni zīmēja spirāles ar krītu — ne lai to bojātu, bet lai pārnēsātu rakstu uz papīra ģimenes laika aprakstiem. Kad lietus nomazgāja krītu, ģimenes ticēja, ka akmens ir absorbējis šajos zīmēs nests rūpes un noguldījis tās augsnē.

## Primary Source Details

- **Einhorn (Ein):** Documents active veneration of stones in Livonia and Zemgale, including the pressing of hands against warmed surfaces. [Paul Einhorn, *Historia Lettica*, 1649]
- **Fabricius (Fab):** Notes the persistence of stone-worship among the Baltic Latvians despite Christianization efforts. [Dionysius Fabricius, *Livonicae historiae*, 1610]
- **Urtāns (Urt):** Fieldwork catalogue of Latvian sacred stones; lists Jelgava region erratics with documented folk ritual. [Juris Urtāns, *Latvijas senā svētvietas*, 1993]
- **Šmits (Šmits):** Collects folk tales and beliefs associating glacial boulders with Pērkons and lightning events. [Pēteris Šmits, *Latviešu mitoloģija*, 1918]
- **Biezais (Biez):** Analyzes the stone-deity relationship within the Latvian sky-god complex. [Haralds Biezais, *Die himmlische Götterfamilie*, 1972]
